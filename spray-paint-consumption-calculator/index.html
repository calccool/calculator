Paint consumption calculator for spray gun (viscosity by viscometer)

Correct calculation of paints and varnishes is not only budget savings, but also a guarantee of compliance with painting technology. An excessive amount of material leads to the formation of smudges and shagreen, and an insufficient amount leads to a “dry” torch and loss of shine.

Key consumption factors when working with a spray gun

When using a spray gun, the actual consumption is always higher than the theoretical consumption indicated on the can. This is due to the phenomenon Overspray (paint mist) and the type of spray technology used.

Mathematical calculation model

To calculate the final paint volume ( $V_{total}$ ), we use a formula that takes into account the total area, substrate absorption and spray losses:

$$V_{total} = \frac{S \cdot n \cdot C_{base} \cdot K_{surf}}{K_{transfer}}$$

Where:

  • $S$ - surface area ( $м^2$ );
  • $n$ - number of applied layers;
  • $C_{base}$ —basic paint consumption (l/ $м^2$ );
  • $K_{surf}$ - surface porosity coefficient (from 1.0 to 1.3);
  • $K_{transfer}$ - spray gun efficiency coefficient.

The role of viscosity and the DIN-4 viscometer

Viscosity is the internal resistance of a fluid to flow. For a spray gun, it is critical: too thick paint will not break into small drops, creating an “orange peel”, and too liquid paint will cause smudges.

Standard DIN-4 determines the flow time of the composition through a nozzle with a diameter of 4 mm. If the current viscosity is higher than the specification, it is necessary to add a thinner. Our calculator uses a rule of thumb:

Each decrease in viscosity by 1 second on a DIN-4 viscometer requires the addition of approximately 2% of the corresponding diluent from the total volume of the base.

Diluent volume formula

If the “Viscosity Laboratory” mode is enabled, the additive volume ( $V_{thinner}$ ) is calculated using the formula:

$$V_{thinner} = V_{total} \cdot ((T_{cur} — T_{target}) \cdot 0.02)$$

Where $T_{cur}$ is the current viscosity, and $T_{target}$ is the target value according to the technical data sheet (TDS) of the product.

Professional painting advice

  • Torch Setting: Always do a test color on a test card or cardboard. The shape of the torch should be a clear elongated oval without “breaks” in the middle.
  • Temperature: All viscosity calculations are valid at temperature 20°C. As the temperature increases, the viscosity decreases, and as the temperature decreases, it increases.
  • Air pressure: For HVLP systems, the gun inlet usually requires 2.0–2.5 bar, in order to obtain the required output 0.7 bar.
