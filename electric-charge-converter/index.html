Electric charge unit converter: Coulombs, Ampere-hours, Electrons

Electric charge is a fundamental physical property of matter, but it is measured differently in different fields of science and technology. Theoretical physicists operate with the charge of an electron, electrical engineers with ampere-hours, and the international SI system uses Coulomb.

Our universal converter allows you to instantly transfer values ​​between all existing measurement systems: from the microcosm (quantum physics) to industrial scales (electric vehicle batteries).

Basic charge units

1. Pendant (C) - SI Standard

The coulomb is the official unit of measurement in the International System of Units (SI).

Definition: 1 A coulomb is a charge passing through the cross section of a conductor at a current of 1 Ampere in 1 second.

The formula for the relationship between charge ( $Q$ ), current ( $I$ ) and time ( $t$ ):

$$Q = I \times t$$

2. Ampere hour (Ah) - Industry and batteries

This unit is used to estimate the capacity of batteries, smartphone batteries and car batteries. It shows how long the power supply can supply a certain current.

  • 1 Ampere-hour (Ah) = 3,600 Coulomb.
  • 1 Milliamp-hour (mAh) = 3.6 Coulombs.

3. Elementary charge ( $e$ ) – Physics of the microworld

This is the minimum portion of charge that exists in nature (the absolute charge of one proton or electron). Used in atomic physics and chemistry.

$$e \approx 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$$

How to convert Ampere hours to Coulombs and Watts?

A common task when working with electronics is converting battery capacity into charge or energy.

Convert Ah to Coulombs:

Since 1 hour = 3600 seconds, the formula is simple:

$$Q_{(C)} = E_{(Ah)} \times 3600$$

Convert Ah to Watt Hours (Energy):

The charge itself does not indicate the energy reserve if we do not know the voltage ( $V$ ).

$$E_{(Wh)} = Q_{(Ah)} \times V_{(V)}$$

Example: A phone battery of 4000 mAh (4 Ah) at a voltage of 3.7 V reserves

$$4 \times 3.7 = 14.8 \text{ Wh}$$

Rare and obsolete units

Our converter also supports historical and specialized units:

  • Abculon (abC): Unit of the SGSM system. One abcoulomb is equal to 10 coulombs. This is a very large value, rarely used in practice.
  • Franklin (Fr) or Statcoulon: Unit of the SGSE system. It is determined through the force of interaction between two charges (Coulomb’s law) of 1 dyne at a distance of 1 cm.
  • Faraday (F): Used in electrochemistry. Equal to the charge of one mole of electrons (Avogadro's number $\times$ elementary charge). Not to be confused with Farad (unit of capacitance)!

$$1 \text{ F} = N_A \times e \approx 96485 \text{ C}$$
