Depth of field calculator: online calculation of depth of field

DOF (Depth of Sharp Space) is the area of ​​space within which objects in a photograph are perceived as clear and sharp. The ability to control this depth is one of the main skills in photography, allowing you to separate an object from the background (bokeh) or, conversely, make the entire landscape detailed.

Our online depth of field calculator allows you to instantly calculate the near and far limits of sharpness, as well as hyperfocal distance for your camera and lens.

Main factors affecting depth of field

The final depth of field value is influenced by four key parameters:

  1. Aperture ( $f$ ): The wider the aperture is open (smaller f-number), the shallower the depth of field.
  2. Focal Length ( $F$ ): Long-focus lenses (telephotos) “blur” the background more than wide-angle ones.
  3. Distance to object ( $s$ ): The closer you get to your subject, the thinner the layer of sharpness becomes.
  4. Matrix size (Scattering circle): The physical size of the sensor determines how much blur a point can tolerate before it becomes “out of focus.”

Mathematical calculation model

The calculator is based on classical optical formulas. First it is calculated hyperfocal distance ( $H$ ):

$$H = \frac{F^2}{N \cdot c} + F$$

Where:

  • $F$ - focal length (mm);
  • $N$ - aperture number;
  • $c$ - circle of dispersion (mm).

Then the sharpness limits are calculated:

  • Near Boundary ( $D_n$ ): $$D_n = \frac{s \cdot (H — F)}{H + s — 2F}$$
  • Far Border ( $D_f$ ): $$D_f = \frac{s \cdot (H — F)}{H — s}$$

What is hyperfocal distance?

This is the magical focal point. If you focus your lens at the hyperfocal distance, the area of ​​focus will extend from half this distance to infinity. This is critical for landscape photography when you want to get sharp rocks in the foreground and clear mountains on the horizon.

Tips for use

  • For portraits: Use $f/1.4 — f/2.8$ values ​​to highlight the person as much as possible.
  • For macro photography: Remember that at short distances the depth of field can be a fraction of a millimeter, so close the aperture to $f/11$ or $f/16$ .
  • Quick input: Use the buttons for quick substitution of popular values ​​(35mm, 50mm, f/2.8) to speed up calculations.
