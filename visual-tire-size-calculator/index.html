Online visual tire calculator: calculation of changes in ground clearance and speed

When replacing factory tires with aftermarket or alternative sizes, it is critical to understand how the vehicle's physical parameters will change. Our visual tire calculator is a high-precision tool that allows you to instantly compare two wheel sizes and see the difference in geometry, dynamics and instrument readings.

The use of non-standard dimensions affects not only aesthetics, but also safety: the center of gravity, the load on the transmission and the accuracy of the ABS/ESP systems change.

Main calculated parameters

The calculator conducts an in-depth analysis based on the following criteria:

  • O.D: Overall height of the wheel assembly.
  • Tread width: Contact patch with the road.
  • Profile height: Distance from the disc to the edge of the tread.
  • Change in clearance: How many millimeters will the car body rise or fall?
  • Speedometer error: Real vehicle speed compared to instrument readings.

Mathematical calculation model

To ensure gold standard accuracy, our algorithm uses classic automotive engineering formulas. All calculations are converted from inches (disc diameter) to millimeters to obtain the final result.

Wheel outer diameter calculation ( $D$ )

The formula takes into account the tire width ( $W$ ), profile percentage ( $P$ ) and wheel diameter ( $d$ ):

$$D = \frac{W \cdot P \cdot 2}{100} + (d \cdot 25.4)$$

Calculation of ride height change ( $\Delta H$ )

The clearance changes by exactly half the difference in the diameters of the old ( $D_1$ ) and new ( $D_2$ ) wheels:

$$\Delta H = \frac{D_2 — D_1}{2}$$

Real speed calculation ( $V_{real}$ )

The speedometer is set to the circumference of the factory wheel. When changing the size, the distance traveled per revolution changes:

$$V_{real} = V_{ind} \cdot \frac{D_2}{D_1}$$

Where $V_{ind}$ is the speed indicator on the speedometer.

Why is it important to consider speedometer error?

Many owners, for example, of cars such as BMW (where the accuracy of electronics settings is critical), they are faced with the fact that after installing wheels of larger diameter, fines for speeding begin to arrive.

This happens because a wheel with a larger diameter travels a longer distance in one revolution. If your speedometer shows 100 km/h and the diameter has increased by 5%, your actual speed will be 105 km/h. Our interactive visualization unit (speedometer) clearly demonstrates this gap in real time.

Recommendations for choosing tires

  1. Tolerance: Try to ensure that the outer diameter of the new wheel does not differ from the factory one by more than +/- 3%.
  2. Gaps in arches: If the width increases significantly ( $W$ ), make sure that the wheel will not touch the suspension components or wheel arch liners when the steering wheel is turned completely.
  3. Load Index: Don't forget to check the load rating of your new tire, especially if you're downgrading to a low profile.
  4. Pressure: Low profile tires require higher pressure to protect the rim from flats.
