Heating boiler power calculator: Accurate online calculation by area and volume

The choice of heating equipment is a balance between comfort and savings. An error in calculations leads to two critical problems:

  1. Lack of power: The boiler will work at its maximum capacity without turning off, which will lead to rapid wear of the burner or heating elements, and the house will still be cold during peak frosts.
  2. Excess power (oversizing): Leads to clocking (frequent switching on/off), increased fuel consumption, the formation of condensation in the chimney and a decrease in system efficiency.

Our calculator uses the volumetric calculation method ( $Q = V \times \Delta T \times K$ ), which is much more accurate than the classic formula “1 kW per 10 m²”, since it takes into account the height of the ceilings and the actual degree of insulation of the building.

Formula for calculating boiler power

To obtain the basic value of thermal power ( $W_{base}$ ), the following relationship is used:

$$W_{base} = \frac{S \cdot H \cdot q_{ud} \cdot k_{reg}}{1000}$$

Where:

  • $S$ - heated room area (m²).
  • $H$ - ceiling height (m).
  • $q_{ud}$ - specific thermal characteristic of the building (W/m³).
  • $k_{reg}$ is the climate coefficient of the region.

Specific thermal characteristic ( $q_{ud}$ )

This parameter determines how much heat the house loses through the walls, windows and roof. Our algorithm uses the following average values:

  • 60 W/m³: Old houses, poor insulation, old windows.
  • 50 W/m³: Standard brickwork, average insulation.
  • 40 W/m³: Modern construction, high-quality double-glazed windows, mineral wool/foam insulation.
  • 30 W/m³: Energy efficient houses (thermos), SIP panels.

Climate coefficients ( $k_{reg}$ )

The boiler power directly depends on the temperature delta (the difference between room and street temperatures).

Hot water supply (DHW) metering

If you plan to use a double-circuit boiler or a single-circuit boiler in conjunction with an indirect heating boiler (IBC), you need to provide a power reserve. Heating water is a priority for the boiler automation: the moment the tap opens, the heating is turned off.

For comfortable use of domestic hot water, it is recommended to add to the calculated power:

  • +20-25% — standard safety factor.
  • Or +4-5 kW for each water intake point during flow-through heating.

Fuel consumption calculation

After determining the required thermal power ( $N$ , kW), it is important to estimate future operating costs. Consumption depends on the calorific value of the fuel and the efficiency of the boiler unit.

Basic flow formulas ( $V$ ):

1. Natural gas (G20)

$$V_{gaz} = \frac{N}{9.45 \cdot \eta}$$

Where $9.45$ is the calorific value of the gas (kWh/m³), $\eta$ is the efficiency (usually 0.9 - 0.92).

Average consumption: ~0.11 m³ per 1 kW of power.

2. Solid fuel (Wood/Coal)

$$V_{wood} = \frac{N}{4.0 \cdot \eta}$$

Where $4.0$ is the average calorific value of dry firewood (kWh/kg). The efficiency of solid fuel boilers is lower (0.75 - 0.8).

Average consumption: ~0.4 kg of firewood per 1 kW of power.

3. Electricity

Here the relationship is linear, since the efficiency of the electric boiler tends to 99%.

$$V_{el} = N$$

Consumption: 1 kW of thermal power consumes 1 kW of electrical energy.

Tips for choosing equipment

  1. Power reserve: Always add 10-15% to the calculated value. This is necessary to compensate for drops in gas pressure in winter or voltage drops in the electrical network.
  2. Cascade connection: For houses with an area of ​​more than 300 m², it is more effective to use two lower-power boilers operating in a cascade than one powerful one. This increases system reliability.
  3. Flame modulation: Choose boilers with deep modulation (for example, from 10% to 100%). This will allow the boiler to operate economically in the off-season, when full power is not required.
