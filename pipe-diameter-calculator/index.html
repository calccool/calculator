Pipeline diameter calculator: Online selection of cross-section and flow speed

Selecting the correct pipeline diameter is always a balancing act. A pipe that is too narrow will cause “noise” in the system, vibration and increased load on the pump due to hydraulic resistance. A pipe that is too wide will unjustifiably increase the cost of materials and installation, and in heating systems it can lead to air pollution.

Our engineering calculator will help you find the “golden mean” by calculating the optimal diameter, fluid speed or required flow rate.

How does hydraulic calculation work?

The calculations are based on the fundamental flow continuity equation. For any incompressible fluid (water, coolant), the relationship between flow rate, speed and cross-sectional area is unchanged.

You can solve three types of problems:

  1. Diameter search (d): If you know how much water needs to be supplied (Flow) and at what speed it should move (recommended SNiP).
  2. Search speed (v): To check whether water will make noise in an already laid pipe of known diameter.
  3. Flow search (Q): To find out the capacity of a pipe at a given speed.

Formulas and mathematical model

For those who are used to checking calculations manually, we present the basic hydraulic formulas used in our algorithm.

Basic flow formula

$$Q = v \times A$$

Where:

  • Q — liquid flow (m³/s)
  • v — flow speed (m/s)
  • A — open section area of ​​the pipe (m²)

Calculation of internal diameter

To find the diameter, we rearrange the formula for the area of ​​a circle:

$$d = \sqrt{\frac{4 \times Q}{\pi \times v}}$$

Important: The calculator automatically converts the units you enter (such as m³/h to m³/s and millimeters to meters) to ensure accurate measurements.

Nominal diameter (DN) and actual diameter

Pay attention to the result "Nearest standard (DN)".

The calculation formula gives a mathematically accurate internal diameter (for example, 34.2 mm). However, pipes in stores are sold in discrete sizes (DN 32, DN 40, etc.).

  • If the calculated value (for example, 34.2 mm) falls between the standards, the engineer will usually select more diameter (DN 40) to reduce resistance.
  • The choice of a smaller diameter (DN 32) is permissible only if the design speed does not exceed the noise threshold.
