Voltage Stabilizer Calculator LM317 (LM350, LM338): Online Calculation

Reference book and calculation of adjustable stabilizer

LM317 is a legendary chip familiar to every radio amateur. This is an adjustable three-terminal positive voltage regulator capable of delivering up to 1.5 A of current over a range of 1.25 V to 37 V.

Our online calculator will help you not only select resistors to obtain the desired voltage, but also (more importantly) check whether your microcircuit will burn out from overheating. The algorithm is also suitable for more powerful analogues: LM350 (up to 3 A) and LM338 (up to 5 A).

Voltage calculation formula

The operation of the LM317 is based on maintaining a reference voltage ( $V_{ref}$ ) equal to 1.25 Volt between the output (OUT) and the control terminal (ADJ). This voltage drops across resistor $R_1$ , creating a constant current.

Basic formula for calculating output voltage:

$$V_{out} = 1.25 \times \left(1 + \frac{R_2}{R_1}\right)$$

Note: The exact engineering formula takes into account the control pin current ( $I_{adj}$ ), but it is so small (about 50-100 µA) that it is often neglected in amateur calculations.

How to choose resistors?

  1. Resistor R1 (Reference): The manufacturer recommends installing the $R_1$ in the 240 Ohm range. This is necessary to ensure the minimum load current (about 5-10 mA) that the microcircuit requires for stable operation. If you set the $R_1$ to a value that is too large (eg 1k ohm) without a load connected, the output voltage may be higher than rated.
  2. Resistor R2 (Adjustment): It is this resistor that sets the output voltage. Typically, instead of a fixed resistor, a potentiometer (variable resistor) is used to create an adjustable power supply.

Thermal Mode and Radiator

The most common mistake when designing a power supply on the LM317 is ignoring heating. A linear stabilizer works like a “smart resistor”, burning excess voltage into heat.

The power that the chip must dissipate ( $P_d$ ) is calculated as follows:

$$P_d = (V_{in} — V_{out}) \times I_{load}$$

Example: If you supply 24V and want to get 5V at 1A, the chip will have to dissipate 19 Watts of heat!

  • Without a heatsink, the LM317 can only dissipate about 0.25 - 0.5 W.
  • With a small radiator - up to 5-10 W.
  • For powers above 10-15 W, massive radiators with active cooling (cooler) are required.
