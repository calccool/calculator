Cable Voltage Drop Calculator (AC/DC)

Online calculation of voltage drop in a conductor

When designing electrical wiring, many pay attention only to the permissible current (so that the cable does not burn out), but forget about voltage loss. This is a critical error, especially for long lines. Our calculator will help you determine exactly what voltage will reach your equipment and whether it meets the standards.

Why is it important to count losses?

Every conductor has resistance. When current flows through it, some of the energy is spent heating the wire, and the voltage at the end of the line becomes less than at the beginning.

If the voltage drop is too high (usually more than 5%), this leads to dire consequences:

  • Glimmer: Incandescent lamps shine at half strength, LED lamps may flicker.
  • Equipment breakdown: Refrigerators and pumps can burn out due to inrush currents at low voltage.
  • Cable overheating: The lost energy turns into heat, destroying the insulation.

How does the calculator work?

The tool takes into account all physical parameters of the circuit:

  1. Core material: Copper has lower resistance ( $0.0175 \text{ Ом} \cdot \text{мм}^2/\text{м}$ ) than aluminum ( $0.028 \text{ Ом} \cdot \text{мм}^2/\text{м}$ ), so there is less loss.
  2. Line length: The longer the cable, the greater the loss.
  3. Section: A thin wire creates more resistance to current flow.
  4. Temperature: When heated, the resistance of metals increases. Our calculator allows you to take into account the operating temperature of the cable for maximum accuracy.

Calculation formulas (Theory)

The calculation is based on Ohm's law and the conductor resistance formula.

1. Single-phase network (220 V) and DC

Losses are calculated for two wires (phase + zero, current flows back and forth):

$$\Delta U = \frac{2 \cdot L \cdot I \cdot \rho}{S}$$

2. Three-phase network (380 V)

For a three-phase system, the formula takes into account the phase shift:

$$\Delta U = \frac{\sqrt{3} \cdot L \cdot I \cdot \rho \cdot \cos\varphi}{S}$$

Where:

  • $L$ - line length (m).
  • $I$ - current strength (A).
  • $\rho$ - resistivity (Ohm mm²/m).
  • $S$ - cable cross-section (mm²).
  • $\cos\varphi$ - power factor (important for reactive loads, such as electric motors).

Acceptable standards (PUE)

According to the standards, the permissible voltage losses are:

  • Up to 3-5% - for indoor lighting networks and sensitive electronics.
  • Up to 10% - for power networks and motors (in some cases, for example, during startup).

Our calculator will automatically highlight the result: Green, if the losses are normal, or Red, if the cable cross-section needs to be increased.
