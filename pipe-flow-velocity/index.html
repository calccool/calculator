Pipeline Fluid Velocity Calculator + Reynolds Number Calculator

When designing pipeline systems (water supply, heating, oil pipelines), the speed of fluid movement is a critical parameter. This is a balance between efficiency and reliability:

  • Speed ​​too high causes water hammer, cavitation, erosion of pipe walls and loud noise. This also leads to significant pressure losses, requiring more powerful pumps.
  • Speed ​​too low leads to siltation (sedimentation of suspended matter), stagnation, and in heating systems - to cooling of the coolant before it reaches the radiator.

Our calculator allows you to instantly find the flow rate ( $V$ ), knowing the flow rate ( $Q$ ) and the internal diameter of the pipe ( $d$ ), and also determine Reynolds number to understand the nature of the flow.

Calculation formulas

The calculation is based on the flow continuity equation. For incompressible fluids, flow rate is equal to the product of cross-sectional area and velocity.

Basic speed formula:

$$V = \frac{Q}{A}$$

Where:

  • $V$ - flow speed (m/s)
  • $Q$ - volume flow ( $м^3/c$ )
  • $A$ - open cross-sectional area of ​​the pipe ( $м^2$ )

Considering that the pipes have a circular cross-section, the area is calculated as:

$$A = \frac{\pi \cdot d^2}{4}$$

Since in practice flow is often measured in $м^3/ч$ and diameter in $мм$ , the calculator automatically converts the units. The final formula for engineering calculations:

$$V = \frac{Q \cdot 353.68}{d^2}$$

Flow Regimes and Reynolds Number (Re)

Speed ​​alone is not enough for accurate hydraulic calculations. It's important to know how exactly the liquid moves: in layers or randomly mixing. For this purpose, a dimensionless criterion is used - the Reynolds number ( $Re$ ).

Reynolds number formula:

$$Re = \frac{V \cdot D}{\nu}$$

Where $\nu$ (nu) is the kinematic viscosity of the liquid (measured in Stokes or $м^2/с$ ). The viscosity of water at 20°C is approximately 1 cSt, but for oil it can be hundreds of times higher, which radically changes the flow pattern.

Interpretation of Re values:

  1. Laminar mode ( $Re < 2300$ ): The liquid moves in even layers without mixing. Typically for viscous liquids (oils, fuel oil) or very low speeds. Friction resistance is minimal.
  2. Transition Mode ( $2300 < Re < 4000$ ): An unstable state where laminar flow can change to turbulent flow. In calculations, it is recommended to avoid this zone or take coefficients as for the turbulent regime.
  3. Turbulent mode ( $Re > 4000$ ): The liquid is actively mixed, and vortices arise. This is the main mode of operation of most water supply and heating systems. Heat exchange is more efficient, but the hydraulic resistance is higher.

Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

Does pipe pressure affect flow rate?

Directly - no. The speed depends only on the flow rate and cross-section (for incompressible fluids). However, pressure differential is the driving force that creates this flow.

Why is it important to know the inner diameter?

Pipe DN 50 (2 inches) can have different internal diameters depending on the material (steel, copper, plastic). An error of 5 mm can result in a speed error of up to 10-15%.

What to do if the speed is too high?

It is necessary to either reduce the flow rate (close the valve, reduce the pump speed), or select a pipe of a larger diameter. Increasing the diameter by 2 times reduces the flow rate by 4 times.
