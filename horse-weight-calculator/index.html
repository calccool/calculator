Horse weight calculator by measurements: accurate calculation and feeding standards

Determining a horse's body weight is not just a curiosity, but a critical aspect of responsible animal ownership. Unlike dogs or cats, it is impossible to put an adult horse on a household scale, and specialized platform scales are expensive and rarely found in private stables.

Knowing weight is necessary for:

  • Calculation of drug dosage: Most anthelmintics, antibiotics and anesthetics are dosed strictly per 100 kg of live weight. A mistake “by eye” can lead to intoxication or lack of therapeutic effect.
  • Diet composition: A horse should consume 1.5–2.5% of its dry matter weight per day. Underfeeding leads to loss of condition, overfeeding leads to laminitis and metabolic syndrome.
  • Load control: Work under the saddle should not exceed 15–20% of the horse's weight (including the weight of the rider and tack).

Accuracy of the measurement method

Research shows that visual weight assessment, even by experienced horse owners, is wrong by an average of 20%. Our calculator uses a modified Carroll and Huntington formula, which gives an error of less than 5% compared to professional scales.

How to use the weight calculator

To get an accurate result, you will need a regular soft measuring tape and a helper to fix the horse in a level position.

Instructions for taking measurements

  1. Bust: Wrap the tape around the horse's chest at the deepest point - just behind the withers and just behind the elbows of the front legs. The tape should fit snugly, pressing the fur, but not cutting into the skin.
  2. Oblique body length: Measure the distance from the glenohumeral joint (the most prominent tuberosity in front of the shoulder) to the ischial tuberosity (the most posterior point under the tail). The tape should run diagonally along the side.

Mathematical calculation model

Our tool is based on an algorithm that takes into account body volume parameters and tissue density coefficient, which changes with the age of the animal.

Mathematically, the formula looks like this:

$$W = \frac{G^2 \times L}{C}$$

Where:

  • $W$ - desired weight in kilograms;
  • $G$ - chest circumference in centimeters;
  • $L$ - oblique length of the body in centimeters;
  • $C$ - coefficient (11900 for adults, 11800 for yearlings, 11700 for foals).

Calculation of daily diet based on weight

After calculating your weight, our calculator will offer you a feeding analysis. The balance between roughage (hay) and concentrated feed (oats, muesli) depends on physical activity.

Feeding recommendations depending on load

  • Light work (walking in hands, light playpen): 90-100% of the diet should be high-quality hay. 1.8-2% of dry matter by body weight is sufficient.
  • Average load (daily training, jumping): The share of concentrates increases to 20-30%. About 2.2% dry matter is required.
  • Heavy load (races, runs, competitions): Concentrates can make up up to 40-50% of the diet to maintain energy balance.

Checklist for correct measurements

  • The horse stands on a flat, hard surface.
  • The animal’s legs are “squared” (vertically and parallel to each other).
  • The head is raised to a natural working position (not lowered to the grass).
  • Measurements are taken while exhaling (for chest circumference).

Using this calculator allows you to avoid dangerous obesity or malnutrition by adjusting your partner's diet in time.
