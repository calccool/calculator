Heated floor pipe flow calculator: calculation of loop length and number of circuits

When installing a water heated floor (WHF), errors in calculations are costly. If you buy less pipe than you need, you will have to make extra connections (fittings) in the screed, which is strictly not recommended due to the risk of leaks. If you buy with a large margin, this is an extra expense, since high-quality pipe made of cross-linked polyethylene (PEX) or heat-resistant polyethylene (PE-RT) is not cheap.

Our calculator allows you to determine not only the total footage, but also the number of hydraulic loops (circuits) so that the system works efficiently and heats the room evenly.

Calculation method and formulas

The calculator algorithm is based on building codes and practical experience of installers. The final length of the pipe consists of the usable laying area and transit sections (connection to the manifold cabinet).

The basic formula for calculating pipe flow is as follows:

$$L = \left( \frac{S}{N} \right) \times k + (D \times 2)$$

Where:

  • L — final pipe length (m);
  • S — area of ​​the room under the heated floor (m²);
  • N — pipe laying pitch (m);
  • k — safety factor for turns and bends (usually 1.1, that is, 10%);
  • D — distance from the room to the collector (m).

How to choose the installation step?

The laying pitch is the distance between adjacent turns of the pipe. The thermal power of the floor and the uniformity of heating (zebra effect) depend on this parameter.

  1. 100 mm (10 cm): It is used in areas with increased heat loss - near panoramic windows, entrance doors and external walls (the so-called “edge zones”), as well as in bathrooms.
  2. 150 mm (15 cm): The optimal pitch for most residential premises when using a pipe with a diameter of 16 mm. Provides comfortable heating without noticeable cold streaks.
  3. 200-300 mm: It is used in industrial premises or if underfloor heating is used only as an addition to radiator heating.

Why is it important to divide the pipe into circuits?

One of the key functions of our calculator is the calculation of the number of loops (contours). Liquid flowing through a pipe experiences hydraulic resistance. If you make one loop too long, the circulation pump will not be able to pump the coolant at the required speed, and the floor at the end of the loop will be cold.

Recommended maximum loop length:

  • For pipe 16 mm: no more than 80–100 meters.
  • For pipe 20 mm: no more than 120 meters.

The calculator automatically divides the total length of the pipe into sections of no more than 80 meters, suggesting how many outlets will be required on the collector group.

Fasteners and additional materials

In addition to the pipe itself, installation requires consumables. Most often, the pipe is attached to mats made of extruded polystyrene foam using anchor brackets (harpoon brackets).

The consumption of staples depends on the installation method (“Snail” or “Snake”), but on average it is taken according to the formula:

$$N_{clips} = L_{pipe} \times 2.5$$

That is, for every meter of pipe, 2 to 3 staples are required, especially often fasteners are placed at turns (arcs) so that the pipe does not spring and violate the laying geometry before pouring the screed.
