Fuse Wire Diameter Calculator: Calculation by Current and Material

In electrical engineering, a situation often arises when a factory fuse-link burns out, and it is impossible to find an identical replacement “here and now.” Repairing a fuse using gauge wire is an acceptable practice if the calculation is done correctly.

Our wire diameter calculator for fuse allows you to determine with high accuracy the required cross-section and diameter of the conductor for a given rupture current (burnout), using the physical law of Preece.

Operating principle and formulas

The calculations are based on the empirical formula (law) of Preece, which relates the melting current to the diameter of the conductor and the characteristics of the material. For wire in free air (not inside sand or oil), the formula looks like this:

$$I = a \cdot d^{1.5}$$

Where:

  • $I$ - melting (burnout) current in Amperes.
  • $d$ - wire diameter in millimeters.
  • $a$ is the material coefficient (Pries constant).

Accordingly, if we need to find the diameter of a wire for a given current, we use the inverse formula:

$$d = \sqrt[3]{ \left( \frac{I}{a} \right)^2 }$$

How to use the calculator

The tool operates in two modes, covering any needs of an engineer or home craftsman:

  1. Diameter selection: Enter the desired trip current (eg 5A) and select the material (eg Copper). The calculator will show how thick the wire needs to be.
  2. Checking the existing wire: If you already have a wire, switch to the "Calculate Current" mode, enter its diameter (measured with a micrometer or caliper), and you will find out at what current it will burn.

Additional features

  • Conversion to AWG: For the convenience of working with foreign cables, we automatically convert millimeters to the American wire gauge standard (American Wire Gauge).
  • Power calculation: By entering the mains voltage, you will see the maximum power that the “bug” can withstand before breaking the circuit.

Important: When repairing fuses (installing “bugs”), the diameter of the wire must be selected for the protection current, which is always higher operating current of the circuit, but below maximum permissible wiring current to prevent fire.
