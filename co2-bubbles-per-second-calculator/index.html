Aquarium CO2 dosage calculator: bubbles per second (BPS) calculation

Carbon dioxide is the “fuel” for photosynthesis, without which it is impossible to create a truly lush natural aquarium or “aquascape”. However, too much gas is lethal to fish, while too little leads to plant stagnation and algae outbreaks.

Our calculator helps solve the main task of the aquarist: converting the abstract need in mg/l into the specific speed of the bubble counter.

How dosage calculation works

Gas supply is usually measured in BPS (bubbles per second - bubbles per second) or BPM (bubbles per minute - bubbles per minute). Our algorithm is based on two techniques:

  1. Empirical method (Basic): Based on statistical data on the relationship between water volume and plant mass density.
  2. Chemical method (Accurate): Based on the relationship between carbonate hardness (KH) and acidity level (pH). This is the “gold standard” for calculating the exact concentration of dissolved gas at the current moment.

Formulas used in calculations

To determine the target CO2 concentration for known water parameters, the Henderson-Hasselbach equation, adapted for aquarium farming, is used:

$$CO2 (мг/л) = 3 \times KH \times 10^{(7 — pH)}$$

Where:

  • KH — degrees of carbonate hardness ( $dKH$ ).
  • pH — current acidity indicator.

To convert the concentration into the flow rate (bubbles), the average flow formula is used:

$$BPM = \frac{TargetCO2 \times Volume}{50}$$

Step-by-step setup instructions

To make your underwater world thrive, follow this algorithm when using the calculator:

  • Determine the net volume of water: Subtract from the total volume of the aquarium the space occupied by soil, stones and driftwood (usually minus 10-15% of the nominal value).
  • Select mode: If you don't have pH/KH tests, use the "Basic" mode. If there are tests, the “Accurate” mode will give 40% more confidence in the result.
  • Start small: Even if the calculator shows 2 bubbles per second, start with 1 and gradually increase the dose over several days.
  • Follow the drop checker: The visual indicator (long test) should be green. Blue - there is not enough gas, yellow - a critical excess.

Why is it important to consider bubbles per minute (BPM)?

For small volumes (nano-aquariums up to 30 liters), the feed rate can be less than one bubble per second. In such cases, adjustment “by eye” becomes impossible. Using the value BPM allows finer adjustment of the needle valve, counting bubbles over a full minute cycle.

Factors Affecting Feed Efficiency

Remember that calculation is the starting point. The actual gas content is influenced by:

  • Diffuser type: Atomizers dissolve gas more effectively than conventional ceramic discs.
  • Current intensity: The strong flow from the filter helps distribute dissolved CO2 throughout the volume.
  • Aeration: Strong disturbance of the water surface or the operation of a compressor actively “weathers” carbon dioxide.
