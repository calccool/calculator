Air Core Coil Inductance Calculator: Online Calculation of Turns and Resonance

Air Core Inductors are the basis of high-frequency electronics. Unlike coils with ferrites, they do not have magnetic hysteresis and do not saturate at high currents. This makes them an ideal choice for:

  • Radio transmitters and receivers (RF circuits).
  • Audio crossovers (filters for acoustics), where the absence of distortion is important.
  • Antenna matching devices.

Our calculator allows you not only to find out the inductance of the finished coil, but also to solve inverse problem: Find out how many turns need to be wound on a mandrel of a certain diameter to get an accurate microhenry (µH) value.

Instructions: How to use the calculator

The tool operates in two modes. Select the appropriate one from the “Calculation mode” drop-down menu.

Mode 1: Find Inductance (L)

Use this mode if you already have a ready-made coil or are planning winding with known parameters.

  1. Diameter (D): Enter the outer diameter of the coil in millimeters.
  2. Winding length (l): Length of the part of the coil covered with wire (in mm).
  3. Number of turns (N): How many full turns of the wire have been made?
  4. Result: You will get the exact inductance in µH and the required wire length (so you know how much to cut from the coil).

Mode 2: Find Turns (N) - for radio amateurs

The most useful feature for design. Let's say you need a coil for 10 µH for the filter.

  1. Enter the desired inductance.
  2. Specify the diameter of the frame (mandrel) and the expected winding length.
  3. The calculator will calculate how many turns you need to wind.

Calculation formula (Wheeler method)

For calculations we use the approximate formula of Harold Wheeler, which is a standard in radio engineering for single-layer cylindrical coils. It provides an accuracy of up to 1% if the winding length is greater than 0.4 of the diameter.

Original formula (in inches):

$$L (\text{мкГн}) = \frac{d^2 \times n^2}{18d + 40l}$$

Where:

  • L — inductance in microhenry.
  • d - coil diameter in inches.
  • l — winding length in inches.
  • n - number of turns.

Our algorithm automatically converts your metric data (mm) to inches, performs the calculation and displays the result in a convenient format.

Calculation of resonant frequency (LC circuit)

A unique feature of our instrument is its built-in resonance calculation. Any inductor connected to a capacitor forms an oscillating circuit.

If you enter the capacitance value of the capacitor (in picofarads, pF), the script will instantly calculate the resonance frequency using Thomson's formula:

$$f = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}$$

This is critical when tuning radios or creating notch filters.

5 tips for perfect winding

  1. Winding density: Try to wind the coil to the coil. If the turns move, the inductance may “float”.
  2. Wire thickness: For high quality factor (Q-factor), use the largest possible diameter wire that will fit into the given winding length.
  3. Parties ratio: The highest quality factor is achieved when the diameter of the coil is approximately equal to its length ( $D \approx l$ ).
  4. Frame: Use dielectrics with low losses (ceramics, fluoroplastic, ABS plastic). Cardboard or wood can absorb moisture, changing the parameters.
  5. Bends: If you are making a coil for an antenna tuner, make taps every 5-10 turns for rough tuning.
