Calculation of battery discharge and charging for home

When using backup power systems (UPS or Inverter + Battery combination), the question often arises: “I used electricity for 3 hours, how much energy is left in the battery and how long will it take to charge it?”

Conventional formulas often give an error, since they do not take into account the efficiency of the inverter during discharge and the loss coefficient during charging. Our calculator solves this problem comprehensively, showing the real state of your system.

How is energy consumption calculated?

To understand how discharged a battery is, it is not enough to simply subtract the Watts. The inverter takes more energy from the battery than it puts into the outlet due to the conversion of 12V to 220V.

The discharge current formula ( $I_{disch}$ ) we use is:

$$I_{disch} = \frac{P_{load}}{V_{bat} \cdot \eta}$$

Where:

  • $P_{load}$ - load power (Watt).
  • $V_{bat}$ - battery voltage (usually 12V).
  • $\eta$ - inverter efficiency (usually 0.85 - 0.9).

Total consumed capacity ( $C_{used}$ ) during operation of $t$ :

$$C_{used} = I_{disch} \cdot t$$

Charging time: why is it different?

Charging time depends not only on the power of the charger, but also on the chemistry of the battery.

  1. Lead-acid (AGM, Gel, Automotive): They have a charge efficiency of about 80-85%. To “fill” 100 Ah into them, the charger must transfer about 120 Ah of energy.
  2. Lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO4): Much more efficient, efficiency is 95-99%. They charge faster with the same current.

Charging time calculation formula ( $T_{charge}$ ):

$$T_{charge} = \frac{C_{used} \cdot K_{chem}}{I_{charge}}$$

Where $K_{chem}$ is the chemistry coefficient (1.2 for AGM/Gel, 1.05 for LiFePO4), and $I_{charge}$ is the charger current.

Instructions for using the calculator

  1. Block “Your system”:
    • Indicate the total capacity of your battery (written on the case, for example, 100 Ah or 200 Ah).
    • Leave the inverter efficiency at 0.9 if you don’t know the exact data.
  2. Block "Consumption":
    • Enter the average load in Watts (for example, gas boiler + laptop = 150 W).
    • Indicate how long the system worked without light.
  3. Block "Charging":
    • Indicate the amperage of your charger (often 10A or 20A for external charges, or the built-in UPS rating).
    • Select the battery type to correctly account for losses.

The calculator will show a visual scale of the remaining charge and the exact time required to restore energy.
