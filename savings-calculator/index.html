Savings accumulation calculator: Compound interest with capitalization and inflation

Planning for your financial future isn't just about putting money away under your mattress. Due to inflation, cash loses its purchasing power over time. To create significant capital, you must use force compound interest and regular investments. Our savings calculator allows you to simulate different financial scenarios, taking into account real economic factors such as rising prices and taxes.

How does the calculator work and what does it take into account?

Unlike simple deposit calculators, this tool offers a professional calculation model. You can vary the parameters to see how even a small increase in your monthly premium affects your bottom line over 10, 20, or 30 years.

The tool takes into account the following parameters:

  • Compound Interest: A mechanism in which profit is accrued not only on the starting amount, but also on the already accumulated interest.
  • Indexation of contributions: In real life, your salary and ability to save are growing. The “Increase in contribution” option allows you to set an annual increase in your deposits (for example, by 5% per year).
  • Real inflation: The calculator discounts the total amount, showing "Purchasing Power" - that is, how many goods you can buy with the accumulated millions in prices today day.
  • Taxation: Calculation of net profit after payment of tax (for example, personal income tax) from investment income.

Calculation formulas (Mathematical Basis)

For those who want to understand the mathematics of the process, the calculator uses modified Time Value of Money formulas.

1. Basic compound interest formula (without additions):

$$FV = PV \times (1 + r)^n$$

Where:

  • $FV$ - Future Value.
  • $PV$ - Present Value.
  • $r$ — Interest rate for the period.
  • $n$ — Number of periods.

2. Real rate of return (Fisher Equation):

To understand whether you are ahead of inflation, Fisher's formula is used. It shows the real effectiveness of investments.

$$1 + r_{real} = \frac{1 + r_{nominal}}{1 + i}$$

Where $i$ is the inflation rate. If your yield is 8% and inflation is 4%, then real capital growth is not 4%, but approximately 3.85%.

Why is indexation of contributions important?

Many investors make the mistake of fixing the monthly replenishment amount for decades in advance. However, $100 today and $100 in 15 years are different amounts. Using the field "Increase in contribution" in our calculator, you can set up an automatic increase in the investment amount (for example, following the indexation of your salary). This is a critical factor in achieving large financial goals such as purchasing real estate or retiring in retirement (FIRE).
