Time lapse calculator: calculate interval, number of frames and memory

Time lapse (interval photography) is a technique in which the camera takes a series of photographs at certain intervals, which are then combined into a video sequence. As a result, slow processes (cloud movement, flower blooming, construction) appear accelerated on the screen. To obtain a high-quality result, it is necessary to accurately calculate the technical parameters before starting to operate the shutter.

How does the time lapse calculator work?

This tool supports two main operating modes necessary for professional planning:

  • Calculation of video parameters: used when you know how long you plan to shoot (for example, 2 hours of sunset) and what interval you will set in the camera. The calculator will show the final length of the video.
  • Interval calculation: used when you have strict specifications for the duration of the finished video (for example, a 15-second video) and you want to understand how often you need to take pictures in order to meet the allotted shooting time.

Mathematical calculation model

Three main variables are used for accurate planning. Below are the formulas used for calculations in our service:

  1. Total number of frames ( $N$ ): $$N = \frac{T_{shoot}}{I}$$ where $T_{shoot}$ is the total shooting time in seconds, $I$ is the interval between frames.
  2. Duration of finished video ( $T_{video}$ ): $$T_{video} = \frac{N}{FPS}$$ where $FPS$ is the frame rate (Frames Per Second).
  3. Required memory ( $M$ ): $$M = N \times S_{photo}$$ where $S_{photo}$ is the average size of one file in megabytes.

Recommended shooting intervals

Choosing the interval is the most important creative step. If the interval is too short, the video will turn out “jerky”; if it is too long, the movement will be too abrupt.

Practical tips for the perfect timelapse

To make your project look professional, pay attention to the following aspects:

  • Frame Rate (FPS): The standard for cinema is 24 frames per second, for television - 25 (PAL). If you want maximum smoothness on YouTube or social media, use 30 or even 60 FPS, but remember that this will require twice as many frames and memory card space.
  • Memory capacity: Always calculate the amount of memory with a margin of 20%. When shooting in RAW format (which is recommended for post-processing), one frame can weigh between 25 and 50 MB.
  • Shutter mode: Use full manual mode (M). If you leave the camera in auto mode, an unpleasant flicker may appear in the video due to changes in exposure from frame to frame.
  • Stability: The slightest movement of the tripod will ruin the result. Use a secure support and turn off the image stabilizer on the lens when shooting on a tripod.
