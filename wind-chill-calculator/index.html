Wind Chill Calculator: Accurate calculation of perceived temperature

Have you ever noticed that at -5°C on a windy day the cold gets to your bones more than at -15°C on a completely calm day? This phenomenon is called wind-chill index (or Wind Chill ). Our online calculator will help you find out your real “felt” temperature so you can properly prepare for going outside and avoid hypothermia.

What is perceived temperature and why does wind cool?

An ordinary thermometer measures the air temperature in the shade and at rest. However, our body constantly generates heat, creating a thin layer of heated air around the skin, which serves as a kind of “invisible fur coat”.

The wind blows away this protective thermal layer. The stronger the wind gusts, the faster the body loses heat and the lower the temperature appears. This effect is especially dangerous for exposed skin areas (face, hands), as it can lead to instant frostbite even with thermometer readings that seem safe.

Wind Chill Calculation Formula

To calculate the wind chill index, weather services (particularly in the USA, Canada and the UK) use a standardized formula. It is relevant at air temperatures below 10°C and wind speeds above 4.8 km/h.

The formula looks like this:

$$T_{wc} = 13.12 + 0.6215 \cdot T_a — 11.37 \cdot V^{0.16} + 0.3965 \cdot T_a \cdot V^{0.16}$$

Where:

  • $T_{wc}$ — wind-chill index (perceived temperature in °C);
  • $T_a$ — actual air temperature (°C);
  • $V$ — wind speed (km/h).

Who is this calculator useful for?

Our tool is indispensable for planning outdoor activities:

  1. For tourists and climbers: In the mountains, the wind blows constantly, and the actual temperature may differ from the forecast by 10-20 degrees.
  2. Skiers and snowboarders: When going down a slope, you create artificial wind with your own speed, which enhances cooling.
  3. To parents: Thermoregulation in children works worse than in adults. The calculator will tell you how many layers of clothing to put on your child.
  4. For builders and workers: To assess the safety of working conditions in open areas.

How to dress properly in strong winds?

If the calculator shows a low perceived temperature, follow the layering principle:

  • Base layer: Thermal underwear (synthetic or merino wool) that wicks away moisture.
  • Middle layer: Fleece or wool sweater to keep warm.
  • Outer layer: A membrane jacket or windbreaker (Windstopper) that blocks airflow but allows the body to “breathe.”
