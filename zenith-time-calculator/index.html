True noon calculator: calculating the time of zenith and height of the Sun

Many people mistakenly believe that the Sun is at its highest point at exactly noon according to wall clocks. However true noon - this is the moment when the Sun crosses the local celestial meridian. The gap between civil time and an astronomical event can be more than half an hour.

There are three main reasons for this discrepancy:

  • Geographical longitude: Within one time zone, the time is the same, but the Sun “travels” across the sky from east to west. For cities on different edges of the same belt, the difference in zenith can be up to 60 minutes.
  • Equation of time: The Earth moves around the Sun in an elliptical orbit and with an axis tilt. Because of this, the speed of the Sun's movement across the sky varies throughout the year.
  • Summer time: Changing the hands artificially shifts noon by another hour.

Why do you need to know the solar zenith time?

Knowing the exact moment of true noon and the peak height of the luminary is necessary in a variety of areas:

  1. Photo: The time of the “golden hour” and the calculation of the hardest shadows at the zenith.
  2. Solar energy: Optimizing the angle of inclination of solar panels to achieve maximum efficiency.
  3. Gardening and architecture: Planning of insolation of areas and illumination of premises.
  4. Navigation and astronomy: Determination of coordinates and calibration of instruments.

How to calculate zenith time: formulas and algorithms

Our calculator uses celestial mechanics algorithms to calculate the position of the Sun. It is based on two key parameters: equation of time (EOT) And solar declination ( $\delta$ ).

Mathematical calculation model

To calculate the day of the year ( $N$ ) and the intermediate coefficient $B$ , the formula is used:

$$B = \frac{360}{365.24} \times (N — 81)$$

Then the equation of time in minutes is calculated:

$$EOT = 9.87 \sin(2B) — 7.53 \cos(B) — 1.5 \sin(B)$$

The resulting true noon time ( $T_{noon}$ ) is calculated as:

$$T_{noon} = 12 — \frac{EOT}{60} — \frac{Longitude}{15} + Timezone$$

Where:

  • Longitude: Your longitude (positive for eastern, negative for western).
  • Timezone: Your time zone offset from UTC.

Calculation of the maximum height of the Sun

The angular altitude of the Sun at the moment of zenith depends on the latitude of the observer and the current declination of the star:

$$Alt = 90^\circ — |Latitude — \delta|$$
