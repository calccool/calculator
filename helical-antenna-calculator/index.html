Helical Antenna Calculator: Calculation of Gain, Sizing and Matching

Helical Antenna Design (Axial Mode)

The Helical Antenna, invented by John Kraus, is one of the most popular types of directional antennas due to its simplicity, high gain and, most importantly, circular polarization. This makes it indispensable in satellite communications, long-distance communication systems for FPV drones and Wi-Fi bridges, where the position of the transmitter relative to the receiver can change.

Our calculator calculates antenna parameters in axial radiation mode (Axial Mode), when the maximum energy is directed along the axis of the spiral.

Operating principle and geometry

For the helix to act as a directional traveling wave antenna rather than a simple spike, the circumference of one turn ( $C$ ) must be close to the wavelength ( $\lambda$ ).

Effective operating range:

$$0.75\lambda < C < 1.3\lambda$$

If the turn length is significantly less than the wavelength, the antenna goes into normal radiation mode (as in portable walkie-talkies) and loses its directional properties.

Calculation formulas

Classic empirical Kraus formulas are used for calculations.

1. Geometric dimensions

The wavelength ( $\lambda$ ) is calculated through the frequency ( $f$ , MHz):

$$\lambda = \frac{299792.458}{f}$$

The winding diameter ( $D$ ) is chosen so that the circumference is equal to the wavelength:

$$D = \frac{\lambda}{\pi}$$

2. Gain

The gain depends on the number of turns ( $N$ ) and the winding pitch ( $S$ ). The more turns, the narrower the beam and the higher the gain.

$$G \approx 15 \cdot N \cdot \frac{S}{\lambda} \cdot \left(\frac{C}{\lambda}\right)^2 \quad (\text{безразмерная величина})$$

In decibels:

$$G_{dBi} = 10 \cdot \log_{10}(G)$$

3. Impedance and Matching

A special feature of the helical antenna is its high input impedance. In axial mode it is about 140 Ohm, which does not allow connecting it directly to a standard 50 Ohm cable (SWR will be > 2.5).

$$Z_{ant} \approx 140 \cdot \frac{C}{\lambda} \approx 140 \, \text{Ом}$$

To match a 50 Ohm cable, the calculator suggests using a quarter-wave transformer - a piece of copper strip or wire of a certain length and width, soldered between the connector and the beginning of the spiral.

The transformer resistance is calculated as the geometric mean:

$$Z_{trans} = \sqrt{Z_{ant} \cdot 50} \approx \sqrt{140 \cdot 50} \approx 84 \, \text{Ом}$$

Helpful assembly tips

  1. Reflector: Mandatory part of the antenna. This can be a round disk or a square plate made of metal (or foil PCB). Its diameter must be at least $0.8\lambda$ .
  2. Wire thickness: Does not critically affect the frequency, but does affect the rigidity of the structure. For 2.4 GHz, 1.5–2.5 mm copper wire is usually used.
  3. Frame: PVC pipes are often used for winding. Keep in mind that the plastic has a dielectric constant of $>1$ , which “electrically lengthens” the wire a little. At high frequencies (5.8 GHz) it is better to use thin dielectric spacers rather than a solid pipe.
