Average Blood Pressure (MAP) Calculator: Calculation and Norms

What is mean arterial pressure (MAP)?

Mean arterial pressure (MAP) is the average pressure in the patient’s arteries during one cardiac cycle. Unlike the usual “upper” (systolic) and “lower” (diastolic) indicators, MAP gives a more accurate idea of ​​how efficiently blood flows to vital organs: the brain, kidneys and heart.

Doctors and resuscitators consider MAP a key indicator perfusion (blood supply) tissues. If this indicator falls below a critical level, organs begin to experience oxygen starvation, which can lead to shock and irreversible damage.

MAP calculation formula

Many people mistakenly believe that average pressure is simply the arithmetic mean between the upper and lower numbers. This is incorrect because the heart is in the relaxation phase (diastole) longer than in the contraction phase (systole).

Our calculator uses the standard medical formula to calculate:

$$MAP = \frac{SBP + 2 \cdot DBP}{3}$$

Where:

  • SBP (Systolic Blood Pressure) - Systolic (upper) pressure.
  • DBP (Diastolic Blood Pressure) - Diastolic (lower) pressure.

There is also an alternative recording via pulse pressure ( $PP$ ):

$$MAP = DBP + \frac{1}{3}(SBP — DBP)$$

Why is it important to know your pulse pressure?

In addition to MAP, our calculator automatically calculates pulse pressure (PP). This is the difference between systolic and diastolic pressure:

$$PP = SBP — DBP$$

  • Norm: 30–50 mm Hg. Art.
  • High PP (>60): Often found in older people. Indicates stiffness of the aorta and large arteries, which is a risk factor for heart attacks and strokes.
  • Low PP (<30): May indicate decreased pumping function of the heart (heart failure) or significant blood loss.

Clinical Application

MAP calculation is critical in the following situations:

  1. For sepsis and shock: Physicians strive to maintain MAP $\ge 65$ mmHg. Art. to prevent multiple organ failure.
  2. For stroke: Pressure control is necessary to maintain cerebral blood flow without increasing cerebral edema.
  3. For head injuries: To calculate cerebral perfusion pressure ( $CPP = MAP — ICP$ ).
