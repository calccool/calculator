Calculation of fluid replacement for hypernatremia

Hypernatremia (increased blood sodium levels > 145 mmol/L) is a condition that most often indicates a relative lack of water in the body, rather than an excess of salt. For competent therapy, the doctor needs to know exactly how much free water (water without electrolytes) should be administered to the patient to return sodium levels to target values.

Our online calculator automates this process using the classic Adrogue-Madias formula and taking into account individual patient parameters.

Formula for calculating water shortage

The calculation is based on determining total body water (TBW) and the ratio of current and target sodium.

The formula looks like this:

$$\text{Free Water Deficit} = TBW \times \left( \frac{Na_{current}}{Na_{target}} — 1 \right)$$

Where:

  • TBW — Total Body Water.
  • $Na_{current}$ — Current (measured) sodium level.
  • $Na_{target}$ — Desired (target) sodium level.

Correction speed: why is it important?

The main rule for treating hypernatremia is don't reduce sodium too quickly.

  • In chronic hypernatremia, rapid administration of water can lead to cerebral edema.
  • Recommended sodium reduction rate: no more than 0.5 mmol/l per hour (or 10-12 mmol/l per day).

Our tool allows you to set a “Correction Time” (for example, 24 or 48 hours) and automatically calculates the required infusion rate in ml/hour.

How to use the calculator?

  4. Set Correction time (default 24 hours) to get the fluid flow rate.

Note: The calculated deficit does not take into account current (continuing) fluid losses (sweat, urine, diarrhea), which must be compensated additionally.
