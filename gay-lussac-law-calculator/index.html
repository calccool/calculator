Gay-Lussac's law calculator online: calculation of gas pressure and temperature (isochoric process)

Gay-Lussac's law is one of the basic gas laws of thermodynamics, describing the behavior of an ideal gas at a fixed volume and constant mass. This thermodynamic process is called isochoric. The main physical meaning of the law is as follows: with a constant volume, the pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.

The more we heat a closed container of gas, the faster the molecules move, hitting the walls of the container harder, which leads to an increase in pressure.

Mathematical formula for an isochoric process

In a strict physical sense, the law is expressed through the equality of the ratios of pressure to temperature for two different states of the gas:

$$\frac{P_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2}{T_2}$$

Where:

  • $P_1$ —initial absolute gas pressure;
  • $T_1$ — initial absolute temperature (strictly in Kelvin);
  • $P_2$ - final pressure after heating or cooling;
  • $T_2$ - final temperature.

Our calculator will automatically convert this proportion depending on what value you need to find. For example, to find the final pressure, the derivative formula is used:

$$P_2 = \frac{P_1 \cdot T_2}{T_1}$$

Practical application of Gay-Lussac's law

Understanding the relationship between pressure and temperature is not just dry physics, but a critical aspect of engineering, chemistry and safety. The law manifests itself in the following everyday and industrial situations:

  1. Storage of gas cylinders: Compressed gas cylinders (propane, oxygen, helium) must not be left in the sun. When heated, the volume of the metal flask remains unchanged, but the pressure inside rapidly increases, which can lead to rupture of the shell.
  2. Car tire pressure: In winter, when leaving a warm garage into the cold, the tire pressure drops. In the summer, when driving on hot asphalt, it increases, on the contrary.
  3. Aerosol packages: The warning label “Do not heat above 50°C” on deodorants and paints is directly based on the Gay-Lussac formula.
  4. Medical autoclaves: Heating the sealed chamber increases the pressure, which allows the steam temperature to rise above the usual 100 °C for ideal sterilization of instruments.
