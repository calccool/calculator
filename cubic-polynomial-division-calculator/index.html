How to divide a polynomial of the third degree into a column?

Dividing polynomials (polynomials) is a fundamental operation in algebra that is necessary for finding roots of equations, factoring, and simplifying complex expressions. When we work with an equation of the form $ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d$ , the most efficient method is synthetic fission or Ruffini rule.

Our calculator allows you to instantly divide a cubic equation by a linear binomial $(mx + k)$ , providing not only a ready-made result in the form of the quotient and remainder, but also an interactive graph of the function.

Basic formulas and calculation principles

The division process is described by the following mathematical relationship:

$$P(x) = Q(x) \cdot D(x) + R$$

Where:

  • $P(x)$ - dividend (3rd degree polynomial: $ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d$ );
  • $D(x)$ - divider (linear function: $mx + k$ );
  • $Q(x)$ - quotient (result of division, polynomial of 2nd degree);
  • $R$ is the remainder of the division.

Why use the Ruffini scheme?

The Ruffini diagram is a simplified way of performing polynomial division that replaces the cumbersome "long division".

Advantages of the method:

  1. Speed: Minimum records and calculations.
  2. Visibility: Allows you to easily track odds at each stage.
  3. Search for roots: If the remainder is $R = 0$ , then the value $x = -k/m$ is the exact root of the original equation.

FAQ: Frequently asked questions

1. What if the remainder is not zero?

This means that the divisor is not a factor of the polynomial. You will get the quotient (second degree polynomial) and the numerical remainder $R$ .

2. Is it possible to divide by fractional coefficients?

Yes, our calculator supports entering decimal fractions (with a dot).

3. How to understand the result of division according to the graph?

The graph shows the behavior of the original cubic equation. This helps to visually assess where the roots are and how the function relates to the found quotient.
