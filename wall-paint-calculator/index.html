Formula for calculating paint consumption

To understand the principle of operation of the calculator, we present a mathematical model of calculations. The basic calculation is based on determining the net area to be painted and multiplying it by the standard consumption.

1. Calculation of wall area (gross and net)

First, the total area of ​​the perimeter walls is calculated, from which the area of ​​the openings (windows and doors) is subtracted.

$$S_{walls} = ((L + W) \times 2 \times H) — S_{openings}$$

Where:

  • $L$ - room length;
  • $W$ - room width;
  • $H$ - ceiling height;
  • $S_{openings}$ - total area of ​​windows and doors.

2. Calculation of ceiling area

If painting of the ceiling is required, its area is calculated separately:

$$S_{ceiling} = L \times W$$

3. Final volume of paint

The final volume ( $V$ ) is calculated taking into account the number of layers ( $K$ ) and the safety factor ( $Z$ ), which depends on the type of surface (primed wall or porous plaster).

$$V = (S_{net} \times R \times K) \times Z$$

Where:

  • $R$ - nominal paint consumption (l/m²), specified by the manufacturer;
  • $Z$ - safety factor (usually 1.05–1.15).

Factors influencing material overuse

Even the most accurate mathematical calculation requires adjustment to real conditions. In our calculator you can select the “Safety Factor”, which takes into account the following factors:

  1. Surface absorbency.
    • Primed putty: minimum consumption.
    • Bare plaster or brick: consumption increases by 15–20% due to high porosity.
    • Paintable wallpaper: the relief structure increases the coverage area by 10–15%.
  2. Application tool.
    • Spray gun: the most economical way.
    • Roller (medium pile): standard consumption.
    • Brush: often leads to an uneven layer and overuse.
  3. Backing color.
    • When repainting a dark wall a light color, an additional (third) coat may be required to completely cover the shade.

How to use the calculator

To get the most accurate result, follow the instructions:

  1. Measure the length, width and height of the room with a tape measure or laser rangefinder.
  2. Calculate the total area of ​​all windows and doors (multiply the height by the width of each opening and add up).
  3. Select your paint type from the drop-down list. If you are still undecided, choose "Acrylic" as the most popular option.
  4. Specify the number of layers. Professional recommendation: Always plan on at least 2 coats for rich, streak-free color.
  5. Indicate the volume of the can you plan to buy (for example, 2.5 l, 5 l or 10 l) so that the calculator will tell you the exact amount of container.

Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

Do I need to subtract windows and doors?

Yes, definitely. In a standard room, openings can occupy up to 15-20% of the wall area. If you don't subtract them, you'll end up buying an extra can of paint. Our calculator does this automatically.

Is one coat of paint enough?

In 90% of cases - no. The first layer is a primer and often lies unevenly, being absorbed into the base. The second coat creates the finish and true color. For premium paints with high hiding power, sometimes one coat is enough, but this is the exception.

How to convert kilograms of paint to liters?

Many manufacturers indicate packaging in kilograms. The density of paint is different from water (it is heavier).

  • Average density of acrylic paint: $1.4 — 1.5$ kg/l.
  • Formula: $V (литры) = Massa (кг) / 1.5$ .
  • Example: A 15 kg bucket is approximately 10 liters of paint.
