Water density versus temperature calculator: Calculation of mass and volume (Handbook)

The density of water is not a constant value. Unlike most liquids, water behaves uniquely when temperature changes, which is critically important to consider when designing heating systems, calculating containers (aquariums, swimming pools) or in laboratory research.

Our water density calculator allows you to instantly determine the exact physical characteristics of a liquid, based on the equations of state accepted in international metrology (CIPM).

Why does the density of water change?

The main factor affecting density is temperature.

  1. When heated Most bodies expand and their density decreases. Water follows this rule at temperatures above +4°C.
  2. Water anomaly: In the range from 0°C to +4°C, water contracts when heated.
  3. Maximum Density achieved at temperature +3.98°C and amounts to 999.972 kg/m³ (almost 1 g/cm³).

Calculation formulas

The basic density formula ( $\rho$ ) relates mass ( $m$ ) and volume ( $V$ ):

$$\rho = \frac{m}{V}$$

However, to calculate the dependence on temperature, our calculator uses complex approximation polynomials (for example, the Thiessen formula or the CIPM-2001 equation), which provide high accuracy up to 4-5 decimal places. In a simplified way, the dependence can be described as follows:

$$\rho(t) \approx 1000 \cdot \left( 1 — \frac{(t + 288.94)}{508929 \cdot (t + 68.13)} \cdot (t — 3.98)^2 \right)$$

How to use the calculator?

The tool operates in two modes:

  1. Reference: Enter only the temperature to get the density in kg/m³ and g/cm³.
  2. Engineering: Additionally, enter the volume of your container (in liters or cubic meters). The calculator will automatically calculate mass of water. This is useful to understand whether the ceiling can withstand the weight of a filled pool or how much the water in the heating system pipes weighs.

Please note: The calculator is designed for distilled (fresh) water. The density of sea water will be higher due to dissolved salts (average 1025 kg/m³).
