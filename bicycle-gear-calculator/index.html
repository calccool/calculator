Bike Gear Calculator: Gear Inches, Ratio and Speed

Choosing the right chainring ratio isn't just a matter of comfort, it's key to keeping your knees healthy and performing well on the course. Our calculator allows you to analyze in detail the mechanics of your bike, be it a high-speed road bike, an endurance gravel bike or a mountain bike for steep uphills.

Understanding transmission parameters helps:

  • Compare different bikes: You can tell if the new bike will be "faster" than the old one, even if they have different wheel sizes.
  • Select a cassette and system: Determine whether a low gear is enough for you for a long climb or whether the cadence will not be “wobbly” on the descent.
  • Optimize roll forward: Calculation of the “stacking” will show the actual distance that the bike travels in one revolution of the pedals.

Basic parameters and calculation formulas

For accurate analysis, we use three metrics recognized by the global cycling community.

Gear Ratio

This is the basic ratio of the number of teeth on the front sprocket to the rear sprocket. It shows how many revolutions the rear wheel will make during one full revolution of the cranks.

$$Ratio = \frac{T_{front}}{T_{rear}}$$

Where:

  • $T_{front}$ — number of front sprocket teeth (System);
  • $T_{rear}$ - number of rear sprocket teeth (Cassette).

Bike styling (Rollout / Development)

A metric parameter indicating the distance in meters traveled in one revolution of the pedals. This indicator takes into account the size of your tire.

$$L = \frac{Ratio \times C_{wheel}}{1000}$$

Where $C_{wheel}$ is the wheel circumference in millimeters.

Gear Inches

A historical standard dating back to the era of the Penny Farthing bike. The value is equivalent to the diameter of the drive wheel in inches.

$$GI = Ratio \times D_{wheel}$$

  • Below 30″: "Tractor" gear for very steep climbs.
  • 45″ – 65″: Universal range for the city and flat ground.
  • Above 90″: High-speed gears for sprints and long descents.

How to use an online calculator

Our tool is equipped with a block interactive visualization, which simulates the rotation of your transmission in real time.

  1. Specify the number of teeth: Enter values ​​for the front (drive) and rear (driven) sprockets. Use quick entry buttons for popular configurations (34T, 42T, 50T).
  2. Select wheel size: Select your standard from the dropdown list. This is critical for calculating Gear Inches and speed.
  3. Set cadence: Enter your usual pedaling rhythm (RPM - revolutions per minute). For most amateurs, a cadence of 80–90 rpm is considered optimal.
  4. Instant result: The calculator will give you the final speed, lay and gear ratio.

Calculation of speed from cadence

To understand what speed you will develop in the selected gear, the formula is used:

$$V = \frac{L \times Cadence \times 60}{1000}$$

Where:

  • $V$ — speed in km/h;
  • $L$ - laying in meters;
  • $Cadence$ - number of pedal revolutions per minute.

Tips for choosing a gear

  • For beginners: Try to avoid “chain skew” (extreme positions on multi-speed transmissions). If you have a hard time turning, it is better to increase the cadence in a lighter gear than to break your knees in a heavy one.
  • For singlespeeds and fixes: Choose your Ratio carefully, as you won't have the option to switch. Classic for the city - about 2.7–3.0.
  • Roll-up visualization: Pay attention to the “Simulation” block under the calculator. It clearly shows how the speed of rotation of the wheel relative to the pedals changes when the size of the stars changes.
