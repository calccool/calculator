Choosing the correct dosage of a drug against ectoparasites (ticks and fleas) is not just about following the instructions, but about ensuring the safety of your dog. An insufficient dosage will leave the animal vulnerable, while an excessive dosage can create unnecessary stress on the body. Our interactive calculator uses an algorithm to instantly select the right pill or combination of pills if your trusty friend weighs more than the standard limits.

How the calculation algorithm works

The calculations are based on a strict link between the concentration of the active substance and the body weight of the animal. For each drug, there is a therapeutic window - the weight range in which a specific dosage is most effective.

Mathematically, the calculation of the required mass of the active substance can be represented by the formula:

$$D_{total} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} T_i$$

Where:

  • $D_{total}$ - total concentration of active substance.
  • $T_i$ is the nominal dosage of the selected tablet.
  • $n$ - number of tablets (for large dogs weighing more than 56-60 kg, a combination of several pieces is often required).

Why is it important to use a calculator when choosing a dosage?

Many owners encounter difficulties when the dog’s weight is on the “border” of two dosages or exceeds the maximum. Our service solves three key problems:

  3. Protection visualization: The built-in graph shows the remaining effect of the drug in real time, helping you not to miss the next treatment date.

Instructions for use and precautions

To ensure that the calculator gives the most accurate results, follow these rules:

  • Weigh the dog before making the calculation. Do not use data from six months ago - the animal’s weight may change.
  • Do not split tablets. Unlike conventional medications, the active ingredient in anti-tick tablets may be unevenly distributed. You need to buy exactly the dosage that the calculator gives.
  • Maintain intervals. Use our Protection Schedule to give your next dose on time. Ticks become active immediately after the drug wears off.

Special instructions for owners

If your dog has a genetic mutation (such as the MDR1 defect, which is common in collies and shelties), be sure to consult with your veterinarian before using any oral parasite medications. Despite the high safety of modern isoxazolines (fluralaner, sarolaner), individual intolerance is a factor that cannot be excluded.

Remember that the tablet should be given during or immediately after feeding for better absorption of the active substance in the gastrointestinal tract.
