Instantaneous Water Heater Calculator: Calculation of Power, Flow and Electrical Wiring

How to choose an instantaneous water heater?

Instantaneous water heaters (in common parlance “flow-through heaters”) instantly heat water passing through a powerful heating element or spiral. Unlike storage boilers, they do not have a tank, so the amount of hot water is not limited by volume, but is limited by the power of the device.

The main mistake when choosing is buying a model with insufficient power. For example, a 3.5 kW device is perfect for washing hands in the summer, but in winter it will be impossible to take a shower under it. Our calculator will help you avoid mistakes by connecting three main parameters: Power (kW), Flow (l/min) And Temperature (°C).

Power calculation formula

The physical principle of heating running water is described by the law of thermodynamics. To heat a certain volume of water per unit of time, energy is required.

For household calculations, a simplified formula is used:

$$P = \frac{F \cdot (T_{hot} — T_{cold})}{14.3}$$

Where:

  • $P$ — Water heater power (kW).
  • $F$ — Water flow (liters per minute).
  • $T_{hot}$ — Desired outlet temperature (38-42°C is considered comfortable).
  • $T_{cold}$ — Cold water temperature at the inlet.
  • $14.3$ — Coefficient of heat capacity of water.

If you need to find out which duct a particular device will produce, the formula can be converted:

$$F = \frac{P \cdot 14.3}{T_{hot} — T_{cold}}$$

Factor "Winter-Summer"

This is a critical point that many people miss.

  • In summer the temperature of the water in the main is about +15…+18°C.
  • in winter she falls to +3…+5°C.

This means that to heat water to a comfortable +40°C in winter, the device needs to overcome a delta ( $\Delta T$ ) of 35 degrees, and in summer - only 22 degrees. The same water heater in winter will produce a stream almost 2 times weaker than in summer.

Electrical safety and cable cross-section

Tankless heaters are one of the most powerful household appliances. A regular outlet is rated for a maximum of 3.5 kW (16 Amps). All devices are more powerful Necessarily are connected by a separate cable from the panel to the terminal block.

The current strength ( $I$ ) is calculated using Ohm's law:

$$I = \frac{P \cdot 1000}{U}$$

Where $U$ is the mains voltage (220V or 380V).
