Electromagnet (Solenoid) Force Calculator: Traction Force Calculator

Online calculation of electromagnet lifting force

Designing electromagnetic actuators, locks, relays or lifting mechanisms requires an accurate understanding of the physics of the process. Our calculator allows you to instantly estimate traction force (force of attraction), which the solenoid creates by acting on a ferromagnetic object (armature).

How does the calculator work?

The strength of the electromagnet depends not only on the electrical parameters, but also on the geometry of the system. The tool takes into account key factors:

  1. Ampere-turns ( $N \cdot I$ ): The product of the number of turns of the coil and the current strength. This is the “engine” of magnetic flux.
  2. Sectional area ( $S$ ): The contact area between the core and the armature. The larger it is, the stronger the attraction.
  3. Air gap ( $g$ ): The most critical parameter. The force decreases as the square of the distance. Even 1 mm of clearance can reduce the force significantly.
  4. Core Material ( $\mu$ ): The ability of a material to concentrate a magnetic field.

Calculation formulas (Physics of the process)

The calculation is based on Maxwell's formula for the traction force of an electromagnet. The process is divided into two stages.

1. Calculation of magnetic induction ( $B$ )

First, we determine the magnetic flux density in the air gap. For a chain with a gap, the formula looks like this:

B =
μ0 · N · I g

Where:

  • μ₀ — magnetic constant ( $4\pi \cdot 10^{-7}$ T m/A).
  • N - number of turns.
  • I — current (Ampere).
  • g — size of the air gap (meters).

2. Calculation of the force of attraction ( $F$ )

Knowing induction, we can find the force. Please note that the force depends on the square of the induction:

F =
B² · A 2 · μ0

Where:

  • F — force in Newtons (N).
  • A — cross-sectional area of ​​the pole (m²).

Our calculator automatically converts the result from Newtons to kilogram-force (kgf) for ease of perception (1 kgf ≈ 9.81 N).

Effect of Core Material

The magnetic permeability ( $\mu$ ) of a material shows how many times it enhances the magnetic field compared to a vacuum.

Important about saturation: The formulas work ideally only in the linear magnetization zone. Most ferromagnets (steel, iron) have a saturation limit of about 1.6 – 2.0 Tesla. If the calculator shows an induction ( $B$ ) higher than this value, the actual force will be significantly less than the calculated one, since the material no longer “absorbs” the field.

Design Tips

  • Reduce the gap: This is the most effective way to increase strength. Reducing the gap by 2 times increases the force by 4 times.
  • Increase the section: A thick core allows for more flow to be generated before saturation occurs.
  • Monitor the heat: Increasing the current gives a linear increase in the field, but a quadratic increase in heating ( $P = I^2R$ ). Use our Ohm's Law calculator to check the heat dissipation.
