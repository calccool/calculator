APR and APY calculator: Converting nominal to effective rate (and vice versa)

In the world of finance, the difference between APR And APY could cost you thousands of dollars. Banks and lenders often use these terms to make an offer seem more attractive, but they are not always transparent. Our calculator will help you see the real picture by converting the nominal rate into an effective rate taking into account the frequency of capitalization.

What is the difference between APR and APY?

The main difference lies in compound interest (Compound Interest).

  • APR (Annual Percentage Rate) - This nominal annual interest rate. It shows how much interest is accrued per year without taking into account the reinvestment of income (capitalization). Most often used in mortgages and auto loans to indicate a "low" rate.
  • APY (Annual Percentage Yield) - This efficient annual return. It takes into account the frequency of interest calculations (daily, monthly, etc.) and the effect of compound interest. This is the real figure that you will earn on a deposit or pay off a debt.

The investor's golden rule:

  • When you take out a loan, look at APR (the lower the better).
  • When you open a deposit, look at APY (the higher the better).

Calculation formulas

Our calculator uses international financial formulas for instant conversion. You can check the calculations manually:

1. Convert APR to APY (Nominal → Effective)

If you know a simple rate, but want to know the real income taking into account capitalization:

$$APY = \left( 1 + \frac{APR}{n} \right)^n — 1$$

2. Convert APY to APR (Effective → Nominal)

If you know the final yield and need to find out the base rate:

$$APR = n \times \left( (1 + APY)^{\frac{1}{n}} — 1 \right)$$

Where:

  • $APR$ - nominal rate (in decimal form, i.e. 5% = 0.05).
  • $APY$ - effective yield.
  • $n$ — number of capitalization periods per year (12 for monthly, 365 for daily).

How to use the calculator?

  1. Select mode: Do you want to know the actual yield (APY) or find the base rate (APR)?
  2. Enter your bid: Indicate the percentage that the bank or broker offers you.
  3. Specify frequency: How often is interest calculated? This is the most important parameter. For credit cards this is often “Daily”, for mortgages it is “Monthly”.
  4. Amount (optional): Enter the amount of your deposit or debt to see the difference not only in interest, but also in real money.

Example from life

You choose between two deposits:

  • Jar: offers 12% per annum with interest accrual at the end of the year.
  • Bank B: offers 11.5% per annum, but with monthly capitalization.

At first glance, Bank A is more profitable. But if you convert Bank B to APY:

$APY = (1 + 0.115/12)^{12} — 1 \approx 12.13\%$

Conclusion: Bank B will bring in more money despite the lower nominal rate.
