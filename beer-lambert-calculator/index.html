Bouguer-Lambert-Beer Law Calculator: Calculate Absorbance, Transmittance and Concentration

Spectrophotometry is one of the most common methods of quantitative analysis in chemistry, biology and pharmaceuticals. This method is based on Bouguer-Lambert-Beer law, which relates the absorption of light by a substance to its concentration and layer thickness.

Our calculator allows you to solve two main tasks for a laboratory assistant:

  1. Direct task: Find the optical density (Absorbance), knowing the parameters of the substance.
  2. Inverse problem: Calculate the unknown concentration of the solution based on the data obtained from the device (spectrophotometer).

Formula and physical meaning

The law states that the optical density of a solution is directly proportional to the concentration of the absorbing substance and the length of the beam path.

Mathematical expression:

$$A = \varepsilon \times l \times c$$

Where:

  • $$A$$ (Absorbance) - Optical density (absorption). Dimensionless quantity.
  • $$\varepsilon$$ (Epsilon) — Molar absorption (extinction) coefficient. Characterizes how strongly a given substance absorbs light at a specific wavelength. Unit: $$M^{-1} \cdot см^{-1}$$ .
  • $$l$$ (Path length) — Optical path length. Usually this is the width of the cuvette (standard - 1 cm).
  • $$c$$ (Concentration) — Molar concentration of the solution (mol/L or M).

Relationship between absorption and transmission

The screens of many devices display not only absorption (

$$A$$

), but also transmission (

$$T$$

or $$%T$$ ).

  • Transmittance ( $$T$$ ) is the ratio of the intensity of light transmitted through a sample to the intensity of incident light.
  • Absorbance ( $$A$$ ) is the logarithmic reciprocal of transmittance.

Conversion formulas:

$$A = -\log_{10}(T)$$

$$T = 10^{-A}$$

How to find concentration using Beer's law

The most common scenario for using the calculator is determining the concentration of an unknown sample. If you know the molar absorption coefficient of a substance (tabular value) and measure

$$A$$

on a spectrophotometer, the formula is converted as follows:

$$c = \frac{A}{\varepsilon \times l}$$

Tips for using the calculator

  1. Units of measurement: Pay close attention to the size. Our tool automatically converts millimoles (mM) and micromoles (µM) to Moles, but this is a common mistake when doing manual calculations.
  2. Accuracy range: Beer's law works ideally for dilute solutions (usually $$A < 1.0$$ ). At high concentrations ( $$A > 2.0$$ ), the linear relationship is broken due to intermolecular interactions. In such cases, the sample should be diluted.
  3. Cuvette selection: A standard cuvette has a path length of 1 cm (10 mm). If you are using micro cuvettes or flow cells, be sure to change the $$l$$ parameter in the settings.
