Credit card repayment calculator: Calculate overpayment and actual term

Credit cards are a convenient financial instrument that instantly turns into a debt trap if you make only the minimum payments recommended by the bank. Our calculator allows you to simulate two debt repayment scenarios and see the real picture of overpayment.

The tool performs an iterative calculation based on the declining balance (Amortization Schedule), taking into account the daily compounding of interest, normalized to a monthly period.

Key Features

  • Deadline calculation: Precise determination of the date when the card balance becomes zero.
  • Overpayment analysis: Demonstration of how much money you will give to the bank in excess of the purchase amount.
  • Comparison of strategies: A clear difference between an “infinite” minimum payment and a fixed amount.
  • Visualization: Interactive graph of debt melting.

Why is minimum payment a trap?

Most banks set the minimum payment at 2-5% of the debt amount. This creates the illusion of loan availability, but mathematically stretches the payment over decades.

Debt Spiral Mechanics:

  1. The amount of debt is decreasing slowly.
  2. Since the payment is tied to a percentage of the balance (for example, 5%), the payment amount also decreases each month.
  3. As a result, the body of the debt (Principal) is paid off in pennies, and most of the money goes to cover interest (Interest).

Mathematical calculation formulas

For those who want to understand how the calculator works under the hood, we use the following financial formulas. The calculation is carried out cyclically for each month $n$ .

1. Calculation of monthly interest rate:

$$r_{month} = \frac{R_{year}}{100 \times 12}$$

Where $R_{year}$ is your annual rate.

2. Calculation of accrued interest for the month:

$$I_n = B_{n-1} \times r_{month}$$

Where $B_{n-1}$ is the debt balance at the beginning of the month.

3. Calculation of the part of the payment used to repay the debt:

$$P_{principal} = P_{total} — I_n$$

Where $P_{total}$ is the amount of your payment.

4. New debt balance:

$$B_n = B_{n-1} — P_{principal}$$

How to close a credit card faster?

If the calculator shows dismal numbers, use one of these proven financial strategies to speed up your repayment:

  1. Avalanche method: Make fixed payments, directing all available funds to the card with the highest interest rate. This is mathematically the most profitable method, minimizing the final overpayment.
  2. Snowball method: If you have several credit cards, pay off the one with the least debt first. This gives the psychological effect of victory and motivation to move on.
  3. Refinancing: If the card rate is above 20-25%, consider taking out a personal loan with a lower rate to close the card.
  4. Payment fixation: Never reduce the payment amount, even if the bank asks for less next month. Pay the same as you paid in the first month (or more).

Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

Does using a card affect the payment?

This calculator assumes that you have stopped using the card and are only working on repayments. If you continue to make purchases, the payment period and overpayment will increase.

What is a grace period?

This is an interest-free period (usually up to 55-100 days). If you repay the debt in full within this period, the interest formula does not apply. The calculator is relevant for cases when you have gone beyond the grace period.

Why does my bank show a different minimum payment amount?

Banks can use different formulas: a fixed percentage of the debt (for example, 3%) or “the amount of interest + a percentage of the debt.” Our calculator uses the most common model, but always check your contract for penny accuracy.
