Online attic roof calculator: Calculation of rafters, sheathing and angle of inclination

A mansard (sloping) roof is an ideal engineering solution for increasing the living space of a house without constructing a full second floor. However, its complex geometry, consisting of four slopes (two lower steep and two upper flat), requires high accuracy in calculations. An error of 5 degrees or 10 centimeters can lead to overuse of materials, leaks or collapse under the snow.

Our calculator was developed by civil engineers. It takes into account not only geometric parameters, but also the physics of materials, snow loads according to SNiP and the economic component of construction.

How the calculator works: step-by-step instructions

To receive an accurate estimate and drawing of the rafter system, fill out three data blocks:

  1. Geometry:
    • Laying width (A1): Distance from the wall of the house to the attic rack. The smaller it is, the wider the room will be, but the steeper the lower slope.
    • Attic width (A2): Net width of living space.
    • Heights (B1 and B2): The height of the attic wall and the height of the ridge, respectively.
  2. Materials and Climate:
    • Select snow area (from I to V). This is critical for calculating the cross-section of the rafters. For example, for region III the load is 180 kg/m², which requires a reinforced frame.
    • Specify the type of coating (metal tile, ondulin, slate) - the weight of the roofing cake depends on this.
  3. Finance:
    • Enter current prices per m² of coating and m³ of wood to get the final turnkey cost.

Formulas and calculation algorithms

The calculator is based on trigonometric functions and the Pythagorean theorem, adapted to building codes.

1. Calculation of the length of the rafters

The roof is divided into right triangles. The length of the rafter leg ( $L$ ) is calculated as the hypotenuse:

$$L = \sqrt{H^2 + W^2} + S_{overhang}$$

Where:

  • $H$ - lifting height (camber B1 or B2).
  • $W$ - laying width (leg A1 or A2/2).
  • $S_{overhang}$ - length of the eaves overhang (taken into account only for the lower rafters).

2. Calculation of inclination angles

The angle of inclination of the slope ( $\alpha$ ) determines the snow melting and wind load:

$$\alpha = \arctan\left(\frac{H}{W}\right) \times \frac{180}{\pi}$$

Important: For the lower slopes of the attic, the optimal angle is considered 60–80° (snow does not linger), for the upper ones - 15–30°.

3. Calculation of roof area

The total area ( $S_{total}$ ) is the sum of the areas of all four slopes, taking into account the overhangs along the gables ( $D_{total}$ ):

$$S_{total} = 2 \times (L_{lower} \times D_{total} + L_{upper} \times D_{total})$$

Calculation of insulation and lumber

The attic is, first of all, a residential area. The calculator automatically calculates the volume of insulation to create a “warm contour”.

  • Insulation: Calculated using the U-shaped contour formula (Walls + Ceiling). We recommend using mineral wool with a thickness of at least 200 mm for the middle zone.
  • Lumber: The volume of wood ( $V_{wood}$ ) is calculated for rafters, mauerlat and sheathing. $$V_{wood} = (L_{total} \times S_{section}) \times 1.1$$ Where 1.1 is the safety factor for trimming (10%).

Common mistakes when building an attic

  1. Incorrect calculation of rafter pitch. With a pitch of more than 60 cm and the use of heavy tiles, the roof may sag. Our calculator will warn you about critical loads.
  2. Ignoring wind load. In high attics, windage plays a big role.
  3. Saving on waterproofing. In sloping roofs, the junctions of the upper and lower slopes (valleys and fractures) are the most vulnerable.
