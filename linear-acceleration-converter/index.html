Linear Acceleration Converter: Convert m/s² to g, km/s² and feet

Linear acceleration is a physical quantity that determines how quickly the speed of a body changes per unit time. There are dozens of different units of measurement used in engineering, physics, and the automotive industry, which often leads to confusion.

Our online acceleration converter solves this problem in a split second. It allows you to instantly convert values ​​from metric to imperial, as well as calculate specific quantities such as overload ( $g$ ) or geodetic galas ( $Gal$ ).

Basic units of acceleration

Depending on the industry, measurement standards can vary dramatically. Below is a list of the most popular units that our tool supports:

  • Meter per second squared ( $m/s^2$ ): The base unit of the SI system. Used in most physical formulas.
  • Standard Gravity ( $g$ ): A unit equal to the acceleration of gravity on Earth. Widely used in aviation and astronautics to measure overloads.
    • $1 g \approx 9.80665 \ m/s^2$
  • Gal ( $Gal$ ): Named after Galileo Galilei. Used in gravimetry and geodesy. Equal to 1 cm/s².
  • Foot per second squared ( $ft/s^2$ ): Standard unit in countries with an imperial system of measures (USA, UK).

Conversion formulas and principles

The basic formula for linear acceleration is as follows:

$$a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$$

Where:

  • $a$ - acceleration
  • $\Delta v$ - change in velocity
  • $\Delta t$ - time during which the change occurred (time interval)

To convert values, our calculator uses conversion factors relative to the base unit ( $m/s^2$ ).

Practical examples of acceleration

To better understand the values ​​that the converter produces, it is useful to compare them with real physical phenomena. We have compiled a reference table of typical accelerations:

  1. Gravity on the Moon: $1.62 \ m/s^2$ (roughly $0.16 g$ ).
  2. Passenger aircraft acceleration: Around $0.2 g$ to $0.5 g$ on takeoff.
  3. Emergency car braking: From $0.8 g$ to $1.0 g$ (on dry asphalt).
  4. Space launch: Astronauts experience g-forces from $3 g$ to $4 g$ when inserted into orbit.
  5. Impact in an accident: Can reach short-term peak values ​​in $50 g$ - $100 g$ .
