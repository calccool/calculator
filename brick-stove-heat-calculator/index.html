Brick kiln heat capacity calculator: calculation of power and cooling time

The efficiency of a brick stove directly depends on its ability to accumulate heat during combustion and gradually release it into the room. This process is called thermal inertia. The higher the heat capacity of the structure, the less often it is necessary to add firewood and the more stable the temperature in the house is maintained.

Our calculator allows you to perform the calculation in two ways:

  • By number of bricks: ideal at the stage of purchasing materials or if the order is known.
  • By oven dimensions: suitable for assessing the characteristics of an already erected structure.

Physics of the process and calculation formulas

The calculator is based on the classic formula of thermodynamics for determining the amount of heat. We take into account not only the mass, but also the specific heat capacity of the material, which differs between ceramic and refractory bricks.

Basic formula for heat capacity

To calculate the accumulated energy, the following relationship is used:

$$Q = m \cdot C \cdot \Delta T$$

Where:

  • $Q$ — total amount of thermal energy (kJ, converted to kWh);
  • $m$ — calculated mass of the furnace active mass (kg);
  • $C$ — specific heat capacity of brick (kJ/(kg °C));
  • $\Delta T$ — the temperature difference between the heated array and the air in the room.

Important parameters for an accurate forecast

To make the calculation as realistic as possible, it is necessary to take into account the operating conditions:

Average temperature of the array

When heated, the surface of the brick heats up unevenly. The inner layers near the firebox can reach 300–400°C, while the outer walls are 60–80°C. To calculate heat capacity, it is customary to use the average heating value of the entire array, which is usually 70–90°C.

Heat release time (cooling)

This is the period during which the furnace releases its accumulated energy. According to the standards, for massive furnaces this cycle is 24 hours. Knowing the total amount of accumulated kWh and cooling time, we obtain the average heat transfer power:

$$P_{avg} = \frac{E}{t}$$

Where $E$ — energy in kWh, and $t$ — time in hours.

Why a brick oven is the best heat accumulator

  1. Stable microclimate: Unlike metal stoves (“stoves”), brick does not create sudden temperature changes.
  2. Fuel economy: One or two fires per day is enough to maintain comfort.
  3. Healthy Warmth: long-wave infrared radiation from a heated brick is most beneficial for humans.
  4. Safety: the temperature of the outer walls rarely exceeds safe limits, which reduces the risk of burns.

Recommendations for power selection

It is generally accepted that for heating 10 m² insulated living space is required 1 kW oven power at average winter temperatures. If your calculation shows a power of 3.5 kW, such a stove will effectively cope with a room of about 35 m².
