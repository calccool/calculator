Capacitor energy and charge calculator: Online calculation of RC circuit

In modern electronics, capacitors play the role of temporary energy storage. Understanding how much energy a component can store and how quickly it releases it to the load is critical when designing power supplies, flash units, audio systems, and timing circuits. Our capacitor energy calculator allows you to instantly calculate key electric field parameters without complex manual calculations.

How does the calculator work?

The tool performs calculations based on the three basic laws of electrostatics and electrical engineering. To obtain the result, it is enough to enter the known parameters: voltage ( $V$ ), capacitance ( $C$ ) and, optionally, circuit resistance ( $R$ ).

The calculator determines the following values:

  1. Potential energy ( $E$ ) - the work that can be done by the electric field of a capacitor during discharge.
  2. Electric Charge ( $Q$ ) - the amount of electricity accumulated on the plates.
  3. Time constant ( $\tau$ ) - an indicator of the inertia of the system, which determines the rate of charge or discharge through a resistor.

Basic calculation formulas

For those who want to understand the physics of the process or check the calculations manually, we present the equations used.

1. Energy stored in a capacitor ( $E$ )

Energy is measured in Joules ( $J$ ) and is proportional to the square of the voltage. This means that when the voltage increases by 2 times, the stored energy will increase by 4 times.

$$E = \frac{1}{2} \cdot C \cdot V^2$$

2. Electric charge ( $Q$ )

Charge is measured in Coulombs ( $C$ ) and shows the total number of charge carriers.

$$Q = C \cdot V$$

3. RC circuit time constant ( $\tau$ )

This parameter shows the time during which the voltage on the capacitor will change by a factor of $e$ (approximately 63%). Measured in seconds ( $s$ ).

$$\tau = R \cdot C$$

Important: In engineering practice, it is believed that a capacitor is fully charged or discharged in a time equal to $5\tau$ (five time constants).

Practical Application

Calculation of capacitor energy is necessary in various areas:

  • Pulse technique: Calculation of the power of a camera flash or strobe.
  • Safety: Assess the danger of residual charge in high-voltage capacitors (such as microwave ovens or tube amplifiers) even after power has been turned off.
  • Car audio: Selection of a buffer capacitor (ionistor) to compensate for voltage drops on the subwoofer during peak loads.
  • Defibrillators: Calculation of the exact dose of energy (Joules) to restore heart rhythm.

Calculation example

Let's say we have a capacitor with a capacity 470 µF, charged to voltage 50 Volt, which is discharged through a resistor 2 kOhm.

  1. Energy: $E = 0.5 \cdot 0.00047 \cdot 50^2 = \mathbf{0.5875 \text{ Дж}}$
  2. Charge: $Q = 0.00047 \cdot 50 = \mathbf{0.0235 \text{ Кл}}$
  3. Time constant: $\tau = 2000 \cdot 0.00047 = \mathbf{0.94 \text{ сек}}$
  4. Full rank: $t = 5 \cdot 0.94 = \mathbf{4.7 \text{ сек}}$
