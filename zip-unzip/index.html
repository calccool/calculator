Technical Basics: How the Compression and Extraction Algorithm Works

To understand why some files compress better than others, you need to look at the underlying mathematics of the format. Classic ZIP is based on the DEFLATE algorithm, an elegant combination of Huffman encoding and the LZ77 dictionary algorithm.

The efficiency of an archiver is traditionally measured by its compression ratio. This formula allows you to mathematically estimate how much disk space was saved:

$$K_c = \left( 1 — \frac{V_{zip}}{V_{raw}} \right) \times 100\%$$

Where $K_c$ is the final compression ratio, $V_{zip}$ is the volume of the received archive in bytes, and $V_{raw}$ is the total physical volume of all source files before packaging.

At the same time, the theoretical limit of how much information can be compressed without loss is not infinite. It is strictly determined by Shannon’s information entropy:

$$H(X) = — \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i) \log_2 P(x_i)$$

In this formula, $P(x_i)$ denotes the probability of a certain character (byte) appearing in the data array. If the entropy of the file is high (for example, it is an already compressed MP4 video or an encrypted array), the DEFLATE algorithm will not be able to significantly reduce its size. Text databases (CSV, JSON, HTML), on the other hand, have low entropy and are compressed by 5–10 times.
