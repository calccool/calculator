Why is it important to know your heart rate zones?

The heart reacts to stress in different ways. Depending on the frequency of strokes, the body uses different energy sources: fats or carbohydrates (glycogen).

  • For weight loss: You need to stay in the moderate intensity zone, where fat is the main fuel.
  • For endurance: It is necessary to train the heart muscle in the aerobic zone.
  • For speed: Short sessions in the anaerobic zone and at the limit of MHR (Maximum Heart Rate) are required.

Calculation formulas: Karvonen vs. Standard

1. Calculation of Maximum Heart Rate (MHR)

We offer two options for calculating the maximum:

  • Fox Formula (Classical): $MHR = 220 — Возраст$ . Suitable for beginners.
  • Tanaka Formula (Modern): $MHR = 208 — (0.7 \times Возраст)$ . More accurate for people over 40 and those with sports experience.

2. Calculation using the Karvonen method

This method operates with the concept Heart rate reserve (Heart Rate Reserve - HRR). This is the difference between your maximum and resting heart rate.

$$HRR = MHR — Пульс_{покой}$$

The final target heart rate (Target HR) for a specific zone (for example, 70%) is calculated as follows:

$$Target HR = (HRR \times 0.70) + Пульс_{покой}$$

It's the addition of resting heart rate at the end of the equation that makes this the only true method for creating a training plan.

How to measure resting heart rate correctly?

To accurately calculate according to Karvonen, you need to know your morning heart rate (RHR - Resting Heart Rate).

  1. Take the measurement in the morning, immediately after waking up, without getting out of bed.
  2. Feel for a pulse in your neck or wrist.
  3. Count the number of beats in 60 seconds (or in 15 seconds and multiply by 4).
  4. For maximum accuracy, measure your heart rate for 3 days in a row and take the average.
