Leap seconds calculator: calculate the difference between TAI and UTC online

In the modern world, there are two main time scales that do not always coincide:

  1. International Atomic Time ( $TAI$ ): Ultra-stable scale based on readings from hundreds of atomic clocks. It goes absolutely evenly.
  2. UTC ( $UTC$ ): The same time according to which our smartphones and computers live. It is tied to the rotation of the Earth.

The problem is that our planet is not the most ideal chronometer. Under the influence of tidal friction of the Moon and other geophysical factors, the Earth's rotation gradually slows down. So that the gap between the “ideal” atomic time and the “real” rotation of the planet does not exceed 0.9 seconds, scientists from the $IERS$ service periodically introduce leap seconds.

How is the difference between $TAI$ and $UTC$ calculated

At the time the system was introduced in 1972, the difference between these scales was already 10 seconds. All subsequent insertions widened this gap. The formula for total displacement is as follows:

$$\Delta T = 10 + N_{leap}$$

Where:

  • $\Delta T$ - total difference ( $TAI — UTC$ ) in seconds;
  • $10$ - starting offset on January 1, 1972;
  • $N_{leap}$ - the number of leap seconds added over time.

Will seconds be added in the future?

Currently, the last second was added on December 31, 2016. There is debate in the scientific community about abolishing this practice by 2035, as insertion seconds creates enormous difficulties for IT systems, navigation protocols and distributed databases.

Physical displacement: how much does the Earth's surface "lag behind"?

Leap second is not just a number on the calendar. This is a sign that the Earth’s surface did not physically have time to rotate to the desired angle relative to the stars.

Since the linear speed of rotation of the Earth at the equator is approximately $465.1$ meters per second, we can calculate the physical “lag” of the equator from the ideal schedule:

$$D = \Delta T \times v_{equator}$$

Where:

  • $D$ - distance in meters;
  • $v_{equator} \approx 465.1$ m/s.
