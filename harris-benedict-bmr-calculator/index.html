Harris-Benedict Calculator: Accurate BMR and Daily Calorie Calculation

Harris-Benedict equation is the gold standard in nutrition for estimating basal metabolic rate (BMR). Our online calculator uses the most current revision of the formula (Roza & Shizgal, 1984) to help you determine the exact amount of energy your body needs to function, lose weight, or gain muscle.

What are BMR and TDEE?

Before creating a diet, it is important to understand two key indicators that our algorithm calculates:

  1. BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) - Basal metabolic rate. This is the number of calories your body burns when completely at rest (for example, if you slept 24 hours a day). This energy is spent on breathing, blood circulation, brain function and cell regeneration.
  2. TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) — Daily energy consumption. This is your BMR multiplied by your physical activity ratio. It is from this figure that you need to start when planning your diet.

Calculation formula

Historically, the equation was derived in 1919, but science does not stand still. We use the updated 1984 version, which is considered more accurate for modern people.

The mathematical model looks like this (parameters: W - weight in kg, H - height in cm, A - age in years):

For Men:

$$BMR = 88.362 + (13.397 \times W) + (4.799 \times H) — (5.677 \times A)$$

For Women:

$$BMR = 447.593 + (9.247 \times W) + (3.098 \times H) — (4.330 \times A)$$

How to use the results for your purposes?

Our calculator doesn't just give you dry numbers, it automatically calculates your calorie deficit or surplus and breaks them down into macronutrients (BNM) for a balanced diet.

  • For weight loss (Weight Loss): It is recommended to create a moderate calorie deficit (about 15-20% of TDEE). This allows you to burn fat while preserving muscle tissue and without slowing down your metabolism.
  • For muscle gain: A surplus (excess) of energy is required. We recommend adding about 15% to your TDEE to build muscle rather than fat.
  • Maintenance: Eating calories equal to TDEE will allow you to keep your current weight stable.

Advantages of the Harris-Benedict method

  • Accounting for individual parameters: Unlike simple formulas (like “weight × 30”), this method takes into account height and age, which is critical because metabolism slows down with age.
  • Gender differences: Takes into account the difference in body composition between men and women (men tend to have more muscle mass, which consumes energy).
  • Reliability: It has been used in clinical dietetics for more than 100 years with constant refinements.

Important: The formula gives high precision for people with an average build. For professional athletes with very large muscle mass, it may underestimate the results, and for people with a high degree of obesity, it may overestimate them slightly. In such cases, it is recommended to use the Ketch-McArdle formula, which takes into account the percentage of body fat.
