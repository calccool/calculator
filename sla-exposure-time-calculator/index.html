Photopolymer resin exposure time calculator for SLA/DLP/LCD printing

The accuracy of photopolymer printing directly depends on how accurately the operating time of the UV matrix is ​​selected for each layer. Even the most expensive resin will not give a high-quality result if the exposure settings do not correspond to the current conditions: layer thickness, air temperature or diode power.

This calculator uses nonlinear approximation algorithms to recalculate time when changing layer height and empirical data on the kinetics of chemical reactions when changing resin viscosity.

Physics of the polymerization process

When UV light (usually wavelength 405 nm ) falls onto the resin bath, photoinitiators start a chain reaction of curing. It is important to understand that light decays exponentially in the volume of the polymer. This means that if you increase the layer thickness by 2 times, the exposure time should not strictly increase by 2 times.

For calculations we use the formula for the dependence of time $T$ on layer thickness $h$ :

$$T_2 = T_1 \cdot \left(\frac{h_2}{h_1}\right)^{0.85}$$

Where:

  • $T_1$ - known (base) exposure time;
  • $h_1$ - base layer thickness;
  • $h_2$ - new target layer thickness;
  • $0.85$ - absorption coefficient for most standard resins (Standard/Rigid).

The influence of temperature on the result

Temperature is the most underestimated factor in SLA printing. As the temperature decreases, the resin becomes more viscous and the mobility of the molecules decreases. This slows down the polymerization process.

If the room is colder 25°C, the illumination time must be increased. Our algorithm applies the following adjustment:

$$T_{final} = T_{base} \cdot (1 + \Delta t \cdot 0.08)$$

Here $\Delta t$ is the difference between the ideal and actual temperature. We are pawning 8% increase in time for each degree drop in temperature.

When to use a calculator

Experienced 3D printing masters use parameter recalculation in the following cases:

  • Switch to detailed printing: For example, when moving from a layer 0.05 mm (standard) on 0.02 mm for printing miniatures.
  • Seasonal changes: If the workshop does not have climate control and the temperature has dropped from summer 28°C until autumn 19°C.
  • Using the new resin: If the manufacturer has specified parameters for only one type of layer.
  • Adhesion problems: If the model comes off the table, the calculation of reinforced bottom (Burn-in) layers is required.

Recommendations for setting up “Burn-in” layers

Bottom Layers serve as the foundation of the model. They glow much longer than others to ensure reliable adhesion to the metal platform.

How to adjust adhesion:

  1. Number of layers: Usually enough 4–6 layers.
  2. Illumination time: It should be in 6–10 times higher than that of the normal layer. If the calculator showed 2.5 seconds for the main layer, then for the lower ones you should set it to about 20–25 seconds.
  3. Transition Layers: Recommended to use 4–10 transition layers for a smooth reduction in exposure time, which reduces the risk of delamination due to internal stress.

Important: Always perform a calibration test (such as "The Town" or "Ameralabs Town") after calculating new parameters. The calculator provides a mathematically sound basis, but the physical state of your LCD matrix and the degradation of UV diodes may make adjustments.
