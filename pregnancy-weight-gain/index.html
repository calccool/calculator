Calculator for weight gain during pregnancy by week: Calculation of norms (WHO/IOM)

Controlling body weight while expecting a child is not a matter of aesthetics, but the most important medical indicator of the health of the mother and fetus. Our calculator is based on the latest recommendations from the US Institute of Medicine (IOM) and WHO standards. It takes into account your preconception body mass index (BMI), gestational age and number of fetuses (single or twins), providing a personalized normal range.

Deviations from recommended values ​​may indicate risks:

  • Insufficient set: The risk of having a low birth weight baby (up to 2500 g) and premature birth.
  • Redundant set: Increases the likelihood of gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, fetal macrosomia (large baby) and complications during childbirth.

How is Body Mass Index (BMI) calculated?

The key factor in determining your individual rate of gain is not just your weight, but your pre-pregnancy weight-to-height ratio. To do this, use Quetelet's formula:

$$ИМТ = \frac{W}{H^2}$$

Where:

  • $W$ - pre-pregnancy weight in kilograms;
  • $H$ - height in meters.

For example, if your weight was 60 kg and your height was 1.70 m, the calculation would be as follows: $60 / (1.70 \times 1.70) = 20.76$ .

Table of norms for weight gain (singleton pregnancy)

Depending on your BMI score, you fall into one of four categories. Each category has its own weight gain norms for the entire period (40 weeks) and the rate of gain in the 2nd and 3rd trimesters.

Features of weight gain with twins

If you are expecting twins, the load on the body increases and the growth rates change significantly. In this case, malnutrition can be critical for children's development.

  • Normal weight (BMI 18.5–24.9): The recommended increase is 17 – 25 kg.
  • Overweight (BMI 25.0–29.9): The recommended increase is 14 – 23 kg.
  • Obesity (BMI ≥ 30): The recommended increase is 11 – 19 kg.

What does weight gain consist of?

Many expectant mothers worry that all the weight they gain is fat deposits. However, this is a myth. In a healthy pregnancy, adipose tissue constitutes only a small part of the increase required for lactation. The main weight is distributed as follows (average values ​​at week 40):

  1. Child: 3.0 – 3.6 kg.
  2. Placenta: 0.6 – 0.7 kg.
  3. Amniotic fluid (amniotic fluid): 0.8 – 1.0 kg.
  4. Uterine enlargement: 0.9 – 1.0 kg.
  5. Breast enlargement: 0.4 – 0.5 kg.
  6. Increased blood volume: 1.2 – 1.5 kg.
  7. Tissue fluid (edema): 1.0 – 1.5 kg.
  8. Maternal fat reserves: 2.5 – 3.5 kg.

Weight dynamics by trimester

Weight gain occurs unevenly. The calculator takes into account these physiological jumps:

  • First trimester (1–13 weeks): During this period, the fetus is still very small. An increase in total is considered normal 0.5 – 2.0 kg for all three months. Some women may even lose weight due to toxicosis.
  • Second trimester (14–27 weeks): The period of active growth of the child and increase in blood volume. Weight begins to grow steadily, on average 300–500 g per week (depending on BMI).
  • Third trimester (28–40 weeks): The fetus actively gains subcutaneous fat. The rate of increase remains the same, but just before birth (at 38-39 weeks), the weight may stabilize or decrease slightly due to the removal of excess fluid.
