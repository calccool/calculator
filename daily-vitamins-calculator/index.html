Daily Value Calculator for Vitamins and Minerals

Vitamins and minerals do not have energy value, but they are absolutely necessary for maintaining normal vital functions, cell division and the functioning of the immune system. Our daily vitamin requirement calculator is based on international standards (WHO and NIH recommendations), allowing you to accurately determine your individual nutrient requirements.

Correct calculation of micronutrient intake allows you to:

  • Avoid hypervitaminosis (toxic excess of substances, especially fat-soluble ones: A, D, E, K).
  • Prevent hidden deficiencies (hypovitaminosis), leading to chronic fatigue, anemia and decreased immunity.
  • Balance your diet when switching to restrictive diets (vegetarianism, keto diet, intermittent fasting).
  • Adjust the dosages of pharmacy multivitamin complexes taking into account the current physiological status.

Calculation algorithms and applied formulas

The calculations are based on Dietary Reference Intakes. To assess the adequacy of your current diet, use the formula for calculating the percentage of daily value (Daily Value, DV):

$$\%DV = \left( \frac{m_{\text{intake}}}{m_{\text{norm}}} \right) \times 100\%$$

Where:

$m_{\text{intake}}$ is the amount of a specific nutrient actually obtained from food (in mg or mcg).

$m_{\text{norm}}$ - individual daily requirement calculated by the calculator for your profile.

Correction factors are used to calculate the need for some water-soluble vitamins. For example, for smokers the basic vitamin C intake is increased according to the formula:

$$C_{\text{adj}} = C_{\text{base}} + 35 \text{ мг}$$

Factors that change daily requirements

The body requires different amounts of nutrients at different stages of life. Our algorithm automatically recalculates values ​​if you specify special parameters:

  1. Age changes: In older people (over 50 years old), the synthesis of vitamin D in the skin physiologically decreases and the absorption of vitamin B12 from food products worsens, which requires an increase in their daily intake.
  2. Sex differences: Women of reproductive age require 2–2.5 times more iron due to monthly blood loss. Men require more zinc to maintain testosterone levels.
  3. Pregnancy and lactation: During this period, the need for folic acid (Vitamin B9) increases critically to 600 mcg to prevent neural tube defects of the fetus, and the consumption of calcium, iron and iodine also increases significantly.
