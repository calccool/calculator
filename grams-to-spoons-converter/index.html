Preparing the perfect meal requires pharmacy precision, but you don't always have a kitchen scale at hand. Our smart bulk solids converter solves this problem in a second. He converts the mass from the recipe into the usual household measuring instruments - glasses and spoons - taking into account the physical density of each ingredient.

Why can't all products be measured the same way?

The main mistake of novice cooks is the belief that one glass always contains 200 or 250 grams of any product. This is wrong. There is one volume, but the mass directly depends on the density (specific gravity) of the substance.

For example, sugar is much heavier than oatmeal, so a standard tea glass will fit 200 g of sugar, but only 100 g of Hercules. Our calculator uses the latest density database for the most accurate results.

Mathematical formula for translation

For the calculation, the classical physical formula for the dependence of mass on volume and density is used:

$$V = \frac{m}{\rho}$$

Where:

  • $V$ — final volume (in spoons or glasses);
  • $m$ - specified product mass in grams;
  • $\rho$ - density of a specific product (g/ml or g/cm³).

For the convenience of cooks, we have integrated coefficients for three main types of containers into the code:

  1. Tea glass (thin): 250 ml.
  2. Faceted glass: 200 ml (to the rim).
  3. Tablespoon: ~15 ml (with a slide).
  4. Teaspoon: ~5 ml (with a slide).

Our calculator is equipped with a unique function Smart Breakdown. If a recipe calls for, for example, 350 grams of flour, the system will not just give you a fractional number of cups, but will suggest the ideal combination:

Tips for Proper Measuring

To ensure your calculations are as accurate as possible, follow the three rules of professional chefs:

  • Do not compact: Spoon the flour or starch into the container without patting the top down. Compacted flour can weigh 20-30% more than normal.
  • Use a slide: By default, all world culinary standards (and our calculator) imply scooping the product into a spoon with a small natural slide.
  • Monitor humidity: Sugar and salt are hygroscopic. If the products were stored in a humid room, their density increases, which means that the actual weight in the spoon will be slightly higher than the calculated one.
