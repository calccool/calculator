Theory: Prime and Composite Numbers

In number theory, all natural numbers (except 1) are divided into two fundamental classes. Understanding the difference is critical for algebra, cryptography, and computer science.

Primes

A prime number is a natural number $p > 1$ that has exactly two natural divisors: one and itself.

  • Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17…
  • Property: The number 2 is the only even prime number. All other prime numbers are odd.

Composites

A natural number $c > 1$ is called composite if it has at least one natural divisor other than 1 and itself.

  • Examples: 4, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12…
  • Property: Any composite number can be represented as a product of prime factors.

Special Case: Unit

Number 1 is neither simple nor composite. It has only one divisor (itself), which violates the definition of both classes.

Canonical factorization

If our calculator identifies a number as a composite number, it automatically breaks it down into the “atoms” of mathematics—the prime factors. According to the fundamental theorem of arithmetic, any natural number $n > 1$ can be uniquely represented as:

$$n = p_1^{a_1} \cdot p_2^{a_2} \cdot \dots \cdot p_k^{a_k}$$

Where:

  • $p_1, p_2, \dots$ - prime numbers.
  • $a_1, a_2, \dots$ - natural exponents.

An example of how the calculator works:

For number 360 the result of the decomposition will look like this:

$$360 = 2^3 \cdot 3^2 \cdot 5$$

This means $360 = 8 \cdot 9 \cdot 5$ . This type of notation is called canonical.

Verification algorithms

For small numbers, the method of enumerating divisors or the “Sieve of Eratosthenes” is often used.

For large numbers, our calculator uses optimized algorithms to instantly find factors up to the square root of a number ( $\sqrt{n}$ ). This significantly speeds up the process compared to manual search.

Why do you need to know neighboring prime numbers?

In the calculator output you will see “Nearest primes”. This is useful for estimating "simplicity intervals". For example, between the prime numbers 89 and 97 there are no other prime numbers. This interval (gap) characterizes the distribution of prime numbers in the natural series.

Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

Is 0 a prime number?

No. Zero is not a natural number in the context of divisibility theory (you cannot divide by zero), and it has an infinite number of divisors.

What is the smallest prime number?

This number 2. It is also the only even prime number.

How to check a large number manually?

  1. Find the root of the number ( $\sqrt{n}$ ).
  2. Write down all prime numbers less than this root.
  3. Try dividing your number by each of the prime numbers written down.
  4. If no number divides a number without a remainder, it is prime. Our calculator does this automatically in milliseconds.
