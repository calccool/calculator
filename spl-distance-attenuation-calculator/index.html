Sound pressure (SPL) and sound attenuation over distance calculator

Calculating Sound Pressure Level (SPL) is a critical step when designing audio systems, organizing concerts, or setting up a home theater. The main difficulty is that sound decays not linearly, but logarithmically, according to the physical law of “inverse squares”.

Our calculator allows you to instantly calculate the final volume at your listening position using two modes:

  • Base: calculation of volume changes with distance from a source with a known SPL level.
  • Advanced: calculation “from scratch”, based on the characteristics of the speaker system (AS) and amplifier power.

Physics of sound attenuation

Under ideal conditions (open space), for every doubling of the distance from a point source, the sound pressure level decreases by 6 dB. This occurs because the energy of the sound wave is distributed over the area of ​​the sphere, which grows in proportion to the square of the radius.

The following formula is used for calculations:

$$SPL_2 = SPL_1 — 20 \cdot \log_{10} \left( \frac{D_2}{D_1} \right)$$

Where:

  • $SPL_2$ - final pressure at target distance;
  • $SPL_1$ - initial pressure (usually at 1 meter);
  • $D_1$ - base distance;
  • $D_2$ - target distance to the listener.

Accounting for speaker power and sensitivity

If you are designing a system, it is important to consider the rated sensitivity of the speakers. It is measured when a signal power is applied 1 Watt at a distance 1 meter. With increasing power and number of emitters, SPL increases:

  1. Gain from power: Each doubling of Watts adds 3 dB to volume. Formula: $10 \cdot \log_{10}(\text{Мощность})$ .
  2. Number of columns: Using two identical columns instead of one (assuming they are consistent) adds even more 3 dB.
  3. Acoustic gain (Boundary Gain): Placing the speaker against a wall or in a corner concentrates sound energy, increasing system efficiency without wasting electrical power.

The influence of the environment on the result

When calculating indoors, it is important to understand that real indicators may differ from theoretical ones due to reverberation (echo).

  • Free field (0 dB): The sound goes into space without returning. Ideal for open areas.
  • Half-space (+3 dB): The speaker stands on a hard floor. The reflection from the floor is added to the main signal.
  • Quarter Space (+6 dB): Column against the wall. Energy is directed to a narrower sector.
  • Eighth of space (+9 dB): Installation in a corner (floor + two walls). A maximum increase in volume, often accompanied by increased bass.
