Payback period calculator (PP and DPP): Calculation of the return on investment period

Payback Period is a fundamental indicator in investment analysis that answers the main business question: “How long will it take to return the invested funds?” Our tool allows you to instantly calculate the break-even point, taking into account both nominal cash flows and the real time value of money.

The calculator determines two critical indicators: PP (Simple Payback Period) for quick assessment and DPP (Discounted Payback Period) for in-depth financial analysis.

Why count two types of payback?

When planning a business project, it is important to distinguish between these metrics:

  • Simple term (PP): Shows how quickly the money will be returned in “absolute” numbers. This method is good for assessing liquidity in a stable economy.
  • Discounted Term (DPP): Takes into account that a dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow. Using the discount rate (WACC or inflation rate) you see the real return on investment, taking into account risks and depreciation of money.

Calculation formulas

The calculator is based on classic financial management formulas.

1. Simple payback period (PP)

If the annual cash flow is the same, the formula is simple:

$$PP = \frac{I_0}{CF}$$

Where:

  • $I_0$ - the amount of the initial investment;
  • $CF$ - annual net cash flow.

If cash flows vary from year to year, the cumulative calculation method is used:

$$PP = A + \frac{B}{C}$$

Where:

  • $A$ is the last period (year) when the accumulated flow was still negative;
  • $B$ - uncovered investment balance at the end of the year $A$ ;
  • $C$ is the cash flow in the year following the year $A$ .

2. Discounted payback period (DPP)

This method reduces all future receipts to the current value (PV - Present Value). Discount formula for each flow:

$$PV = \frac{CF_t}{(1 + r)^t}$$

Where:

  • $CF_t$ - cash flow for the period $t$ ;
  • $r$ — discount rate (in decimal form, for example 0.1 for 10%);
  • $t$ - year number.

The final maturity of the DPP occurs when the sum of the discounted flows ( $\sum PV$ ) becomes equal to the investment amount.

Example of project efficiency calculation

Consider investing in opening a pizzeria.

  • Investments: $100,000
  • Discount rate: 10%

Analysis of results:

  1. According to the simple method (PP): The project will pay off in the middle of the 3rd year ( $A=2$ , $B=30000$ , $C=50000$ ). $$PP = 2 + \frac{30000}{50000} = 2.6 \text{ года}$$
  2. According to the discounted method (DPP): Due to the depreciation of money, the project will break even at the beginning of the 4th year. $$DPP = 3 + \frac{2103}{27320} \approx 3.08 \text{ года}$$

How to choose a discount rate?

To correctly calculate DPP, it is recommended to enter in the “Discount rate” field:

  • For personal finance: bank deposit rate or real inflation rate (for example, 5-10%).
  • For business: weighted average cost of capital (WACC) or the desired rate of return that compensates for the risks of the project (usually 10-20%).
