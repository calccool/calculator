When planning a server storage or NAS system, beginners often make two critical mistakes: they do not take into account redundancy losses (parity/mirroring) and they forget about the difference between the manufacturers' decimal terabytes and the operating systems' binary terabytes.

Our calculator allows you to instantly calculate how much real space will be available for your files and what level of protection the selected configuration will provide.

Basic functions of the calculator

  • Popular levels supported: RAID 0, 1, 5, 6 and 10.
  • Binary conversion (Base 2): Automatic translation from marketing TBs to real TiBs that you will see on Windows or Linux.
  • Data visualization: A visual pie chart of the distribution of space between data and protection.
  • Fault tolerance rating: Accurate calculation of the number of disks that can fail without losing information.

Features of each array type

RAID 0 (Stripe)

Combines disks into one logical volume for maximum speed. Data is divided into blocks and written in parallel.

  • Plus: 100% capacity utilization.
  • Minus: Zero reliability.

RAID 1 (Mirror)

Complete duplication of data from one disk to another.

  • Plus: Highest reliability.
  • Minus: The usable capacity is equal to the volume of just one disk, no matter how many there are in the mirror.

RAID 5 (Parity)

The most popular standard for servers. Uses block interleaving with checksums (parity).

  • Efficiency: The volume of exactly one disk for the entire array is lost.
  • Balance: Good reading speed and average reliability.

RAID 6 (Double Parity)

An evolution of RAID 5, but with dual parity distribution.

  • Reliability: The array survives even if any two disks fail at the same time.
  • Capacity: The volume of two disks is subtracted.

RAID 10 (1+0)

A combination of mirroring and striping. This is an "array of arrays" requiring an even number of drives.

  • Performance: Highest write speed among fault-tolerant systems.

The problem of “disappearing” terabytes: GB vs GiB

Users often wonder why, after assembling an array of four 1 TB disks, the system shows not 4 TB (in RAID 0), but approximately 3.63 TiB. This is not a hardware error, but a difference in number systems.

Manufacturers use the decimal system ( $Base 10$ ):

$$1 \text{ TB} = 10^{12} \text{ байт}$$

Operating systems use the binary system ( $Base 2$ ):

$$1 \text{ TiB} = 2^{40} \text{ байт} = 1,099,511,627,776 \text{ байт}$$

Our calculator takes into account the conversion factor to obtain the most accurate result:

  • For TB → TiB the coefficient is $\approx 0.9095$
  • For GB → GiB the coefficient is $\approx 0.9313$

Use the "Consider actual capacity" switch to get data that will match the metrics in your server's Disk Management console.
