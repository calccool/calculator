Heated floor calculator: Calculation of cable power and laying pitch

Correct calculation of an electric heated floor is a matter of not only comfort, but also fire safety. An error in calculations can lead to two extremes: either the floor will remain cold (a “zebra” of warm and cold stripes), or the cable will burn out from local overheating under the furniture.

Our engineering calculator will help you design a heating system by selecting the optimal power and spacing of the heating cable or mats.

Calculation principle: Usable area and “Dead zones”

The main rule for installing electric floors is never lay cables under stationary furniture (wardrobes, kitchen units, sofas without legs). Unlike water floors, the electric cable is not self-regulating (with the exception of expensive self-regulating models). If you cover its heat sink with furniture, it will overheat and fail.

Therefore, the calculation always begins with the calculation useful heating area ( $S_{heat}$ ):

$$S_{heat} = S_{total} — S_{furniture}$$

Where:

  • $S_{total}$ - Total area of ​​the room.
  • $S_{furniture}$ — The area occupied by furniture and indentations from the walls (usually 5–10 cm).

Formulas for calculating cable sections

If you use heating mats, everything is simple - you buy a mat with an area equal to the usable area. But if you mount cable tie (which is cheaper and more reliable), you need to calculate the length of the section and the pitch of the mounting tape.

1. Calculation of total power

$$P_{total} = S_{heat} \times P_{spec}$$

Where $P_{spec}$ is the selected power density (for example, 150 W/m²).

2. Calculation of cable length

$$L_{cable} = \frac{P_{total}}{P_{cable}}$$

Where $P_{cable}$ is the linear power of the cable itself (usually 15–20 W/m).

3. Calculation of laying step (h)

This is the distance between the turns of the cable when laying in a “snake” pattern. The uniformity of heating depends on this parameter.

$$h (см) = \frac{S_{heat} \times 100}{L_{cable}}$$

Checking the load on the thermostat

Standard household thermostats are designed for maximum current 16 Amps (approximately 3.5 kW of power).

Our calculator automatically calculates the current using Ohm's law:

$$I = \frac{P_{total}}{220}$$

If the rated current exceeds 16A (or even approaches this value, for example >12A for safety margin), the system must be connected through magnetic starter (contactor) or split into two independent heating zones with different thermostats.
