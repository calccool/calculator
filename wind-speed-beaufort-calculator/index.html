Wind force calculator in knots and points on the Beaufort scale

The Beaufort scale is an international twelve-point system adopted by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) for visually assessing wind strength. Unlike anemometers, which provide precise numbers, the Beaufort scale allows you to determine wind speed by its effect on land objects or the state of the water surface.

Our calculator performs the opposite task: you enter the exact speed value in units convenient for you ( m/s, knots, km/h or mph ), and the algorithm instantly determines the corresponding score, the name of the wind and its destructive power.

Mathematical formula for calculation

The relationship between wind speed at 10 meters above the ground and the Beaufort Index is described by an empirical formula proposed in 1946:

$$v = 0.836 \cdot B^{3/2}$$

Where:

  • $v$ — wind speed in meters per second (m/s);
  • $B$ - wind strength in Beaufort scale.

To calculate a score based on a known speed in m/s, our calculator uses the converted formula:

$$B = \left(\frac{v}{0.836}\right)^{2/3}$$

How to use the wind force calculator

To get the most accurate result, follow these simple instructions:

Step 1: Selecting Units of Measurement

Select the system in which you received the data from the drop-down list. Available:

  • Meters per second (m/s) - standard for CIS weather services.
  • Knots - the main standard in shipping and aviation.
  • Kilometers per hour (km/h) - a familiar format for motorists.
  • Miles per hour (mph) - used in the USA and UK.

Step 2: Data entry and sea analysis

Enter a numeric value in the Wind Speed ​​field. If you are planning a sea voyage, activate the switch "Sea State Analysis". This will add to the results the calculation of estimated wave heights, which is critical for the safety of small vessels.

Step 3: Interpretation of results

Click the "Calculate" button. The calculator will not only show dry numbers, but thanks to the built-in visualizer, it will change the color of the interface and the speed of the “flow” animation so that you can clearly feel the dynamics of the wind.

Why is this important for security?

Understanding the Beaufort scale is necessary:

  1. For sailors and yachtsmen: To make a decision about going to sea (storm warning usually starts with 6-7 points).
  2. For builders: To assess the admissibility of working at height and with tower cranes.
  3. For tourists: When planning hikes in the mountains or wooded areas.
  4. For drone operators: Wind speeds above Beaufort 4-5 can be critical for most consumer quadcopters.
