Calculator for converting fractions of a year into months, days and hours online

In everyday life, we rarely say “I will finish the project in 1.42 years.” However, in bank statements, seniority calculations or depreciation schedules, such values ​​are the norm. Our calculator performs a mathematically precise decomposition of fractional numbers, turning the abstract “0.7 year” into understandable calendar segments.

The tool works in two directions:

  • From shares to exact time: Instantly breaks a decimal number into years, months, days and even minutes.
  • From time to fractions: helps you find out what part of a year there are, for example, 4 months and 12 days.

Algorithm and calculation formulas

To ensure the unification of calculations, the calculator uses the international financial standard (Basis 30/360), where the following constants are taken as a basis:

  • 1 year = 365 days
  • 1 month = 30 days
  • 1 week = 7 days

The conversion is carried out step by step. Let's look at the formula using the $F$ value (share of year) as an example:

  1. Selecting whole years: $$Y = \lfloor F \rfloor$$
  2. Calculation of balance in days: $$D_{total} = (F — Y) \times 365$$
  3. Selecting full months: $$M = \lfloor D_{total} / 30 \rfloor$$
  4. Determination of remaining days: $$D_{rem} = D_{total} \pmod{30}$$

If "Advanced Mode" is activated, the calculator calculates the rest of the day ( $D_{frac}$ ) down to the minutes using the $1 \text{ день} = 24 \text{ часа}$ ratio.

When might this be needed?

  1. Jurisprudence: Calculation of limitation periods or periods of ownership of property.
  2. HR and accounting: Determining the exact length of service for part-time employment.
  3. Education: Solving problems in physics or astronomy where time is often represented in decimal form.
  4. Construction: Planning stages of long-term projects.

Important Note: The calculator uses a standard “banking” month of 30 days. This is a generally accepted constant that avoids confusion with different numbers of days in months (28, 30 or 31) in mathematical modeling.
