Oven temperature calculator: Celsius, Fahrenheit and Gas Mark

Temperature conversion formulas

If you need to perform the calculation manually, classical thermodynamic formulas are used.

Convert from Celsius to Fahrenheit

To find the temperature in Fahrenheit ( $T_{F}$ ), knowing the value in Celsius ( $T_{C}$ ), use the formula:

$$T_{F} = T_{C} \cdot 1.8 + 32$$

Convert from Fahrenheit to Celsius

Recalculation is performed as follows:

$$T_{C} = \frac{T_{F} — 32}{1.8}$$

Convection: why do you need to reduce the temperature?

Convection mode (fan) provides more intense circulation of hot air. This speeds up the heat exchange process, which causes the dish to cook faster and may burn on the outside while remaining raw on the inside.

The golden rule of cooking: if you turn on convection, reduce the temperature by 20 °C (or on 25 °F ) relative to that indicated for the “top-bottom heat” mode. Our calculator does this automatically, highlighting the value for convection in a special color.

Recommendations for choosing temperatures for different dishes

Choosing the right heat is 50% of success in cooking. Here are the main guidelines:

Low temperatures (110–140 °C)

Ideal for “long” processes: preparing delicate meringues, drying fruit or slow simmering tough meat. With this mode, the moisture evaporates gradually, maintaining the texture of the product.

Average temperatures (150–190 °C)

Most popular range. 180°C is standard for most muffins, biscuits and casseroles. It is at this temperature that the Maillard reaction actively occurs (formation of a golden brown crust and aroma).

High temperatures (200–250 °C)

Used for quick frying, baking al dente vegetables and making homemade bread or pizza. High heat instantly “seals” the juices inside the product.

Important tip: Always preheat the oven at least 15-20 minutes before cooking. Even if the thermometer shows the desired number, the walls of the chamber must warm up completely for stable heat distribution.
