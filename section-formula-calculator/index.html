Online calculator for dividing a segment in a given ratio: Internal and External division

This tool is designed to quickly solve one of the classic problems of analytical geometry - finding the coordinates of a point that divides a segment connecting two given points in a certain proportion. Whether you're solving a school problem or performing an engineering calculation, our calculator will provide accuracy and clarity.

How does the calculator work?

The problem of dividing a segment in this regard is formulated as follows: two points $A(x_1, y_1)$ and $B(x_2, y_2)$ are given on the plane. It is necessary to find the coordinates of the third point $P(x, y)$ , which lies on the line passing through $A$ and $B$ , and divides the segment $AB$ so that the ratio of the lengths of $AP$ to $PB$ is equal to the given number $\lambda$ (or the ratio $m/n$ ).

Our algorithm takes into account two possible scenarios:

  1. Internal division: Point $P$ lies between points $A$ and $B$ .
  2. External division: Point $P$ lies on the continuation of the straight line, outside segment $AB$ .

Basic formulas

The following mathematical formulas are used to calculate the coordinates of point $P(x, y)$ . Let the relation be $AP : PB = m : n$ . Then the division coefficient is $\lambda = \frac{m}{n}$ .

For internal division:

The coordinates of the point $P$ are found as the weighted average of the coordinates of the ends of the segment:

$$x = \frac{m x_2 + n x_1}{m + n}, \quad y = \frac{m y_2 + n y_1}{m + n}$$

For outer division:

If point $P$ divides the segment externally, the formulas change sign (this is equivalent to using a negative ratio):

$$x = \frac{m x_2 — n x_1}{m — n}, \quad y = \frac{m y_2 — n y_1}{m — n}$$

Important: With external division, the ratio $m$ cannot be equal to $n$ , since this would result in division by zero (parallel lines never intersect).

Practical Application

Dividing a segment in a given ratio is often used in:

  • Cartography and navigation: To interpolate coordinates between two waypoints.
  • Computer graphics: In algorithms for rasterizing lines and constructing Bezier curves.
  • Physics: To find the center of mass of a system of material points.
  • Construction: When marking foundations and load-bearing structures according to drawings.
