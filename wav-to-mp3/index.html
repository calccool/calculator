Comparison of audio formats: why choose MP3?

Before converting, it's important to understand what you're giving up and what you're gaining when moving from Waveform to MPEG-1 Audio Layer III.

Mathematical model of conversion and compression

The LameJS algorithm underlying the encoder removes those frequencies that the human ear is not able to perceive against the background of louder sounds (auditory masking effect). This allows you to radically reduce the weight of the file.

The size of an uncompressed WAV file directly depends on the mathematical recording parameters and is calculated using the following formula:

$$S_{wav} = R_{sample} \times B_{depth} \times C \times T$$

Where $S_{wav}$ is the final size in bytes, $R_{sample}$ is the sampling frequency (for example, $44100$ Hz), $B_{depth}$ is the bit depth in bytes (for 16-bit this is $2$ bytes), $C$ is the number of channels (1 for mono, 2 for stereo), and $T$ is the duration of the audio track in seconds.

After converting to MP3, the file size no longer depends on the sampling frequency and sound depth. It is determined solely by the selected bitrate (data stream):

$$S_{mp3} = \frac{B_{rate} \times 1000}{8} \times T$$

Where $B_{rate}$ is the bitrate you set in kilobits per second (kbps). By choosing the $192$ kbps value, you will reduce the size of the original file by almost 7 times without noticeable distortion.

When the Loudness Normalization option is activated, each audio sample is multiplied by the calculated gain to achieve the target peak value:

$$V_{norm} = V_{orig} \times \frac{10^{-1/20}}{|V_{peak}|}$$

Here $V_{peak}$ is the maximum amplitude in the original signal. This step ensures that quiet voice recordings, once exported, will sound clear and loud on any device.
