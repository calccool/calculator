Pete Riegel's Formula: The Gold Standard for Forecasting

The calculations are based on an empirical formula developed by American engineer and running researcher Pete Riegel in 1977. It takes into account the athlete’s natural deceleration as the distance increases due to the endurance coefficient.

Mathematically, the relationship looks like this:

$$T_2 = T_1 \times \left(\frac{D_2}{D_1}\right)^{1.06}$$

Where variables mean:

  • $T_1$ — The time during which you have already run the control distance.
  • $D_1$ — Control distance (for example, 21.0975 km).
  • $D_2$ — The distance for which the forecast is being made (Marathon — 42.195 km).
  • $1.06$ — Speed ​​degradation coefficient applicable to most trained runners.

Key Factors in Forecast Accuracy

It is worth remembering that dry mathematics is only the foundation. In order for your actual result to coincide with the calculated one, you need to take into account the following variables:

  • Aerobic base: The Riegel formula only works ideally if you have done a sufficient amount of training (long runs of 25-32 km).
  • Route terrain: If the half marathon was flat and the marathon is hilly (like Boston), add 3-5% to the forecast.
  • Weather conditions: Temperatures above +15°C significantly increase the load on the cardiovascular system, reducing the rate.
  • Nutrition strategy: Lack of glycogen after the 30th kilometer is the main reason for the drop in speed, which the formula cannot predict.

How to use the calculator

  1. Select mode: Use the “Half Marathon” tab for a standard calculation, or “Any Distance” if you have a recent 5K or 10K result.
  2. Enter time: Enter the hours, minutes and seconds of your best recent run.
  3. Explore splits: Enable the “Calculate checkpoints” option to get time stamps for distances 5, 10, 21.1 and the critical point of 30 km.
  4. Follow the tempo scale: The interactive ring visualizes the intensity of your run, helping you navigate your speed zones.

Use this data to set your watch at the start and remember: the marathon begins after the 30th kilometer. Proper calculation is your insurance against premature finishing!
