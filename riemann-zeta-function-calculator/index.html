Riemann Zeta Function Calculator: Calculating Values ​​in the Complex Plane

The Riemann zeta function is one of the most mysterious and important objects in modern mathematics. It connects the distribution of prime numbers with the laws of complex analysis and underlies the famous Riemann hypotheses - one of the seven Millennium Challenges, for which a $1 million reward has been awarded.

Our interactive tool allows you to calculate $\zeta(s)$ values ​​not only for real numbers, but also for the entire complex plane, where $s = \sigma + it$ .

Definition and mathematical meaning

In the classical definition for the region where the real part of the argument is $\sigma > 1$ , the function is specified as a convergent Dirichlet series:

$$\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s} = \frac{1}{1^s} + \frac{1}{2^s} + \frac{1}{3^s} + \dots$$

However, our calculator uses analytical continuation algorithms (via the Dirichlet Eta function), which allows us to obtain correct values ​​for the $0 < \sigma < 1$ region (critical band) and even for negative values.

Euler's identity

Leonhard Euler proved the fundamental connection between the zeta function and prime numbers:

$$\zeta(s) = \prod_{p} \frac{1}{1 — p^{-s}}$$

Where the product is taken over all prime numbers $p$ . This identity explains why studying the zeros of the zeta function provides clues to the distribution of prime numbers.

How to use the calculator

To get the result, you must specify the complex argument $s$ :

  1. Real part ( $\sigma$ ): Horizontal coordinate Re. Determines the convergence of series.
  2. Imaginary part ( $t$ ): Vertical coordinate Im. Responsible for the oscillatory nature of the function.

The tool visualizes the position of the entered point relative to Critical line ( $Re = 1/2$ ), where, according to the Riemann hypothesis, all non-trivial zeros of the function lie.

The most interesting area to explore is the band where $0 < Re(s) < 1$ . This is where the “non-trivial zeros” of the function are located, that is, the points where $\zeta(s) = 0$ .
