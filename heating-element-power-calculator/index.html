Tubular heating element power calculator: Calculation of specific load and maximum power

The correct calculation of a tubular electric heater (TEH) is a balance between the required thermal energy and the physical survivability of the element. The main mistake when choosing or manufacturing a heating element is exceeding the permissible surface load. If you apply too much power to a short tube, it will overheat and burn out in a matter of minutes.

Our heating element power calculator allows you to determine the safe power limit for a heater of a given size in a specific environment (water, air, oil).

Calculation theory: Active area and Specific load

The power of any heater does not depend on the electrical resistance of the spiral (it can be wound in any way), but on the ability of the shell to remove heat. This parameter is called specific surface load ($q$).

The calculation algorithm is constructed in two stages:

  1. Calculation of the tube development area ( $S$ ): We find the surface area of ​​the cylinder (heating element tube) through which heat exchange occurs. $$S = \pi \cdot D \cdot L$$ Where $D$ is the diameter of the tube, $L$ is the active length of the heating element.
  2. Calculation of maximum power ( $P$ ): Knowing the area, we multiply it by the permissible load for a particular environment. $$P = S \cdot q_{доп}$$

How to use the calculator

To design a reliable heater, follow these steps:

  1. Measure the length: Enter the active tube length in centimeters. Important: do not take into account the “cold zones” (ends of the tube with contact pins) where there is no helix.
  2. Specify diameter: Enter the outer diameter of the tube in millimeters (standard values: 8, 10, 13 mm).
  3. Select environment: This is a critical step. A heating element designed for water (11 W/cm²), when turned on in air, will instantly become red-hot and burn out, since air only allows a load of 2.5 W/cm².
  4. Get the result: The calculator will show the maximum electrical power (in kW) that a heating element of such dimensions can withstand without destruction.

Why is this important?

Ignoring the specific load calculation leads to two problems:

  • Excess power: Shell rupture, current breakdown to the housing, spiral burnout.
  • Power reduction: The heating element will be “eternal”, but heating the volume will take an unreasonably long time.
