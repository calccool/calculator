Basic Formula (Euclidean Distance)

In most cases, “distance” refers to the length of the shortest path between points—a straight line. In mathematics this is called Euclidean distance. It is derived from the Pythagorean theorem, applied twice (for the plane and then for space).

The calculation formula is as follows:

$$d = \sqrt{(x_2 — x_1)^2 + (y_2 — y_1)^2 + (z_2 — z_1)^2}$$

Where:

  • $d$ — the required distance (length of the segment $AB$ );
  • $\Delta x = x_2 — x_1$ — coordinate difference along the X axis;
  • $\Delta y = y_2 — y_1$ — coordinate difference along the Y axis;
  • $\Delta z = z_2 — z_1$ - difference in coordinates along the Z axis.

Example calculation

Consider the problem of finding the distance between points $A(1, 2, 3)$ and $B(4, 6, 8)$ .

1. Find the coordinate differences (vector projections):

  • $\Delta x = 4 — 1 = 3$
  • $\Delta y = 6 — 2 = 4$
  • $\Delta z = 8 — 3 = 5$

2. Calculation of Euclidean distance:

$$d = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16 + 25} = \sqrt{50} \approx 7.071$$

3. Calculation of Manhattan distance:

$$d_{man} = |3| + |4| + |5| = 12$$

4. Calculation of Chebyshev distance:

$$d_{cheb} = \max(3, 4, 5) = 5$$

Practical Application

Understanding distance in 3D space is critical in the following areas:

  • Game development (GameDev): To determine collisions (collisions of objects), calculate the distance to the target or the radius of destruction.
  • Robotics: To calculate the trajectory of the manipulator in space.
  • Construction and architecture: To check the diagonals of rooms and structures.
  • Data analysis: Clustering data in multidimensional space (for example, the k-nearest neighbors method).

Coordinates of the midpoint of the segment

Additionally, the calculator finds a point equidistant from the ends of the segment (the middle). Its coordinates $(x_m, y_m, z_m)$ are found as the arithmetic mean of the corresponding coordinates of the ends:

$$x_m = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}; \quad y_m = \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}; \quad z_m = \frac{z_1 + z_2}{2}$$
