Compound interest calculator with replenishment: online capitalization calculation

Compound interest is a financial mechanism in which accrued interest is added to the principal amount of the deposit and begins to generate income in the next period. It was not for nothing that Albert Einstein called this phenomenon the “eighth wonder of the world.” Unlike simple interest, where returns are linear, compound interest creates exponential growth in your capital.

Our calculator allows you not only to see the final figure, but also to simulate the real financial situation, taking into account monthly contributions, government taxes and currency depreciation due to inflation.

How is compound interest different from simple interest?

When using simple interest, income is accrued only on the original deposit body. With compound interest (capitalization), reinvestment occurs:

  • Simple interest: you withdraw your profits every month. The accrual base is not growing.
  • Compound interest: the profit remains in business. Every month the amount on which interest is calculated becomes a little larger.

Formula for calculating compound interest with replenishment

For those who want to understand the mathematical model of the process, the calculation of the total amount ( $A$ ) for a monthly replenishment is as follows:

$$A = P(1 + \frac{r}{n})^{nt} + PMT \frac{(1 + \frac{r}{n})^{nt} — 1}{\frac{r}{n}}$$

Where:

  • $P$ — initial amount (starting capital);
  • $r$ — annual interest rate (in decimal form);
  • $n$ — number of capitalization periods per year (for monthly — 12);
  • $t$ — investment period in years;
  • $PMT$ - monthly replenishment amount.

Why is it important to consider inflation and taxes?

A figure with six zeros at the end of the calculation looks attractive, but a professional investor always looks at the “net” yield.

Tax burden (personal income tax)

In most countries, investment income is taxed. In Russia, for example, the standard rate is 13% or 15%. The calculator automatically subtracts this percentage from the profit earned so that you can see the real amount that will remain in your hands.

Inflation and purchasing power

Inflation is the “invisible thief” that depreciates money. If inflation is 7% per year, then in 10 years a million rubles will buy significantly fewer goods than today. Our tool calculates the real purchasing power of your future capital in today's prices.

5 tips for maximum results

  1. Start as early as possible. Time is the main factor in the formula. The earlier capitalization is launched, the more aggressively the chart grows.
  2. Discipline is more important than sum. Even a small but regular replenishment (for example, 3,000 - 5,000 rubles) after 15-20 years can turn into serious capital due to the accumulation effect.
  3. Take advantage of tax breaks. In the Russian Federation, the use of an IIS (Individual Investment Account) allows you to minimize taxes or receive a deduction, which increases the effective rate.
  4. Keep an eye on the actual rate. Your goal is for returns to exceed inflation. If the deposit is at 8% and inflation is 9%, you are actually losing money.
  5. Reinvest everything. Do not take interest “for life” at the stage of capital formation. Let them work for you.
