What is blue hour and why is it important?

Blue Hour is a period of twilight when the Sun is far enough below the horizon and the sky takes on a rich, deep blue hue. At this time, the overall illumination drops, but the sky does not turn black, which allows you to create the perfect balance between natural light and artificial city lights.

Key benefits of blue hour:

  • Color Contrast: The deep blue sky pairs beautifully with the warm yellow and orange lights of the buildings and lanterns.
  • Softness: The absence of direct sunlight eliminates harsh shadows.
  • Atmosphere: The photographs take on a mystical, calm or futuristic mood.

Mathematical calculation model

The calculation of the “mode light” time is based on determining the height of the Sun above the horizon. The calculation uses the equation of time and the declination of the star.

The basic formula for determining the hour angle ( $w$ ) at which the Sun reaches a given altitude ( $alt$ ):

$$\cos(w) = \frac{\sin(alt) — \sin(lat) \cdot \sin(dec)}{\cos(lat) \cdot \cos(dec)}$$

Where:

  • $alt$ - the height of the Sun (for example, $-6^\circ$ for the beginning of the blue hour);
  • $lat$ - latitude of the area;
  • $dec$ is the declination of the Sun, depending on the day of the year.

Tips for shooting during blue hour

To obtain technically high-quality personnel during this period, we recommend adhering to the following rules:

  • Use a tripod: Light output during the blue hour is limited, which will require long shutter speeds (1 to 30 seconds).
  • Shoot in RAW: This will give you maximum flexibility when adjusting white balance and pulling out shadow detail.
  • Keep track of time: Blue "hour" actually lasts between 20 and 40 minutes depending on your latitude and time of year. The closer to the equator, the faster twilight passes.
  • Aperture for "stars": Stop down to $f/8 — f/16$ to transform point sources of city light into beautiful, radiant stars.
