Hyperfocal distance calculator: calculating depth of field for ideal landscapes

For a landscape photographer, there is nothing more frustrating than coming home and discovering that the beautiful flowers in the foreground are harsh and the mountains in the background are soapy, or vice versa. Hyperfocal distance is a focal point that solves this problem.

If you focus the lens at the hyperfocal distance, then the zone of sharpness (DOF) will extend from half this distance to infinity. This allows you to achieve maximum detail across the entire frame, which is critical for panoramic and landscape photography.

Benefits of using hyperfocal

  • Maximum depth of field: You use the capabilities of your optics 100%.
  • Confidence in the result: You don't have to guess by eye whether a closed aperture is enough.
  • Time saving: One precise metering replaces a series of frames for focus stacking in simple conditions.

Formulas and mathematics of sharpness

The calculation is based on optical laws and the concept of “Circle of Confusion”. The smaller the matrix, the smaller the circle of dispersion must be for the image to appear clear to us.

The basic formula for the $H$ hyperfocal distance is as follows:

$$H = \frac{f^2}{N \cdot c} + f$$

Where:

  • $f$ — lens focal length (in mm);
  • $N$ - aperture value (f-number);
  • $c$ - circle limit (depending on the sensor).

Knowing $H$ , you can easily calculate the near limit of sharpness $D_n$ for any focusing distance $s$ :

$$D_n = \frac{s \cdot (H — f)}{H + s — 2f}$$

Practical tips for sharp photos

To ensure your photos are technically flawless, follow these guidelines:

  • Don't close your aperture all the way down. At values ​​ $f/16$ or $f/22$ , a diffraction effect occurs, which makes the picture less clear. It is optimal to use $f/8$ or $f/11$ .
  • Focus a little further. If you make a mistake and focus closer than the hyperfocal distance, infinity will “fly out” of sharpness. It is better to focus 10-20 cm further than the calculated point.
  • Consider the crop factor. The physical focal length written on the lens barrel is always substituted into the formula. Our calculator will automatically adjust the calculations based on the selected matrix.
  • Use a tripod. Hyperfocal calculations often require closed apertures, which leads to long shutter speeds.
