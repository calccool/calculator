Calculator of hydraulic resistance and pressure loss in pipes (Darcy-Weisbach Formula)

Theory for calculating hydraulic losses

When designing water supply, heating or industrial piping systems, it is critical to determine exactly how much pressure will be lost due to fluid friction against the pipe walls. Our calculator uses Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is the gold standard in modern hydraulics due to its versatility and precision.

Basic formulas

Pressure loss ( $\Delta P$ ) is calculated as follows:

$$\Delta P = \lambda \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho \cdot V^2}{2}$$

Where:

  • $\lambda$ (Lambda) — coefficient of hydraulic friction (dimensionless).
  • $L$ — length of the pipe section (m).
  • $D$ — internal diameter of the pipe (m).
  • $\rho$ (Po) — liquid density (kg/m³).
  • $V$ — average flow speed (m/s).

Flow Regimes and Reynolds Number

The key factor affecting resistance is the fluid flow pattern. It is determined Reynolds number ( $Re$ ):

$$Re = \frac{V \cdot D}{\nu}$$

where $\nu$ is the kinematic viscosity of the liquid.

Our algorithm automatically determines the mode and selects the desired formula for the friction coefficient ( $\lambda$ ):

  1. Laminar mode ( $Re < 2300$ ): The fluid movement is calm, layered. Poiseuille formula used: $$\lambda = \frac{64}{Re}$$
  2. Transient and Turbulent Mode ( $Re > 2300$ ): Vortexes arise and resistance increases sharply. We use universal Altschul formula, which takes into account both wall roughness and Reynolds number: $$\lambda = 0.11 \cdot \left( \frac{\Delta}{D} + \frac{68}{Re} \right)^{0.25}$$

Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

Why does the internal diameter need to be specified?

Hydraulic calculations are carried out using the “live cross-section” of the flow. For example, a 32 mm polypropylene pipe may have a wall thickness of 4.4 mm, which means the internal diameter is only 23.2 mm. An error in diameter gives a colossal error, since losses depend on the diameter to the fifth power!

What is the difference between Pascals and meters of water?

These are units of pressure. $1 \text{ бар} \approx 100\,000 \text{ Па} \approx 10.19 \text{ метров вод. ст.}$ Our calculator displays both values ​​for convenience.

How does the Reynolds number affect the calculation?

At low $Re$ (laminar flow), wall roughness has almost no effect on losses. However, with large $Re$ (turbulent flow), any defect on the inner surface of the pipe creates vortices, sharply increasing energy losses.
