Gestational age calculator based on CTR (coccygeal-parietal size)

The coccygeal-parietal size (CPR) is a key indicator measured during an ultrasound examination (ultrasound) in the first trimester of pregnancy. It represents the distance from the highest point of the head (crown) to the lowest point of the body (tailbone) of the embryo when it is in a naturally relaxed state. In obstetric practice, CTE is considered the “gold standard” for determining the exact gestational age, since in the early stages (from 6 to 14 weeks) all embryos grow at almost the same rate, in contrast to the second and third trimesters, where genetic factors and nutritional characteristics come into play. Using our calculator allows you to double-check the obstetric period calculated based on the date of the last menstruation and adjust the estimated date of birth (EDD) with an accuracy of 3-5 days.

How the calculation works and main features

Our algorithm is based on clinically proven fetometric data (in particular, the modified Robinson & Fleming formula). The tool works in two directions, covering any questions of future parents:

  • Determination of the term according to the KTR: You enter the value in millimeters from the ultrasound protocol, and the system calculates your exact due date in weeks and days, and also calculates the date of your upcoming birth.
  • Determination of the norm by term: If you know your due date, the calculator will show what the size of the fetus should be at this stage (a range of values ​​from the 5th to the 95th percentile).
  • Development visualization: To make the dry numbers clearer, we added a visual comparison of the size of the baby with fruits and berries (from “pea” to “orange”).
  • Weight rating: Based on the term, an approximate calculation of the weight of the fetus is made.

Formula and measurement accuracy

The calculations are based on the dependence of embryo growth on time. For those interested in the mathematical model, the basic formula for determining gestational age (GA) in days is as follows:

$$GA_{(days)} = 8.052 \times \sqrt{CRL} + 23.73$$

Where:

  • $GA$ - Gestational Age (Gestational age in days).
  • $CRL$ - Crown-Rump Length (CTR in millimeters).

It is important to note that this method is only relevant until the 14th week of pregnancy. After this period, the fetus becomes more mobile, begins to actively unbend, and measuring CTE accurately becomes technically difficult. From this point on, doctors move on to measuring the biparietal head size (BPD) and abdominal circumference.

Frequently asked questions

Why is the term for KTR different from the period for monthly?

This is a normal situation. The obstetric term counts pregnancy from the first day of the last menstruation, assuming that ovulation occurred on the 14th day. If you ovulate late or have an irregular cycle, the embryo may be “younger” than expected. In this case, doctors often change the PDR, focusing specifically on the data of the first ultrasound and CTE.

What to do if CTE lags behind the norm?

A single measurement is not a diagnosis. An error in the ultrasound machine, the position of the fetus (“curled up”), or inaccurate data entry can distort the result. The doctor usually prescribes a repeat study after 7-10 days to assess growth dynamics. The most important thing is the presence of a heartbeat and an increase in size over time.
