What is Compound Interest?

Albert Einstein called compound interest “the eighth wonder of the world.” Its essence is simple: you receive income not only on your initial investment, but also on the interest accumulated earlier. This creates a snowball effect where your capital grows exponentially.

The basic compound interest formula used in financial calculations is:

$$A = P \left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}$$

Where:

  • A - total amount.
  • P — initial amount (deposit body).
  • r — annual interest rate (in decimal form).
  • n — the number of capitalization periods per year (for example, 12 for monthly).
  • t — deposit term in years.

Our calculator uses a more complex iterative model to correctly account for regular additions over different time periods.

Tool capabilities

To get the most accurate forecast, fill out the following fields:

  1. Starting capital: The amount you have now.
  2. Regular replenishment: Money you are willing to save monthly or annually. Even small amounts over the long term turn into millions.
  3. Capitalization frequency: Choose how often the bank calculates interest (monthly is the most profitable option for the depositor).
  4. Inflation Accounting: A unique feature of our calculator. She shows real cost your money in the future. For example, 1 million in 10 years with inflation of 5% will allow you to buy goods worth only ~600 thousand at today’s prices.

Why consider the effective rate (EIR)?

In the calculation results you will see the parameter EIR (Effective Interest Rate). This is the real return on your investment. If the bank offers 12% per annum with monthly capitalization, then in reality you will earn not 12% per annum, but 12.68%, since the interest accrued in the first months will itself begin to generate profits by the end of the year.
