Calculator for calculating the volume of an expansion tank for heating systems

In closed heating systems, water or antifreeze increases in volume when heated. Since the liquid is practically incompressible, without a special compensating device, the pressure in the pipes can rise to critical values, which will lead to rupture of connections or activation of the emergency valve. This problem is solved membrane expansion tank (expanzomat).

Our calculator allows you to perform an accurate engineering calculation of the required tank volume, taking into account not only the displacement of the system, but also the physical properties of the coolant.

Why can’t you take the tank “by eye”?

There is a popular rule: “Take a tank with a volume of 10% of the entire system.” However, this method often gives an error because it does not take into account:

  1. Liquid type: Antifreezes (ethylene glycol or propylene glycol solutions) expand 10–20% more than water.
  2. Working pressure: The higher the response threshold of the safety valve, the more efficiently the tank operates (the larger the usable volume).
  3. Temperature: Expansion at 40°C and at 85°C are significantly different.

Calculation method and formula

The calculator's operating algorithm is based on the European standard for the design of heating systems. The calculation is carried out in three stages.

1. Determination of expansion factor ( $e$ )

First, it is calculated how much the volume of the liquid will increase when heated.

$$V_{exp} = V_{sys} \times e$$

Where:

  • $V_{sys}$ - total volume of coolant in the system (boiler, radiators, pipes).
  • $e$ - coefficient of thermal expansion (depends on the temperature and composition of the liquid).

2. Tank efficiency calculation ( $D$ )

The membrane tank is never 100% filled with water; part of the volume is always occupied by compressed air. Efficiency (useful volume) is calculated using the formula:

$$D = \frac{P_{max} — P_{0}}{P_{max} + 1}$$

Where:

  • $P_{max}$ - maximum permissible pressure (safety valve setting pressure), bar.
  • $P_{0}$ — pre-inflation pressure of the tank air chamber, bar.

3. Final volume formula ( $V_{tank}$ )

$$V_{tank} = \frac{V_{exp} \times k}{D}$$

Where $k$ is the safety factor (usually 1.1–1.2) so that the system does not operate at its limit.

How to use the calculator?

To get the correct result, fill in the following fields:

  1. Set pressure ( $P_0$ ): This is the air pressure in an empty tank. It should be equal to the static pressure of your system (water column height in meters / 10) + 0.2 bar. Example: System height 5 meters (0.5 bar) + 0.2 = 0.7 bar.
  2. Valve pressure ( $P_{max}$ ): Usually for private houses it is 3 bar. For older systems with cast iron radiators it may be 1.5–2 bar.
  3. System volume: If you do not know the exact displacement, it can be estimated by the boiler power: approximately 15 liters for every 1 kW power.

Installation Tips

  • Installation location: It is recommended to install the expansion tank on the “return” (pipeline that returns cooled water to the boiler), in front of the circulation pump. There the temperature is lower, which extends the service life of the membrane.
  • Orientation: Tanks with a volume of up to 24 liters can be mounted with the pipe up or down. Large tanks are best placed on legs.
  • Service: Once a year it is necessary to check the air pressure in the tank. This needs to be done only with drained coolant (when the water pressure on the membrane is zero).
