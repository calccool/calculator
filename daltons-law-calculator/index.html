Dalton's law calculator: calculating the partial pressure of a gas mixture online

When working with gas mixtures in chemistry, physics, and engineering, it is critical to understand how pressure is distributed between the various components. Our online calculator allows you to instantly calculate the total pressure of a mixture of chemically non-interacting ideal gases, as well as determine the contribution of each individual gas.

The instrument is based on a rule of thumb discovered by John Dalton in 1801. It states that the pressure of a mixture of ideal gases is equal to the sum of their partial pressures at constant temperature and volume.

Mathematical rationale and formulas

For calculations, the calculator uses the basic equations of the molecular kinetic theory of ideal gases. The total pressure of the $P_{total}$ mixture is calculated using the following formula:

$$P_{total} = P_1 + P_2 + \dots + P_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P_i$$

Where $P_i$ is the partial pressure of each component. Partial pressure is the pressure that a gas would exert if it alone occupied the entire available volume of the vessel at the same temperature. Calculating this value for a specific gas is based on the Mendeleev-Clapeyron equation:

$$P_i = \frac{n_i \cdot R \cdot T}{V}$$

For in-depth analysis of the composition of a mixture, our tool additionally calculates the mole fraction of each substance $x_i$ , which shows the ratio of the amount of substance of one gas to the total number of moles in the mixture:

$$x_i = \frac{n_i}{n_{total}}$$

Instructions for using the calculator

The tool's interface is designed to minimize routine conversions of physical quantities. To get an accurate result, follow these steps:

  1. Enter the number of moles: List the amount of substance for each gas separated by commas (for example: 1.5, 2.0, 0.5). The calculator will automatically determine how many gases are in the mixture.
  2. Specify the ambient temperature: Enter a value and select a scale (Kelvins or Celsius). The internal algorithm will perform the conversion to absolute temperature itself.
  3. Set available volume: Enter the tank size in liters or cubic meters.
  4. Analyze the result: In the final table you will see the total pressure of the system in three different units of measurement (atm, kPa, bar), as well as a detailed breakdown of each component of the mixture indicating its mole fraction as a percentage.

Practical application of Dalton's law

Understanding partial pressures is not only necessary in an academic environment. This calculation is critical in the following areas:

  • Design of ventilation and air conditioning systems.
  • Calculation of breathing mixtures for deep sea diving (for example, Nitrox or Trimix), where it is necessary to strictly control the partial pressure of oxygen to avoid toxicity.
  • Medical anesthesiology and respiratory support.
  • Chemical industry in the synthesis of substances in the gas phase.
