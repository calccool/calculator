Cost calculator for charging an electric car from a home outlet

Charging an electric vehicle (EV) at home is the most cost-effective and convenient way to replenish your energy supply. However, to accurately understand costs, it is not enough to simply multiply the battery capacity by the cost per kilowatt. Our professional calculator allows you to calculate real costs, taking into account physical energy losses and the parameters of your electrical network.

How to use the calculator

To get an accurate result, you need to know the basic characteristics of your car and home network parameters:

  1. Battery capacity: Full battery capacity (for example, 40 kWh for a Nissan Leaf or 82 kWh for a Tesla Model 3 Long Range).
  2. Current and desired charge: Indicate how much percent is left in the battery and to what level you plan to charge it (up to 80% is recommended to preserve battery life).
  3. Rate: Price per 1 kWh in your region (can be calculated separately for day and night tariffs).
  4. Charging power: A regular outlet produces about 2.3 kW, a reinforced one - up to 3.6 kW.

Mathematics of the process: how we count

The calculator is based on physical formulas that take into account not only the “filled” energy, but also losses due to current conversion by the car’s inverter and cable heating.

Energy consumption formula

To find out how much energy a car will actually take from the network, use the formula:

$$E_{total} = \frac{C \cdot (SoC_{end} — SoC_{start})}{100 \cdot \eta}$$

Where:

  • $C$ - total battery capacity (kWh);
  • $SoC_{start}$ — initial charge level (%);
  • $SoC_{end}$ — final charge level (%);
  • $\eta$ - coefficient of performance (efficiency), usually ranges from 0.85 to 0.95.

Charging time calculation

The time that the car will spend at the outlet directly depends on the power of your electrical network:

$$T = \frac{E_{total}}{P}$$

Where $P$ is the power of the charger or outlet (kW).

Why is it important to consider efficiency?

The charging process is never 100% efficient. When transferring alternating current (AC) power from a wall outlet to a direct current (DC) battery, losses occur:

  • Heat loss: Heating of the charging cable and internal wiring.
  • Auto system operation: During the charging process, the electric vehicle can start a cooling or heating system for the battery.
  • Inversion: Current conversion in the vehicle's on-board charger.

Our calculator defaults to 90% efficiency, which is the gold standard for modern electric vehicles when charging at home.

Tips for saving on home charging

To get the most out of your electric vehicle, follow these guidelines:

  • Use multi-tariff meters: Charge the car at night (usually from 23:00 to 07:00), when the cost of electricity can be 2-3 times lower.
  • Do not discharge “to zero”: The optimal operating range for lithium-ion batteries is from 20% to 80%. This not only extends the life of the battery, but also allows you to accept a charge faster.
  • Check wiring: For charging power of 3.6 kW and above, make sure that the cable size is suitable for the load to avoid overheating.
  • Install Wallbox: A fixed charging station is safer than a conventional socket and allows for smart charging scheduling features.
