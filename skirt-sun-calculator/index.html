Skirt calculator “sun” and “half-sun”: calculation of pattern and fabric consumption online

Creating a perfectly fitting skirt begins with precise mathematical calculations. Skirts with “sun” and “half-sun” cuts are classics that never go out of fashion due to their elegance and flexibility. However, even experienced craftsmen can make mistakes in calculating radii or underestimate material consumption, especially when working with non-standard fabric widths.

Our online calculator automates complex geometric calculations, allowing you to focus on creativity. The tool instantly builds a drawing in proportions and produces ready-made numbers for application to the fabric.

How to use the calculator

To obtain an accurate drawing, you need to enter only two basic measurements and select the type of cut. The calculator supports two modes: full “sun” (a skirt without seams or with two seams from a whole circle) and “half-sun” (a more discreet version of a semicircle).

Mathematical basis of calculation

The basis for constructing a pattern is the circumference formula. The main task is to find the radius of the notch for the waist ( $R1$ ) and the radius of the bottom cut ( $R2$ ).

Formulas for the sun skirt

For a full circle ( $360^\circ$ ), the waist radius is calculated using the formula:

$$R1 = \frac{Ot}{2 \cdot \pi}$$

Where is $\pi \approx 3,14$ . General radius for cutting the bottom:

$$R2 = R1 + Дю + Пн$$

Formulas for a half-sun skirt

For the half circle ( $180^\circ$ ), the notch needs to be deeper to maintain the length of the waistline:

$$R1 = \frac{Ot}{\pi}$$

Fabric consumption and layout features

One of the most common problems is the lack of fabric when cutting the “sun” into the floor. Our calculator takes into account the width of the fabric and warns about the need for additional seams.

What to look for when choosing fabric:

  • Pile direction: If you are using velvet or fabrics with a directional pattern, the jack layout is prohibited - this will increase consumption.
  • Roll width: If the diameter of the skirt ( $2 \cdot R2$ ) is larger than the width of the fabric (for example, 160 cm with a width of 145 cm), the skirt will have to be cut from several parts.
  • Shrinkage: Before cutting, be sure to decatenate (wet-heat treatment) the fabric, since natural compositions can shrink up to 10%.

Recommendations for allowances

  1. Waist: Usually the allowance is not included in the formula, since the waistline may stretch a little during the sewing process.
  2. Bottom: For large flared skirts, a narrow hem (0.5–1.5 cm) or processing with a rolled seam on an overlocker is recommended so that the edge does not fold too roughly.
  3. At the seams: If the skirt will have seams, add 1.5 cm to each section.

Use the resulting $R1$ and $R2$ values ​​to mark them with soap or chalk from the corner of the fabric folded in four (for a “sun”) or in half (for a “half-sun”).
