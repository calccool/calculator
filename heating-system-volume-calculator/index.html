Heating system volume calculator: accurate calculation of water and antifreeze

Why do you need to know the volume of the heating system?

Accurate calculation of the volume of coolant (water or non-freezing liquid) is a critically important step in the design of engineering systems for a private home. Often this indicator is determined “by eye”, which leads to unnecessary expenses or technical problems.

Our online calculator allows you to instantly calculate the total displacement of the system, taking into account all components: from main pipes to radiator sections and underfloor heating circuits.

Knowing the exact volume is necessary in three cases:

  1. Buying antifreeze. High-quality propylene glycol is expensive. Knowing the exact volume, you will buy exactly as many canisters as you need without overpaying for the extra 10–20 liters.
  2. Selection of expansion tank. This is a major security issue. If the tank is smaller than required (usually 10–15% of the total volume), when heated, the pressure in the system will increase critically, which will lead to the release of liquid through the emergency valve or rupture of connections.
  3. Boiler selection. For electric and gas boilers, it is important to understand the inertia of the system. Too much water requires more energy and time for initial heating.

How the calculation is made: basic formulas

The calculator summarizes the volumes of all nodes of the heating network. The calculation algorithm is based on the geometric data of the pipes and the specifications of the equipment.

The basic calculation formula is as follows:

V_total = V_boiler + V_radiators + V_pipes + V_floor + V_expansion

Where:

  • V_total — Total volume of the system.
  • V_boiler — Boiler capacity (The volume of the boiler heat exchanger is indicated in the passport).
  • V_radiators — Volume of all radiators (Total volume of sections or panels).
  • V_pipes — Volume of pipes (Volume of liquid in supply pipes and lines).
  • V_floor — Underfloor heating volume (The volume of coolant in the underfloor heating pipes).

Reference data: specific volume of components

If you don't have your equipment data sheet handy, our calculator uses average industry standards that ensure high accuracy (no more than 5% error).

1. Volume of water in radiators (per 1 kW of power or 1 section)

Different types of batteries hold different amounts of liquid. Cast iron has the largest capacity, while steel panel and bimetallic radiators have the smallest.

2. Volume of liquid in pipes (liters per 1 linear meter)

The main part of the coolant is often located in the pipes. The calculator takes into account the internal cross-section of pipes, which differs between polypropylene (PPR) and metal-plastic/cross-linked polyethylene (PEX).

  • 16 mm (PEX/Metal-plastic): 0.113 l/m (l/m)
  • 20 mm (PEX/Metal-plastic): 0.201 l/m (l/m)
  • 20 mm (PPR PN25): 0.137 l/m (l/m)
  • 25 mm (PPR PN25): 0.216 l/m (l/m)
  • 32 mm (PPR PN25): 0.353 l/m (l/m)
  • 40 mm (PPR PN25): 0.556 l/m (l/m)

Please note: Underfloor heating pipes (usually 16 mm PEX) are considered separately due to their large length. On average, 1 square meter of heated floor takes from 5 to 7 meters of pipe (depending on the installation pitch).

Expansion tank recommendations

Having received the final figure in the calculator, you can select a membrane tank.

  • For water: tank volume must be at least 10% from V_total.
  • For antifreeze (glycol): tank volume should be 15% from V_total, since the coefficient of thermal expansion of non-freezing liquids is higher.
