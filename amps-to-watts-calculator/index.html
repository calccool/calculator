Basic physical quantities

To work with electrical networks, it is important to distinguish between three key parameters:

  • Current strength (Amps, A): the amount of electricity passing through a conductor.
  • Voltage (Volts, V): “pressure” in the network (usually 220V in everyday life, 380V in industry, 12V in cars).
  • Power (Watts, W): general work performed by current (for example, heating a heating element or rotating a motor).

Calculation formulas: Current mathematics

The conversion of values ​​depends on the type of electrical circuit. We use the following standard formulas:

Direct current (DC)

The simplest option, used in automotive electrics or LED strips (12/24V). There are no reactive losses here.

$$P = I \times U$$

$$I = \frac{P}{U}$$

Alternating current (AC), 1 phase

Standard for apartments and private houses (220V). This is where the power factor $\cos \phi$ (Power Factor) appears.

$$P = I \times U \times \cos \phi$$

$$I = \frac{P}{U \times \cos \phi}$$

Alternating current (AC), 3 phase

Industrial networks 380V. The root of three ( $\approx 1.73$ ) is involved in the calculation, since the load is distributed between the phases.

$$P = \sqrt{3} \times I \times U \times \cos \phi$$

$$I = \frac{P}{\sqrt{3} \times U \times \cos \phi}$$

What is power factor (cos φ)?

This is a measure of how efficiently electricity is used.

  1. Resistive load ( $\cos \phi \approx 1.0$ ): electric stoves, heaters, incandescent lamps. Almost all energy is converted into heat/light.
  2. Inductive load ( $\cos \phi \approx 0.7 — 0.9$ ): refrigerators, air conditioners, vacuum cleaners, hammer drills. Some of the energy is spent creating magnetic fields in the motors.

When calculating the current for a powerful motor, always set the value $\cos \phi$ indicated on its nameplate, otherwise you risk choosing a wire that is too thin.

Practical advice on calculations

How to choose a cable cross-section?

Although our calculator gives a guideline value, remember the "golden rule" for copper cable when exposed wiring:

  • 1 mm² withstands approximately 10 Amps.
  • For a 16A outlet, a cable is traditionally used 2.5 mm².
  • For a powerful 32A hob - no less 6 mm².

Safety margin

When converting Watts to Amps for circuit breaker selection, always add a 20% margin. If the device consumes 14A, it is better to install a 16A machine. If the calculation shows exactly 16A, you should think about switching to the next rating (20A) and the corresponding core cross-section to avoid false alarms and overheating.
