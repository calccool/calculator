Cardiac Output Calculator (Fick Method): Calculation of CO, CI and SV

Cardiac output (CO, Cardiac Output) is a fundamental indicator of hemodynamics, reflecting the volume of blood that the heart ventricle releases into the systemic circulation in one minute. In domestic medicine, this parameter is often called Minute Volume of Blood Circulation (MCV).

Assessing cardiac output is critical for diagnosing heart failure, septic conditions and monitoring the effectiveness of fluid therapy in intensive care. The most accurate (“gold standard”) measurement is considered Fick's principle, based on the body's oxygen consumption.

How does a Fick calculator work?

The calculation is based on the law of conservation of mass: the volume of oxygen absorbed by the lungs from the air is equal to the volume of oxygen carried by the blood to the tissues. Fick's formula looks like this:

$$CO = \frac{VO_2}{C_aO_2 — C_vO_2}$$

Where:

  • $CO$ — Cardiac output (l/min).
  • $VO_2$ — Oxygen consumption by the body (ml/min).
  • $C_aO_2$ — Oxygen content in arterial blood.
  • $C_vO_2$ — Oxygen content in mixed venous blood.

Our calculator automatically calculates your blood's oxygen capacity using your hemoglobin level and saturation, eliminating complex manual calculations.

Input parameters and formulas

To obtain an accurate result, the calculator uses the following medical constants and algorithms:

  1. Body Surface Area (BSA): Calculated using the formula Du Bois. This is necessary to calculate the Cardiac Index (CI), which compensates for differences in patient size.
  2. Oxygen consumption ( $VO_2$ ):
    • Direct method: Enter the actual value measured by spirometry.
    • Calculation method: Using the Lafarge standard - $125 \text{ мл/мин} \times BSA$ .
  3. Blood oxygen capacity: Calculated using the Hüfner constant ( $1.34 \text{ мл O}_2$ per 1 g of hemoglobin).

$$CaO_2 = 1.34 \times Hgb \times \frac{SaO_2}{100}$$

Clinical Application

Understanding deviations from the norm helps to differentiate various pathological conditions:

Low cardiac output (inactivity)

A decrease in values ​​below normal (CI < 2.5 l/min/m²) may indicate:

  • Acute or chronic heart failure (decreased myocardial contractility).
  • Hypovolemia (blood loss, dehydration).
  • Cardiogenic shock.
  • Cardiac tamponade or massive pulmonary embolism.

High cardiac output (Hyperdynamia)

An increase in indicators above normal (CI > 4.0 l/min/m²) is typical for:

  • Sepsis (in the early stages, “warm shock”).
  • Thyrotoxicosis (hyperfunction of the thyroid gland).
  • Severe anemia (compensatory tachycardia).
  • Arteriovenous shunts.
