What is the difference between decimal (SI) and binary (IEC) prefixes?

Frequent confusion arises because hard drive manufacturers and operating systems count data volumes differently. Our converter strictly follows these standards.

Decimal standard (SI):

Used by storage device manufacturers (HDD, SSD). Based on the powers of the number 10.

$$1 \text{ MB} = 10^3 \text{ kB} = 10^6 \text{ B}$$

Binary standard (IEC):

Used by operating systems (such as Windows) to display the actual amount of memory. Based on powers of number 2.

$$1 \text{ MiB} = 2^{10} \text{ KiB} = 2^{20} \text{ B}$$

This is why the hard drive is 1 TB (Terabyte) in the system is displayed approximately as 931 GiB (Gibibyte). The converter automatically applies the correct $\frac{\text{Base}_\text{from}}{\text{Base}_\text{to}}$ coefficient for perfect accuracy.

Supported Data Groups

We have collected the most extensive database of quantities, divided into logical categories:

  1. Basic units: Bits (b) and Nibbles (4 bits).
  2. Decimal bytes (SI): From the usual Kilobytes (kB) and Megabytes (MB) to Ronnabytes (RB) and Quettabytes (QB) approved in 2022.
  3. Binary bytes (IEC): Kibibytes (KiB), Mebibytes (MiB), Gibibytes (GiB) and other quantities for accurate calculation of RAM and PC architecture.
  4. Historical and systemic: Characters (char), Machine words (16/32/64 bits), Unix disk blocks (512 bytes) and x86 memory pages (4096 bytes).
  5. Physical media: Allows you to find out how many old 3.5″ floppy disks (1.44 MB) or CD-ROM will fit on your modern Blu-ray disc.
  6. Exotic and hypothetical: Future quantities such as Brontobyte, Geopbyte and Saganbyte.
