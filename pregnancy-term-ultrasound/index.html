Gestational age calculator using ultrasound: Calculate by KTR, BPR and DB

Ultrasound examination (ultrasound) is currently the “gold standard” in determining the gestational age. Unlike the calendar method, which is based on the date of the last menstrual period and assumes an ideal 28-day cycle, fetometry (measuring the size of the fetus) allows you to establish the true gestational age based on the physical development of the embryo.

Our calculator uses international medical algorithms to interpret your ultrasound protocol data.

Basic parameters of fetometry

To calculate the period, three key indicators are used, the choice of which depends on the trimester of pregnancy:

  1. KTP (Coccyx-parietal size) / CRL (Crown-Rump Length) This is the maximum distance from the crown to the coccyx of the embryo. Measured in the first trimester (from 6 to 14 weeks). It is considered the most accurate indicator: the error is only $\pm 3$ days.
  2. BDP (Biparietal Diameter) / BPD (Biparietal Diameter) The distance between the parietal tubercles of the skull (essentially the width of the head). Used in the 2nd and 3rd trimesters, when the fetus is already too large to measure CTE.
  3. DB (Female Length) / FL (Femur Length) Measuring the femoral bone. It is an auxiliary parameter for clarifying the period in the later stages of gestation.

Mathematical calculation models

In modern obstetric practice, there is no single linear formula, since fetal development occurs spasmodically. Our algorithm is based on regression models developed by leading institutes of fetal medicine.

Formula for early stages (according to KTR)

One of the most cited is the Robinson and Fleming formula. The dependence of the period (in days) on the length of the embryo (in mm) is described by the equation:

$$\text{Gestational Age (Days)} = 8.052 \cdot \sqrt{CRL \times 1.037} + 23.73$$

A simplified empirical formula for quick estimation is also used:

$$\text{Weeks} = \frac{CRL(\text{cm}) + 6.5}{1}$$

Formula for the II and III trimester (according to BPR and DB)

Starting from 12-14 weeks, the relationship becomes non-linear. The Hadlock polynomial model is used:

$$\text{GA (Weeks)} = 9.54 + 1.482 \cdot (BPD) + 0.1676 \cdot (BPD)^2$$

Where BPD is the size in centimeters.

Why is the term according to ultrasound different from obstetric?

Often, expectant mothers are faced with a situation where the “period” period does not coincide with ultrasound data. This happens for several reasons:

  • Late ovulation: If conception did not occur on the 14th day of the cycle, but, for example, on the 20th, the fetus will be “younger” than the calendar date by almost a week.
  • Irregular cycle: With a cycle longer than 28 days, the obstetric method produces a large error.
  • Features of development: Genetically large or miniature fruits may produce indicators that differ from the average table values ​​(especially in the third trimester).

Accuracy of the method

The diagnostic value of ultrasound varies depending on the stage of pregnancy. The earlier the study is done, the more accurate the forecast of the date of birth (DOD).

  • up to 12 weeks: Accuracy $\pm 3-5$ days. The results of the first screening are considered the most reliable for establishing PDR.
  • 12–20 weeks: The error increases to $\pm 7-10$ days.
  • after 20 weeks: The size of the fetus depends more on genetics and nutrition, the error can reach $\pm 2-3$ weeks. At this stage, ultrasound is not used for dating, but to assess growth rates.

Important: This calculator provides informational data. The final conclusion about the gestational age and date of birth should be made by a gynecologist, comparing ultrasound data, the date of the last menstruation and the results of the hCG analysis.
