Yarn consumption calculator: calculation by weight, thread length and pattern

One of the most common problems when knitting is a lack of thread at the most crucial moment. It may be impossible to purchase one additional skein of the same lot (batch), and another lot may differ noticeably in shade. Our calculator solves this problem using two professional methods: by calculating the footage when replacing yarn and by weighing the control sample.

Method 1: Replacing yarn (by meter)

This method is ideal if you have a model description, but want to use a different brand of thread. The main mistake is to focus only on weight (for example, buy 500 grams instead of 500). Yarn of different compositions has different densities.

For correct recalculation, the formula for the total length of the thread is used:

$$L_{total} = \frac{W_{pattern}}{W_{ball\_orig}} \cdot L_{ball\_orig}$$

Where:

  • $W_{pattern}$ - required yarn weight according to description (g).
  • $W_{ball\_orig}$ - weight of one skein of original yarn (g).
  • $L_{ball\_orig}$ - length of thread in a skein of the original (m).

After finding $L_{total}$ , the calculator calculates how many grams of new yarn will cover this distance and how many whole skeins you will need to buy.

Method 2: Calculation based on control sample

This is the gold standard for custom knitting. Knit a small square (usually 10x10cm), wash it and let it dry. After that, weigh it on a kitchen scale to the nearest gram.

The mathematical logic is simple: we find the weight of yarn required per square centimeter of fabric:

$$W_{total} = \frac{W_{swatch}}{S_{swatch}} \cdot S_{project}$$

Where:

  • $W_{swatch}$ is the actual weight of your sample (g).
  • $S_{swatch}$ - sample area (width $\cdot$ height in cm).
  • $S_{project}$ is the total area of ​​all parts of the future product.

Three tips for accurate results

  1. Always knit the pattern using the same number of needles, with which you will knit the entire product. Different densities radically change the flow rate.
  2. Carry out WTO (wet heat treatment) sample before weighing. Some types of yarn (for example, cotton or bobbin cashmere in impregnation) change weight and volume after washing.
  3. Save the label until the end of work. It indicates the lot number (Lot), which will be needed if the calculations turned out to be incorrect and you will have to buy additional yarn.
