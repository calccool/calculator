Karvonen heart rate zone calculator for cycling: accurate calculation online

Training by eye in cycling often results in either overtraining or lack of progress. The Karvonen method is considered the “gold standard” for determining exercise intensity, since it takes into account not only your age, but also your individual physiological characteristics - Resting heart rate (RHR).

Unlike simplistic formulas, this calculator allows you to calculate your heart's operating range more accurately, which is critical for training for gravel races, road marathons, or simply burning fat effectively on the bike.

What is the advantage of the Karvonen method

The standard formula (220 minus age) is often wrong because it does not take into account the athlete’s degree of fitness. Karvonen's method operates with the concept heart reserve - the difference between the maximum heart rate and the heart rate at complete rest.

The basic mathematical calculation model is as follows:

$$TargetHR = ((MaxHR — RHR) \times \%Intensity) + RHR$$

Where:

  • MaxHR: Maximum heart rate.
  • RHR: Resting heart rate (measured in the morning immediately after waking up).
  • %Intensity: Intensity of the selected training zone.

Decoding training zones for cyclists

Each zone in cycling is responsible for the development of certain qualities of the body. Proper timing in these zones is key to increasing your FTP (functional threshold power).

How to set up a calculator correctly

To get the most accurate data, pay attention to the following parameters:

Determination of resting heart rate (RHR)

The best way to find out your RHR is to measure your pulse in the morning, without getting out of bed, for 3-5 days and take the average. For professional cyclists this figure can be 35-45 beats/min, for fans - 55-65 beats/min.

Selecting a MaxHR formula

Our calculator has two main methods for calculating maximum heart rate:

  1. Tanaka formula: Considered most accurate for people over 30 years of age and actively training athletes. $$MaxHR = 208 — (0.7 \times Age)$$
  2. Classic formula: A simple Haskell-Fox method suitable for beginners. $$MaxHR = 220 — Age$$
  3. Manual entry: If you've taken a stress test in a lab or recorded your true maximum during a hill finish, use this option.

Practical advice for cyclists

  • Z2 is your best friend: Most volume (up to 80% of training time) should take place in the second zone. This is the foundation without which progress in the mountains is impossible.
  • Correction for heat: Remember that when the air temperature is high, your heart rate may be higher by 5-10 hits at the same power. This is called "cardiovascular drift".
  • Listen to the body: If the calculator shows Z2 and you feel very short of breath, you may be overtired or sick today.
