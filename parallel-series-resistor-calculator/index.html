Online Resistor Calculator: Parallel and Series Connection

Calculation of electrical circuit resistance

When designing electronic circuits, repairing equipment, or working with Arduino, a situation often arises when a resistor of the required value is not at hand. In this case, engineers resort to a trick: they combine several resistors in one circuit to obtain the required value.

Our online resistor calculator allows you to instantly find the final resistance for any combination of elements, as well as calculate current and power parameters to avoid overheating of components.

Types of resistor connections

There are two main ways to connect components, and their physics work completely differently.

1. Serial connection (Series)

In this case, the resistors go one after another, like a train. Electric current has no alternative paths and flows through every element.

  • Rule: The total resistance is always more than the highest denomination in the chain.
  • Formula: $$R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \dots + R_n$$

2. Parallel connection

The current is divided into several streams, flowing through all resistors simultaneously. It is similar to how water flows through several parallel pipes.

  • Rule: The total resistance is always less, than the smallest denomination in the circuit.
  • Formula: $$\frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \dots + \frac{1}{R_n}$$

To quickly calculate two resistors, a simplified formula is often used:

$$R_{eq} = \frac{R_1 \times R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

Calculation using Ohm's Law

Our tool is not just an adder. If you specify the power supply voltage ( $V$ ), the calculator will automatically calculate:

  1. Total Current ( $I$ ): The current drawn by the entire circuit. $$I = \frac{V}{R_{eq}}$$
  2. Total Power ( $P$ ): The energy released by the circuit as heat. This is a critical parameter when choosing resistors (eg 0.25W, 0.5W or 5W). $$P = V \times I$$

Examples of use

  • You need 500 Ohms, but there are only 1000 Ohms: Connect two 1000 ohm resistors parallel, and you will get exactly 500 ohms.
  • You need 150 Ohms, but there are 100 and 50 Ohms: Connect them sequentially.
  • You need strong resistance: By connecting 4 weak resistors (0.25W) in parallel, you will get an equivalent that can dissipate 4 times more heat (1W).
