What is the Drake Equation?

The Drake Equation is a probabilistic argument used to estimate the number ( $N$ ) of active, communicating extraterrestrial civilizations in the Milky Way galaxy.

The formula looks like this:

$$N = R_* \times f_p \times n_e \times f_l \times f_i \times f_c \times L$$

Where:

  • $$N$$ - the number of civilizations with which we have a chance to come into contact.
  • $$R_*$$ is the rate of star formation in our Galaxy (stars per year).
  • $$f_p$$ is the fraction of stars with planetary systems.
  • $$n_e$$ - average number of planets potentially suitable for life (per star with planets).
  • $$f_l$$ is the probability of life arising on a suitable planet.
  • $$f_i$$ - the probability that life will develop into an intelligent form.
  • $$f_c$$ - the proportion of civilizations that have developed technologies for communication (radio signals).
  • $$L$$ is the lifetime of such a civilization (the period during which they transmit signals into space).

Analysis of parameters: From astronomy to sociology

The equation is divided into two parts: astronomical (which we have learned to measure quite accurately) and socio-biological (where we can only make guesses).

1. Astronomical factors ( $R_*, f_p, n_e$ )

Thanks to the Kepler telescope and modern exoplanet research, we know that planets are the rule rather than the exception.

  • In the Milky Way, about 1.5 – 3 new stars.
  • Almost every second star has planets ( $f_p \approx 0.5 — 1.0$ ).
  • Planets in the “habitable zone” (Goldilocks zone), where water can be liquid, are quite common.

2. Biological factors ( $f_l, f_i$ )

This is where the uncertainty begins. Life on Earth arose almost as soon as conditions became suitable, which speaks in favor of a high $f_l$ value. However, intelligence appeared only after billions of years of evolution. Is intelligence an inevitable outcome of evolution or a rare accident?

3. Technological factors ( $f_c, L$ )

The most critical parameter is

$$L$$

(lifetime). If technological civilizations are prone to self-destruction (nuclear war, ecology, AI) 100-200 years after the invention of radio, then the Galaxy is empty. If they exist for millions of years, we are surrounded by neighbors.

Fermi Paradox: “Where is everyone?”

If our calculator shows a high value of $N$ (for example, thousands of civilizations), a logical question arises, formulated by Enrico Fermi: “Why don’t we see their tracks?”.

Possible explanations:

  1. Unique Earth hypothesis: The coefficients of $f_l$ and $f_i$ are negligible.
  2. Great filter: Civilizations perish before they can go into deep space.
  3. Zoo: Higher civilizations know about us, but deliberately do not interfere.
  4. Time difference: We have existed for too short a time by cosmic standards for our signals and the signals of others to coincide in time and space.
