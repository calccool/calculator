Dextrose and GIR Calculator: Calculate Glucose Concentration for Infusion

In clinical practice, especially in neonatology, pediatrics and intensive care, there is often a need for dextrose (glucose) solutions of non-standard concentrations (for example, 12.5% ​​or 15%), which are not in the original packaging. Doctors have to mix a “basic” solution (for example, 5% or 10%) with a concentrated solution (40% or 50%).

Our tool automates this process, eliminating the risk of arithmetic error, and additionally calculates two critical security parameters: GIR And Osmolarity.

Basic calculation formulas

The calculator uses proven pharmacological formulas to calculate mixture parameters.

1. Calculation of final concentration

When mixing two solutions of different concentrations, the law of conservation of dry matter mass is used:

$$C_{mix} = \frac{V_1 \times C_1 + V_2 \times C_2}{V_1 + V_2}$$

Where:

  • $V_1, V_2$ — volumes of mixed solutions (ml).
  • $C_1, C_2$ — concentration of solutions in percent (%).

2. Calculation of GIR (Glucose Infusion Rate)

The rate of glucose supply is a key parameter to prevent hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia. Measured in mg/kg/min.

$$GIR = \frac{Rate \times Conc \times 1000}{Weight \times 60 \times 100}$$

Where:

  • Rate — infusion rate (ml/h).
  • Conc — dextrose concentration (%).
  • Weight — patient weight (kg).
  • 1000 — conversion of grams to milligrams.
  • 60 — convert hours to minutes.

3. Estimation of osmolarity

The osmolarity of the dextrose solution is calculated approximately based on the molar mass of glucose monohydrate (198 g/mol).

$$Osm \approx Conc (\%) \times 50.5$$

Important: If the calculated osmolarity exceeds 900 mOsm/l, administration of the solution through a peripheral venous catheter is prohibited due to the high risk of phlebitis and tissue necrosis. Central venous access is required.

GIR standards for different patient groups

When prescribing fluid therapy, it is important to adhere to target GIR values ​​(mg/kg/min) to maintain normoglycemia:

  • Premature newborns: 4 – 8 mg/kg/min.
  • Full-term newborns: 3 – 5 mg/kg/min.
  • Children: 2 – 4 mg/kg/min.
  • Adults: 2 – 3 mg/kg/min (usually does not exceed 4-5 mg/kg/min with parenteral nutrition).
