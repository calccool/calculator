Arrhenius Equation Calculator: Calculate Reaction Rate Constant (k)

Chemical kinetics is a branch of chemistry that studies the rate of reactions. One of the fundamental laws in this area is Arrhenius equation, established by the Swedish physical chemist Svante Arrhenius in 1889.

Our online calculator allows you to instantly calculate the reaction rate constant ( $k$ ), taking into account temperature dependence and energy barrier. This is an indispensable tool for chemistry students, technologists and researchers.

Arrhenius formula

The equation describes the dependence of the rate constant of a chemical reaction on temperature. In exponential form it looks like this:

$$k = A \cdot e^{-\frac{E_a}{RT}}$$

Where:

  • $k$ — reaction rate constant (units depend on the order of the reaction).
  • $A$ — pre-exponential factor (frequency factor). Characterizes the total number of molecular collisions per second.
  • $E_a$ — activation energy ( $Дж/моль$ or $кДж/моль$ ). The minimum energy required for reactants to react.
  • $R$ — universal gas constant ( $8.314\ Дж \cdot моль^{-1} \cdot K^{-1}$ ).
  • $T$ — absolute temperature in Kelvin ( $K$ ).

What is the Boltzmann factor?

Expression

$$e^{-E_a/RT}$$

, which our calculator calculates, is called the Boltzmann factor. It shows the fraction of molecules whose kinetic energy exceeds the activation energy $E_a$ .

Important: Even a small increase in temperature or decrease in activation energy (for example, when using a catalyst) leads to an exponential increase in the reaction rate.

How to use the calculator

  1. Enter Activation Energy ( $E_a$ ). Choose convenient units of measurement: Joules, kJ, kcal or electron volts. The calculator will automatically convert them to SI.
  2. Specify Temperature ( $T$ ). You can enter values ​​in Celsius ( $^\circ C$ ), the calculator itself will convert them to Kelvin using the formula: $$T(K) = t(^\circ C) + 273.15$$ .
  3. Set the Multiplier ( $A$ ). If it is unknown, values ​​of the order of $10^{11} — 10^{13}$ (for bimolecular gas reactions) are often used for estimation calculations.

Linear form of the equation

To experimentally determine the activation energy, the logarithmic form of the Arrhenius equation is often used. If you plot the dependence of $\ln(k)$ on the inverse temperature $1/T$ , you get a straight line:

$$\ln(k) = \ln(A) — \frac{E_a}{R} \cdot \frac{1}{T}$$

The tangent of the slope of this straight line allows us to find the activation energy: $\tan(\alpha) = -E_a/R$ .

Application

This calculation is critical in:

  • Pharmaceuticals: to predict the shelf life of drugs (accelerated aging).
  • Ecology: to estimate the decay rate of pollutants.
  • Food industry: for calculating pasteurization and sterilization modes.
