Operating principle: Continuity equation

The method is based on the physical law of conservation of mass: the volume of blood passing through the left ventricular outflow tract (LVOT) must be equal to the volume passing through the aortic valve opening (in the absence of shunts or regurgitation).

The mathematical calculation algorithm consists of three steps:

  1. Calculation of LVOT cross-sectional area (CSA): The measured outflow tract diameter ( $D$ ) is used. $$CSA_{LVOT} = \pi \times (\frac{D}{2})^2$$
  2. Calculation of stroke volume (Stroke Volume): The cross-sectional area is multiplied by the integral of the linear velocity of blood flow in the outflow tract ( $VTI_{LVOT}$ ). $$SV = CSA_{LVOT} \times VTI_{LVOT}$$
  3. Calculation of valve area (AVA): Stroke volume is divided by the velocity integral on the valve itself ( $VTI_{AV}$ ) obtained using continuous wave Doppler. $$AVA = \frac{SV}{VTI_{AV}}$$

Interpretation of results (Gradation of stenosis)

Our calculator automatically classifies the received data according to modern recommendations ESC/EACTS And AHA/ACC.

Why is indexing by body area needed?

In clinical practice, the absolute value of AVA can be misleading if the patient has an unusual constitution (very large or very thin).

For example, the $0.9 \text{ —Å–º}^2$ valve area for a person weighing 50 kg may be a moderate stenosis, but for an athlete weighing 110 kg it may be critical.

To do this, our tool calculates iAVA (Indexed Aortic Valve Area):

$$iAVA = \frac{AVA}{BSA}$$

Where $BSA$ (Body Surface Area) is the body surface area calculated using Dubois' formula based on the patient's height and weight.

Required measurements to enter

  • LVOT Diameter(cm): Measured in the parasternal position along the long axis in mid-systole.
  • VTI LVOT (cm): Measured with pulsed wave Doppler (PW) in the apical 5-chamber or 3-chamber position.
  • VTI AV (cm): Measured by continuous wave Doppler (CW) through the aortic valve.
