Jet lag calculator: online calculation of time zone adaptation time

Jet lag (time zone change syndrome) is a temporary disruption of the body's “internal clock” (circadian rhythms) caused by rapid movement across multiple time zones. Your body continues to live according to the time of the departure point, while the environment requires immediate restructuring.

The main cause of discomfort is desynchronization of production melatonin (sleep hormone) and cortisol. Our online calculator helps you mathematically predict how long it will take your metabolism to fully adapt to new conditions.

How to use the jet lag calculator?

The tool takes into account three key factors that influence the speed of biorhythm adjustment:

  1. Flight direction: A flight to the East is traditionally considered more difficult, since the day is shortened, and it is more difficult for the body to “fall asleep earlier” than to “go to bed later.”
  2. Number of crossed belts: The mathematical model limits the calculation to 12 belts, since this is the maximum theoretical deviation.
  3. Time in the air: Staying in an airplane seat for a long time creates additional stress (fatigue), which adds up to 1 day to the recovery time.
  4. Age factor: With age, the plasticity of circadian rhythms decreases, which requires a correction factor.

Formula for calculating adaptation time

Our calculator uses an advanced algorithm based on physiological indicators. The general calculation formula is as follows:

$$T = (D_{base} \cdot Z + P_{flight}) \cdot K_{age}$$

Where:

  • $T$ - total number of days to full normal.
  • $D_{base}$ - basic direction coefficient (0.66 for West, 1.0 for East).
  • $Z$ - number of time zones crossed.
  • $P_{flight}$ - penalty for flight duration (0.5 days for a flight > 8 hours, 1.0 day for a flight > 14 hours).
  • $K_{age}$ - age adaptation multiplier.

How to speed up adaptation: expert advice

To reduce the time calculated by the calculator, use the light correction method:

  • When flying to the West: In the evening, try to get as much bright sunlight or artificial light as possible. This will help stretch out the day. In the morning, on the contrary, it is recommended to avoid direct sun in the first hours after waking up.
  • When flying to the East: In the morning, strive for maximum illumination (walking outside, light lamps). In the evening, it is necessary to darken it as early as possible and, if necessary, take melatonin in consultation with the doctor.
  • Hydration: Drink more water during the flight. Dehydration increases symptoms of jet lag and slows metabolic adjustments.
  • First day rule: Try not to go to bed before local evening (at least until 20:00–21:00), no matter how sleepy you feel.
