Online ground loop resistance calculator

Reliable grounding is the main condition for the electrical safety of any residential or industrial facility. A properly designed ground loop conducts dangerous leakage and short-circuit currents to ground, protecting people from electric shock and equipment from damage.

The presented calculator allows engineers, designers and home craftsmen to quickly calculate the equivalent resistance of a complex ground loop consisting of a system of vertical rods (pins) and a horizontal strip connecting them. The calculation is based on classical electrical formulas and takes into account the most important environmental parameters.

What determines the quality of the ground loop?

The final resistance to current flow is influenced by many physical and geometric factors:

  • Soil type and condition: Clay, sand and rock conduct current in completely different ways. Humidity and temperature also make significant adjustments.
  • Laying depth: The deeper the electrodes are driven, the more stable their contact with moist soil layers, which are less susceptible to freezing in winter or drying out in summer.
  • Geometric dimensions: The length and diameter of the vertical pins, as well as the width of the connecting strip, directly determine the area of ​​​​metal contact with the ground.
  • Number of elements and their mutual influence: Increasing the number of pins reduces the total resistance, but due to the shielding effect, their total efficiency decreases (for this, a utilization factor is used in the calculations).

Formulas and calculation algorithm

The basis of the calculator's operation is calculating the resistance of individual elements and then combining them into a parallel electrical circuit.

The resistance of a single vertical grounding conductor with a circular cross-section is calculated using the following formula:

$$R_v = \frac{\rho}{2 \pi L} \left( \ln\left(\frac{2L}{d}\right) + \frac{1}{2} \ln\left(\frac{4t + L}{4t — L}\right) \right)$$

Where $R_v$ is the resistance of one rod, $\rho$ is the electrical resistivity of the soil, $L$ is the length of the rod, $d$ is its diameter, and $t$ is the depth from the ground surface to the middle of the rod.

The resistance of the horizontal ground electrode (connecting strip) is determined as follows:

$$R_h = \frac{\rho}{2 \pi L_h} \ln\left(\frac{2 L_h^2}{b h}\right)$$

Here $L_h$ is the total length of the strip, $b$ is the width of the strip, $h$ is the depth of the trench.

The final equivalent resistance of the entire grounding loop takes into account the utilization coefficients of vertical ( $\eta_v$ ) and horizontal ( $\eta_h$ ) elements, since they are laid close to each other and their current spreading fields overlap:

$$R_{total} = \frac{\left( \frac{R_v}{n \cdot \eta_v} \right) \cdot \left( \frac{R_h}{\eta_h} \right)}{\left( \frac{R_v}{n \cdot \eta_v} \right) + \left( \frac{R_h}{\eta_h} \right)}$$

The value $n$ in this formula represents the total number of vertical bars.

Resistivity of main types of soils

The key parameter in the equations is soil resistivity $\rho$ . For preliminary calculations, if precise geodetic measurements on the site have not been carried out, it is customary to use average tabular values.

Regulatory requirements (PUE)

According to the Electrical Installation Rules (PUE), the final value of the ground loop resistance is strictly standardized depending on the type of network and voltage. For standard electrical installations with a voltage of 380 V (three-phase network) or 220 V (single-phase network) with a solidly grounded neutral, the resistance should not exceed 4 Ohms. When using low-power generators or transformers, this threshold can be increased to 10 Ohms.

For lightning protection systems for private houses, a resistance of no more than 10 Ohms is considered optimal, and for high-precision telecommunications and medical equipment the requirements are more stringent - from 1 Ohm to 2 Ohms. If the calculator shows that the calculated value exceeds the norm, it is necessary to adjust the project: add additional rods, increase their length, or apply deep modular-pin grounding technology.
