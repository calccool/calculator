Calculator for calculating cable cross-section by power and current (Online + PUE)

Choosing the correct cross-section of the electrical cable is not a matter of economy, but of fire safety. An error in calculations can lead to overheating of the insulation, short circuit and failure of expensive equipment.

Our online cable cross-section calculator performs calculations based on physical laws and regulatory tables of PUE (Chapter 1.3), taking into account critical factors: conductor material, laying method, temperature and line length.

Calculation formulas: how does it work?

The calculator algorithm is based on two key checks: heating (allowable current) and voltage loss.

1. Calculation of current ( $I$ )

First of all, it is necessary to convert the power of the devices ( $P$ ) due to the current flowing through the circuit.

For single-phase network (220 V):

$$I = \frac{P}{U \cdot \cos\phi}$$

For three-phase network (380 V):

$$I = \frac{P}{\sqrt{3} \cdot U \cdot \cos\phi}$$

Where:

  • $P$ - total load power (Watt).
  • $U$ - mains voltage (220 or 380 Volts).
  • $\cos\phi$ - power factor (0.8–1.0), reflecting the presence of reactive load (motors, transformers).

2. Voltage drop calculation ( $\Delta U$ )

On long lines the cable acts as a resistor. If the resistance is too high, the voltage at the end of the line will drop below normal.

$$\Delta U = \frac{I \cdot \rho \cdot L}{S} \cdot K$$

Where:

  • $\rho$ - resistivity (0.0175 for copper, 0.028 for aluminum).
  • $L$ - cable length (m).
  • $S$ - cross-sectional area (mm²).
  • $K$ - coefficient (2 for one phase, 1.73 for three).

Influence of installation method on cable selection

Many calculators ignore this parameter, but it is critically important. The cable heats up when current passes. If the heat is not removed, the insulation melts.

  • Open wiring: The cable is cooled by air. Permissible currents are higher.
  • In a pipe or wall: Heat removal is difficult. Sectional margin required.
  • In the ground: The soil removes heat well, which allows you to use a cable with a smaller cross-section for the same load.

Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

Copper or Aluminum?

Copper (Cu) has lower resistance, is more ductile and is less oxidized. Modern standards for residential premises require the use of only copper. Aluminum (Al) is cheaper and lighter, but requires a cross-section 1.6 times larger than copper for the same load. It is justified for powerful input cables or overhead lines (SIP).

What is $\cos\phi$ (Cosine Phi)?

This is the power factor.

  • For incandescent lamps, heaters, kettles $\cos\phi = 1$ .
  • For electric motors, pumps, refrigerators, washing machines $\cos\phi \approx 0.7 — 0.8$ . If you do not know the exact value, for mixed household load it is recommended to set 0.95.

How to choose a circuit breaker?

Circuit breaker protects cable, not a device. Its rating must be less than the maximum permissible current of the cable.

  • Example: The 2.5 mm² cable can withstand 27A. You cannot set the machine to 32A - the cable will burn out before the machine breaks down. The correct choice is 16A or 20A. Our calculator automatically selects a safe denomination.
