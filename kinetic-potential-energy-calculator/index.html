Online kinetic and potential energy calculator: calculation and formulas

In classical physics, the mechanical energy of an object is determined by its motion and position in space relative to other bodies. Our online tool allows you not only to calculate individual types of energy, but also to simulate their mutual transformation in the process of free fall.

Types of energy in classical mechanics

Mechanical energy is divided into two main types, which describe different physical states of an object:

  1. Kinetic Energy ( $E_k$ ): The energy possessed by a body due to its motion. It depends on the mass of the object and the square of its speed.
  2. Potential energy ( $E_p$ ): The energy of interaction of a body with a force field (in this case, gravitational). It is determined by the height of the body's rise above the conditional zero level.

Basic calculation formulas

For accurate calculations, the calculator uses fundamental physical equations:

  • For kinetic energy: $$E_k = \frac{m \cdot v^2}{2}$$ Where $m$ is mass (kg), and $v$ is speed (m/s).
  • For potential energy: $$E_p = m \cdot g \cdot h$$ Where $g$ is the acceleration due to gravity, and $h$ is the height (m).
  • Law of conservation of energy: In a closed system in the absence of friction forces, the total mechanical energy remains unchanged: $$E_{total} = E_k + E_p = const$$

How to use the calculator

Our service provides three operating modes, switching between which is carried out in the built-in tabs:

  • Kinetic energy: Enter mass and speed to find out the energy of a moving object (such as a car or a bullet).
  • Potential energy: Enter mass and altitude by selecting a planet to calculate the energy of a body at rest at altitude.
  • Fall simulation: A unique mode that shows how potential energy transforms into kinetic energy during the fall from the initial height to the current point.

Additional units of measurement

In addition to standard Joules (J), the calculator automatically converts results to alternative units:

  • Kilojoules (kJ) — for large objects;
  • Kilocalories (kcal) — to estimate the energy equivalent;
  • Watt hours (Wh) - for comparison with electrical energy.
