Fourth Degree Equation: Review and Calculator

Fourth degree equation (or quartic equation ) is a polynomial equation in which the highest power of the unknown variable $x$ is 4. The general form of such an equation looks like this:

$$a x^4 + b x^3 + c x^2 + d x + e = 0$$

Where $a, b, c, d, e$ are the coefficients, and $a \neq 0$. According to the fundamental theorem of algebra, such an equation always has four roots. These roots can be real or complex, different or identical.

Solution Method (Step Logic)

Solving a biquadratic equation always reduces to solving a quadratic one. This process occurs in three key steps, which our calculator displays in the results:

  1. Variable Substitution: A new variable is introduced $y$ , which is equal to the square of $x$ : $$y = x^2$$
  2. Solution of the quadratic equation for $y$ : Substituting $y$ into the biquadratic equation, we obtain the standard quadratic equation: $$a y^2 + c y + e = 0$$ The roots of $y_{1,2}$ are found through the discriminant $D$ using the well-known formula: $$D = c^2 — 4 a e$$ $$y_{1,2} = \frac{-c \pm \sqrt{D}}{2 a}$$
  3. Reverse replacement and finding the roots of $x$ : Having obtained the roots of $y_1$ and $y_2$ , we return to the original variable $x$ : $x = \pm \sqrt{y}$ .
    • If $y > 0$ , we get two real roots: $x = \pm \sqrt{y}$ .
    • If $y = 0$ , we get one real root: $x = 0$ .
    • If $y < 0$ , we get two purely imaginary (complex) roots: $x = \pm \sqrt{|y|} i$ .

Calculation example

Let's look at a classic example that you can enter into a calculator:

Equation: $x^4 — 10 x^2 + 9 = 0$ (where $a=1, c=-10, e=9$ ).

  1. Quadratic equation for $y$ : $y^2 — 10 y + 9 = 0$
  2. Find $y$ : $D = (-10)^2 — 4(1)(9) = 100 — 36 = 64$ . $y_1 = \frac{10 + \sqrt{64}}{2} = 9$ $y_2 = \frac{10 — \sqrt{64}}{2} = 1$
  3. Find $x$ :
    • From $y_1 = 9$ : $x = \pm \sqrt{9} \implies \mathbf{x_1 = 3, x_2 = -3}$
    • From $y_2 = 1$ : $x = \pm \sqrt{1} \implies \mathbf{x_3 = 1, x_4 = -1}$

Our calculator will not only give these four real roots, but will also show the intermediate equation for $y$ and its roots.
