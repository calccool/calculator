Calculator for diluting alcohol with water, taking into account contraction

Proper dilution of ethyl alcohol is not just an arithmetic problem, but a complex physical and chemical process. The main difficulty lies in the phenomenon contractions, due to which the final volume of the mixture is always less than the sum of the initial volumes of water and alcohol.

Our tool allows you to perform calculations in two ways:

  • Dilution with water: determining the exact volume of water that needs to be added to the alcohol to obtain the target strength.
  • Mixing alcohols: calculation of the final strength and volume when combining two solutions with different ethanol contents.

What is contraction and why 1+1 does not equal 2

When you mix 1 liter of pure alcohol and 1 liter of water, the output you will get is not 2 liters of vodka, but approximately 1.93 liters. This is due to the fact that water molecules are smaller than alcohol molecules and they “occupy” free space in the interatomic structure of ethanol.

This effect reaches its maximum when obtaining a solution with a strength of about 45–53%. Without taking this “shrinkage” into account, your strength calculations will have an error, which is critical for quality drinks.

Formulas used in calculations

For a basic calculation without taking into account temperature (at standard 20°C), a modified dilution formula is used:

$$V_w = V_s \cdot \left( \frac{C_1}{C_2} — 1 \right)$$

Where:

  • $V_w$ - required volume of added water (ml);
  • $V_s$ — initial volume of alcohol (ml);
  • $C_1$ - initial alcohol strength (%);
  • $C_2$ - required solution strength (%).

To calculate the final volume taking into account contraction ( $V_{real}$ ), an empirical compression factor is used, which is integrated into the algorithm of our calculator.

How to mix correctly: expert advice

To ensure that your drink remains clear and does not acquire an unpleasant “hard” taste, follow the three rules of the distiller’s “Gold Standard”:

Main rules of mixing

  • Pour alcohol into water: This is the most important rule. When alcohol is poured into water, the reaction occurs smoothly. If you do the opposite (pouring water into alcohol), a sharp thermal jump occurs, which leads to the formation of side compounds and clouding of the liquid (opalescence).
  • Use purified water: The ideal option is artesian or bottled water with low mineralization. Tap water, due to the abundance of salts, almost always produces a white precipitate upon contact with alcohol.
  • Let the mixture rest: Immediately after mixing, chemical processes continue. The drink needs at least 2-3 days (preferably a week) in a dark, cool place to complete hydration and stabilize the taste.
