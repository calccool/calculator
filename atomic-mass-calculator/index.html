Theory: How is atomic mass calculated?

Method 1: Relative Atomic Mass ( $A_r$ )

In nature, most chemical elements exist as a mixture of isotopes. Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same number of protons), but with different masses (different number of neutrons).

The atomic mass listed in the Periodic Table is weighted average masses of all stable isotopes, taking into account their abundance.

Calculation formula:

$$A_r = \frac{\sum (m_i \times P_i)}{100}$$

Where:

  • $m_i$ is the mass of the $i$ isotope (in amu).
  • $P_i$ — abundance of the $i$ isotope in percent (%).

Example (Chlorine):

Chlorine consists of two main isotopes: Cl-35 (75.78%) and Cl-37 (24.22%).

$$A_r(Cl) = \frac{(34.969 \times 75.78) + (36.966 \times 24.22)}{100} \approx 35.45 \text{ а.е.м.}$$

Method 2: Nuclide mass (via particles)

If you want to know the mass of a particular atom, you need to add up the masses of all its subatomic particles. An atom consists of a nucleus (protons + neutrons) and an electron cloud.

Particle masses in atomic mass units (u or amu):

Calculation formula:

$$m_{atom} = (N_p \times m_p) + (N_n \times m_n) + (N_e \times m_e)$$

Where $N$ is the number of corresponding particles.

Important: This method gives the so-called "rest mass of particles". In nuclear physics, the real mass of a nucleus is always slightly less than the sum of the masses of nucleons due to mass defect (nuclear binding energy), but for basic calculations summation is the standard method.

Mass Number vs Atomic Mass

Students often confuse these concepts. Our calculator helps you see the difference:

  • Mass number (A): It's always integer (sum of protons and neutrons). For example, for Carbon-12 it is exactly 12.
  • Atomic mass: This is a fractional number that takes into account the exact weight of particles and electrons. For the same Carbon-12, the atomic mass is equal to 12.00000 u (by definition), but for other isotopes it is fractional (for example, C-13 = 13.00335 u).
