Basic rules for calculating shutter speed

It is traditionally believed that in order to get a sharp photo, the denominator of the shutter speed must be no less than the focal length of the lens. However, with the advent of multi-megapixel cameras and different sensor formats, this formula has become more complicated.

Mathematical calculation model:

To calculate the exact value, use the following formula:

$$T_{safe} = \frac{1}{F \cdot K \cdot R \cdot 2^{-S}}$$

Where:

  • $F$ — lens focal length (mm);
  • $K$ — sensor crop factor (ESF);
  • $R$ — resolution coefficient (depending on megapixels);
  • $S$ — number of stabilization stages (Stops).

Why does focal length affect sharpness?

The larger the focal length (zoom), the more not only the object in the frame increases, but also the amplitude of the slightest trembling of your hands. At a wide angle (e.g. 16mm), a little wobble is almost unnoticeable, but at a telephoto lens (200mm) it will turn the photo into an abstraction.

Taking into account crop factor and high resolution

If you use a camera with a smaller sensor (APS-C or Micro 4/3), the effective focal length increases. For example, a 50mm lens on a Sony camera with a 1.5x crop gives the same angle of view as a 75mm full frame. At the same time, hand trembling also “increases” by 1.5 times.

Modern sensors with a resolution of 45-60 megapixels require even faster shutter speeds, since the slightest shift in light is recorded on neighboring pixels due to their high density. Our calculator takes this factor into account through the “Camera Resolution” parameter.

Video shooting and the 180 degree rule

For videographers, the calculation is structured differently. In order for movement in a frame to look natural to the human eye (cinematic), the shutter speed must be tied to the frame rate (FPS).

Shutter angle formula:

$$Shutter = \frac{1}{FPS \cdot (\frac{360}{Angle})}$$

The optimal angle is 180°, at which the shutter speed is half the frame rate. If you're shooting at 24 fps, the ideal shutter speed is 1/48 (or the closest 1/50).

5 Tips for Maximum Sharpness When Shooting Handheld

  1. Correct grip: Press your elbows toward your body, creating a “tripod” out of your own body.
  2. Breathing technique: Press the release button while half-exhaling, smoothly, without jerking.
  3. Using Burst Shooting: In a series of 3-5 frames, the middle shots are usually sharper than the first and last ones due to the lack of moment when pressing/releasing the button.
  4. Fulcrum: If the shutter speed is critically slow, lean your shoulder against a tree or wall.
  5. Trust the stub, but check: The effectiveness of the stabilizer (IBIS) decreases with increasing focal length. What works at 35mm may not work at 300mm.
