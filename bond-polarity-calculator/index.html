How to determine the type of chemical bond?

The type of bond between atoms depends on how strongly each of them attracts shared electron pairs. This ability is called electronegativity (Electronegativity, $\chi$ ).

To find out the nature of the bond, it is necessary to find the modulus of the difference in electronegativity of two elements.

Calculation formula

The basic equation for determining polarity is:

$$\Delta\chi = | \chi_A — \chi_B |$$

Where:

  • $\chi_A$ is the electronegativity of the first element.
  • $\chi_B$ is the electronegativity of the second element.

Important: The limit of 1.7 is conditional. Bonds with $\Delta\chi > 1.7$ are considered to be predominantly ionic, but in practice, “pure” ionic bonding (100%) does not exist. Even in the $CsF$ (cesium fluoride) crystal, the bond has some degree of covalency.

Calculation of percent ionic character

Unlike simple tables, our tool performs in-depth analysis by calculating Ionic Character Percentage. This allows us to understand how strongly the electron cloud is shifted towards the more electronegative atom.

To do this we use Pauling's equation:

$$Ionic\ Character\ (\%) = (1 — e^{-0.25 \times (\Delta\chi)^2}) \times 100$$

Examples of calculations

  1. Molecule $Cl_2$ (Chlorine):
    • $\chi(Cl) = 3.16$
    • $\Delta\chi = |3.16 — 3.16| = 0$
    • Result: Pure covalent non-polar bond (0% ionic character).
  2. Molecule $HCl$ (Hydrogen Chloride):
    • $\chi(H) = 2.20$, $\chi(Cl) = 3.16$
    • $\Delta\chi = 0.96$
    • Result: Covalent polar bond. Shift of electron density towards chlorine.
  3. Molecule $NaCl$ (Common salt):
    • $\chi(Na) = 0.93$, $\chi(Cl) = 3.16$
    • $\Delta\chi = 2.23$
    • Result: Ionic bond (high percentage of ionic character).
