Room heat loss calculator: Calculation of heating power and radiators

Why do you need a heat loss calculation?

Choosing a heating system “by eye” is a risk. If you buy a boiler that is too weak or install few radiators, the house will be cold in winter. If you take equipment with a large supply, you will overpay for installation and will lose money every month on inefficient energy consumption.

Our online heat loss calculator allows you to perform engineering calculations with high accuracy without delving into complex SNiP reference books. It determines how much heat is lost through walls, windows and ventilation, and how much energy is needed to compensate for these losses.

What does heat loss depend on?

Many people use the old rule: "100 watts per 1 square meter". However, this method worked for old wooden windows and brick walls. In modern conditions it often gives an error of up to 50%.

Our algorithm takes into account 5 key factors:

  1. Temperature difference ( $\Delta T$ ): The difference between the desired indoor temperature and the minimum outdoor temperature in your area.
  2. Area of ​​enclosing structures: Not only floor meters are taken into account, but also ceiling heights, as well as the number of external (“street”) walls.
  3. Glazing: Up to 40% of heat escapes through windows. The calculator distinguishes between ordinary glass and modern energy-saving double-glazed windows.
  4. Insulation: The wall material (brick, concrete, sandwich panels) has different thermal resistance.
  5. Infiltration: Heat loss for heating fresh air entering through ventilation or cracks.

Calculation formula (Basic physics)

The simplified heat loss formula underlying the calculations looks like this:

$$Q = S \times \Delta T \times k \times (1 + \text{Reserve})$$

Where:

  • $Q$ — required heat loss (W).
  • $S$ - area of ​​enclosing structures.
  • $\Delta T$ — temperature difference ( $T_{in} — T_{out}$ ).
  • $k$ — heat transfer coefficient of the structure.

How to interpret the result?

After entering the data, the calculator will give you several numbers:

  1. Thermal power (kW): This is the power your boiler or heater should produce during the coldest days of the year.
  2. Number of sections: Approximate calculation for standard bimetallic or aluminum radiators (with heat transfer ~150 W/section).
  3. Consumption per season: Forecast of energy consumption (in kWh) for the heating period. By multiplying this number by your electricity or gas tariff, you will find out the approximate cost of heating.

Advice: Always provide a power reserve (usually 15-20%), since the actual quality of construction may differ from the design, and frosts may be stronger than the average. In our calculator you can configure this parameter manually.
