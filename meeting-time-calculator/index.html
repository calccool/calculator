Time difference calculator for scheduling calls

In the context of globalization and remote work, the main problem is synchronizing schedules. When people in London are just drinking their morning coffee, the working day in Singapore is already over. Our calculator is designed specifically to help you instantly find Overlap (intersection window) — a time when all participants are in good working spirit, and not woken up by a bell in the middle of the night.

The tool doesn’t just subtract the hours, it visualizes the “daylight hours” of both parties, allowing you to clearly see how comfortable the selected time is for your interlocutor.

How does the mathematical calculation model work?

To calculate the exact time on the recipient's side, an algorithm is used that takes into account the offset relative to Coordinated Universal Time (UTC). The formula looks like this:

$$T_{target} = (T_{base} + (UTC_{target} — UTC_{base})) \pmod{24}$$

Where:

  • $T_{target}$ — total time of the interlocutor;
  • $T_{base}$ - local time you set;
  • $UTC_{target}$ — recipient time zone;
  • $UTC_{base}$ is your time zone.

Important: If the final value is negative, the system automatically makes a “cyclic shift” by $+24$ hours, and if it exceeds 24, it subtracts them so that you always see the correct time of the current or next day.

Visualization of daylight synchronization

A special feature of this calculator is its interactive Timeline. It is painted in a gradient that imitates the position of the sun:

  1. Dark blue zones: Deep night (sleep).
  2. Blue Zones: Morning or evening (personal time).
  3. Yellow middle: Active daylight hours.

When calculating, the system automatically issues one of three synchronization statuses:

  • Perfect: Both participants are in the interval from 09:00 to 18:00.
  • Compromise: The time is outside of working hours, but falls within the waking period (06:00 - 22:00).
  • Bad time: One of the participants is forced to get in touch at night.

Five tips for international planning

  1. Use rule 10-16: Try to schedule calls so that for both parties the time falls within the interval from 10:00 to 16:00. This is the golden window of productivity.
  2. Consider lunch breaks: In Europe and Asia, lunch times can vary greatly. This is usually 13:00 local time.
  3. Take care of Fridays: Don't schedule calls on Friday evenings for the party with time ahead - you're taking away people's personal weekends.
  4. Check for daylight saving time: Please be aware that the transition dates in the US and Europe are not the same, which could result in a 1 hour difference for several weeks.
  5. Copy the result: Use the “Copy” button in our calculator to immediately paste the final parameters (your time / your interlocutor’s time) into the description of an event in the calendar or a message in Telegram/Slack.
