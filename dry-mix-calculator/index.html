Dry mix consumption calculator: Screed, Sand concrete, Plaster and Self-leveling floor

When planning renovation work, be it pouring screed, leveling walls or laying tiles, one of the main problems is purchasing materials. An error in calculations leads to two scenarios:

  1. Lack of material: Stopping work, additional logistics costs and the risk of getting “color discrepancies” or cracks due to interruptions in pouring.
  2. Surplus: Loss of money and problem with disposal of heavy bags.

Flow calculation formula

The calculation algorithm is based on the geometric volume and bulk density of the mixture. For manual calculation you can use the following formulas:

$$M = S \times H \times R \times K$$

Where:

  • $M$ - final mass of dry mixture (kg);
  • $S$ - room area ( $м^2$ );
  • $H$ - average layer thickness (cm). Attention: the formula uses a value normalized to flow rate (usually per 1 cm or 1 mm);
  • $R$ - standard mixture consumption ( $кг/м^2$ with a thickness of 1 cm);
  • $K$ - safety factor (usually 1.05 - 1.10).

To find out the number of bags ( $N$ ), you need to divide the total weight by the weight of one package:

$$N = \lceil \frac{M}{W_{bag}} \rceil$$

Where $W_{bag}$ is the weight of one bag (kg), and the symbol $\lceil \dots \rceil$ means rounding up to the nearest whole number.

How to determine the average layer thickness?

This is the most difficult parameter when calculating screed or plaster, since the base is rarely perfectly level.

  1. Find the zero point: Using a laser level, determine the highest point of the floor or wall.
  2. Measure the differences: Take measurements at several points in the room (the more, the more accurate).
  3. Calculate the average:
    • For example, at one point the layer is 2 cm (minimum), at another because of the hole it is 6 cm.
    • Average thickness: $(2 + 6) / 2 = 4$ cm.
  4. It is the meaning 40 mm must be entered in the “Layer thickness” field.

Factors affecting consumption (Stock)

We recommend always pawning reserve 5-10% for the following reasons:

  • Irregularities in the base: Small potholes and cracks that are difficult to take into account when measuring the level.
  • Mixing losses: Part of the mixture remains on the walls of the container, mixer or rule.
  • Human factor: Scattering when opening the bag or packing.

Calculation of mixing water

Our calculator also shows the approximate volume of water required to prepare the solution. This is critical for planning work at sites where there is no central water supply and water has to be brought in.

💡 Expert advice: Never add more water than indicated in the instructions. Excess moisture leads to stratification of the solution, a decrease in brand strength and the appearance of shrinkage cracks during drying.

Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

Question: Is it possible to use sand concrete M-300 for plaster?

Answer: Theoretically yes, but it is difficult and ineffective. Sand concrete has a large fraction and slides off the walls. For walls, it is better to use specialized cement or gypsum plasters.

Question: What is the minimum layer for a self-leveling floor?

Answer: Depends on the brand. Thin-layer levelers can be cast from 2-3 mm. Basic levelers (coarse) require a layer of 10 mm. Always check the manufacturer's data sheet.

Question: How long does it take for a 5 cm thick screed to dry?

Answer: Complete drying of the cement-sand screed is calculated using the formula: 1 week per 1 cm of thickness (up to 4 cm), and 2 weeks for each subsequent centimeter. A layer of 5 cm will dry for about 6 weeks until it reaches full strength and moisture content acceptable for laying parquet.
