Room acoustic mode calculator: calculation of standing waves and Schröder frequency online

Every owner of a home studio or listening room is faced with the problem of “droning” bass. This phenomenon is caused standing waves (acoustic modes) - resonances that occur when the sound wavelength coincides with the physical dimensions of the room.

Our calculator allows you to instantly calculate problematic frequencies and determine Schröder frequency, above which the room stops dictating its terms to the sound.

Basic functions of the calculator

  • Calculation of axial modes: determination of the main resonances along three axes (length, width, height).
  • Temperature recording: sound speed correction for precise calculation accuracy.
  • Spectrum visualization: interactive map of frequencies from 20 to 200 Hz.
  • Harmonic analysis: calculation up to the 4th harmonic for each axis.

Mathematical base of calculations

The calculator is based on fundamental formulas of room acoustics.

1. Speed ​​of sound

The speed of sound is not a constant and directly depends on the air temperature ( $T$ ). We use the refined formula:

$$c = 331.3 + 0.606 \cdot T$$

Where $c$ is the speed of sound in m/s, and $T$ is the temperature in degrees Celsius.

2. Frequency of axial modes

To calculate the main resonances between parallel surfaces, the formula is used:

$$f_n = \frac{c \cdot n}{2L}$$

  • $f$ —mode frequency (Hz);
  • $c$ - speed of sound;
  • $L$ - distance between walls (m);
  • $n$ - harmonic number (1, 2, 3...).

3. Schroeder frequency ( $f_s$ )

This is the critical transition point, above which the density of modes becomes so high that they cease to cause individual peaks and troughs, merging into a common background.

$$f_s = 2000 \sqrt{\frac{RT_{60}}{V}}$$

Our calculator uses the $RT_{60} = 0.4$ sec reverberation time standard for prepared small rooms.

How to analyze results

When working with a visualization chart, pay attention to the following points:

  1. Line thickening: If the length, width and height markers are close to each other (the difference is less than 5 Hz), a powerful resonance will occur in this zone. This is a black hole for your mix.
  2. Low frequency threshold: The very first mode (usually along the longest wall) determines the physical limit of bass reproduction in your room.
  3. Pressure zones: In the corners of the room, all modes have maximum sound pressure. This is why corners are the best place to place Bass Traps.

Practical tips for improving acoustics

  • Avoid cubic shapes: In square rooms, fashions coincide along two axes, doubling the problem.
  • Rule 38%: Try not to place the sound engineer's chair in the center of the room or close to the wall. The point at 38% of the length of the room is often the most flat in terms of frequency response.
  • Acoustic treatment: Use high-density mineral wool absorbers to dampen corners and early reflection areas.
