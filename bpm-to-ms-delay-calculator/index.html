Music Latency Calculator: Converting BPM to Milliseconds and Hertz

In modern music production, precision is the key to clear and rhythmic sound. If the parameters Delay (delays) or Reverb (reverbs) are out of sync with the tempo of the project, the mix becomes muddy and rhythmic elements begin to conflict with each other.

Our calculator allows you to instantly change the tempo of a song ( BPM ) in milliseconds, which is critical for configuring hardware processors or VST plugins that do not have Sync built-in.

Why convert BPM to milliseconds?

Synchronizing effects across the grid allows you to achieve the effect of “breathing” the track. This is especially important for:

  • Pre-delay settings: Setting the correct reverb pre-delay allows you to separate the dry vocal signal from the space.
  • Sidechain and compression: Attack and Release timing according to tempo for perfect pumping.
  • LFO and modulation: frequency synchronization of filters and oscillators in Hertz (Hz).

Formulas for musical calculations

Musical mathematics is based on the duration of a quarter note. The calculation uses a standard formula based on the fact that there are 60,000 milliseconds in one minute.

$$T_{ms} = \frac{60000}{BPM}$$

Where $T_{ms}$ is the duration of the quarter note (1/4) in milliseconds. Based on this value, other types of delays are calculated:

  • Straight: standard share value.
  • Dotted: increases duration by 50%. Formula: $T \cdot 1.5$ .
  • Triplet: three notes in the space of two. Formula: $T \cdot 0.666$ .

To convert time to frequency (Hertz), the formula is used:

$$f_{Hz} = \frac{1000}{T_{ms}}$$

Delay and LFO Oscillator modes

This calculator supports two operating modes, covering all the needs of a sound engineer:

Conversion to milliseconds (Delay/Reverb)

This mode is designed for spatial effects. You get precise values ​​for adjusting the reverb tails and delay feedback. The use of dotted values ​​gives the rhythm a characteristic "jumping" effect, popular in Dub or progressive trance styles.

Conversion to Hertz (LFO Hz)

Required to configure modulation parameters. If you want your Auto-Pan or Wah-Wah effect to fire exactly once per measure or quarter note, use the values ​​in this table. This ensures that the phase of the effect always coincides with the downbeat.

How to use Tap Tempo

If you don't know the exact BPM of an audio recording, use the built-in button Tap Tempo. Just press it in time with the music (at least 4 times), and the algorithm will automatically determine the average tempo and calculate all the necessary delay parameters in real time.
