What is the QT and QTc interval?

On the electrocardiogram (ECG) interval QT is a measurement from the beginning of the Q wave to the end of the T wave. It reflects the total duration of electrical activity (depolarization and repolarization) of the ventricles of the heart.

The problem is that the actual QT interval is highly dependent on the heart rate (HR):

  • At high heart rate (tachycardia) QT interval shortens.
  • At low heart rate (bradycardia) QT interval lengthens.

To standardize this measurement and be able to compare it between different patients, it was introduced Corrected QT (QTc). QTc is a calculated value that indicates what the QT interval would be if the patient's heart rate were 60 beats per minute.

Why is QTc important?

Abnormal QTc is a critical marker of risk for life-threatening arrhythmias.

  • Long QTc (Long QT Syndrome, LQTS): The most common and dangerous pathology. It indicates slow repolarization and creates a high risk of developing torsade de pointes (TdP) ( Torsades de Pointes ), which can lead to sudden cardiac death.
  • Short QTc (Short QT Syndrome, SQTS): A rarer, but also dangerous condition associated with the risk of atrial and ventricular fibrillation.
