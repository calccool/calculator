Resistor Color Coding Calculator (4, 5 and 6 bands)

How to determine resistance by colored stripes?

Resistors are the most common components in electronics. Due to their small size, it is difficult to print numbers on the housing, so an international color coding system (IEC 60062 standard) was adopted.

Our online resistor calculator allows you to instantly decipher this code. You don't need to memorize a color chart or do mental calculations—just choose the colors you see on the body of the part.

Supported marking types

The tool works with all three encoding standards:

  1. 4 stripes (Standard accuracy): The most popular type. The first two stripes are numbers, the third is the multiplier, the fourth is the tolerance (error).
  2. 5 stripes (High precision): Used for precision resistors (tolerance up to 0.05%). The first three stripes are numbers, the fourth is the multiplier, the fifth is the tolerance.
  3. 6 bands (with temperature stability): Same as 5 bands, but adds a sixth ring indicating the Temperature Coefficient of Resistance (TCR or PPM).

Calculation formulas

The calculator uses the following algorithms to calculate the denomination ( $R$ ):

For 4 stripes:

$$R = (10 \cdot D_1 + D_2) \times M$$

For 5 and 6 stripes:

$$R = (100 \cdot D_1 + 10 \cdot D_2 + D_3) \times M$$

Where $D_{1,2,3}$ is the values ​​of the numbers corresponding to the color of the stripe, and $M$ is the multiplier.

Tips for use

  • Where to start reading? Usually the first stripe is located closer to one of the edges of the case. Also, the tolerance band (gold or silver) is often separated from the rest by a small gap and is located at the end.
  • What is PPM? This Parts Per Million (ppm). If a resistor has TKS = 50 ppm/°C, this means that when the temperature changes by 1 degree, its resistance will change by no more than 0.005%.
  • Visualization: Our calculator draws the resistor in real time. Compare the image on the screen with the part in your hands to ensure the correct color selection.
