Electromagnet Force Calculator: Calculation of Induction and Load Capacity

Electromagnets are used in everything from door locks and relays to powerful industrial cranes that lift tons of metal. However, designing even the simplest coil requires precise calculations. An error in choosing the wire cross-section or core material can lead to the magnet being too weak or, conversely, consuming an unreasonably large amount of energy.

Our electromagnet force calculator allows you to instantly determine the lifting force of a device based on its physical parameters. Unlike simple online tools, we take into account the magnetic permeability of the core, which is critical to get real results.

Basic calculation parameters

The strength of an electromagnet depends not only on the current, but also on the properties of the magnetic circuit. To get an accurate result, you will need to enter the following information:

  • Current (I): The current in amperes flowing through the winding.
  • Number of turns (N): The number of turns of wire in a coil. The more turns, the stronger the magnetic field at the same current.
  • Coil length (L): The length of the solenoid or the average length of the magnetic line in the core.
  • Sectional area (S): The area of ​​the end of the core through which the magnetic flux is closed (usually in m²).
  • Core material: The most important parameter. Air is a poor conductor of the magnetic field, while iron amplifies it thousands of times.

Calculation formulas

The calculation is carried out in two stages. First it is calculated magnetic induction (B) inside the solenoid:

$$B = \frac{\mu \cdot N \cdot I}{L}$$

Where:

  • $B$ - magnetic induction (Tesla).
  • $\mu$ - absolute magnetic permeability of the core ( $\mu = \mu_0 \cdot \mu_r$ ).
  • $\mu_0$ - magnetic constant ( $4\pi \cdot 10^{-7}$ ).
  • $\mu_r$ - relative magnetic permeability of the material.

Knowing induction, we can calculate force of attraction (F) assuming there is no air gap (perfect contact):

$$F = \frac{B^2 \cdot S}{2 \cdot \mu_0}$$

Important: Ferromagnetic materials have a saturation limit. For most steels the maximum induction is about 1.6–2.0 Tesla. If the calculator shows 5 or 10 T, this is a theoretical value; in reality, the force will stop increasing after reaching the saturation point.
