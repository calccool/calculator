The eye color of an unborn child is one of the most exciting mysteries for future parents. Will the baby look at the world with his mother's brown eyes or will he inherit his father's heavenly blue? Our online eye color calculator uses proven genetic algorithms to calculate the probability of inheritance of traits.

How do eye color genetics work?

Contrary to popular myth, eye color is not inherited by simply mixing the “colors” of parents. This is a complex biological process determined by the amount of melanin pigment in the iris and the work of several genes.

For calculations, we use a simplified but effective two-gene model that takes into account the interaction of two key pairs of genes on chromosomes 15 and 19:

  1. EYCL1 (Gey): Responsible for green and blue colors.
  2. EYCL3 (Bey2): Responsible for the brown color.

Dominant and recessive traits

To understand the calculator's prediction, it is important to know the basic rule of genetics: strong (dominant) genes suppress weak (recessive) genes.

  • 🟤 Brown color: The strongest dominant trait. If one of the parents has this gene, the likelihood of the child having brown eyes increases dramatically.
  • 🟢 Green color: Dominant over blue, but inferior to brown.
  • 🔵 Blue color: Recessive trait. For a child to have blue eyes, he must receive a “blue” gene from both parents and not a single “brown” gene.

Inheritance Formulas (Simplified Model)

For those who want to understand the mathematics of the process, we use Mendel's law of splitting. Let us denote alleles (gene variants) by letters: A - dominant (brown), a - recessive (light).

  1. Homozygous parents: If one parent has the genotype $AA$ (pure brown), and the second $aa$ (blue), then all children will receive the combination $Aa$ : $$P(A) = 100\%$$ Result: 100% of children with brown eyes (but carriers of the blue gene).
  2. Heterozygous parents (both brown-eyed, but carriers of the light gene): When crossing $Aa \times Aa$ , the following combinations are possible: $$AA (25\%) + 2Aa (50\%) + aa (25\%)$$ Result: 75% probability of brown eyes ( $AA$ and $Aa$ ), 25% probability of light eyes ( $aa$ ).

Why is the result not 100% accurate?

Our calculator gives the most likely forecast. However, human genetics is more complicated than school textbooks:

  • Polygenicity: Eye color is influenced not by 2, but by more than 6 genes (including HERC2, OCA2, SLC24A4 etc.).
  • Mutations: Spontaneous genetic changes can lead to unexpected results.
  • Albinism and Heterochromia: Rare genetic characteristics in which pigment is absent or unevenly distributed (eyes of different colors).
  • Changes with age: Most babies are born with light blue eyes. Permanent color is established by the age of 3, when melanocytes accumulate a sufficient amount of pigment.
