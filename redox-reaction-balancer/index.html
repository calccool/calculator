Calculator for equalizing redox reactions (ORR) online

This tool is designed to automatically select coefficients in chemical equations where the oxidation states of atoms change. Unlike simple stoichiometric calculators, our algorithm takes into account not only the law of conservation of mass, but also law of conservation of electric charge, which is critical for ionic equations and the half-reaction method.

How to use the calculator

To obtain the correct result, it is important to follow the format for recording chemical formulas and charges.

  1. Substance input: Enter reactants on the left side and products on the right side, separating them with an equal sign=.
  2. Separation: Be sure to put a sign between individual substances+.
  3. Charges and ions: To indicate the charge of an ion, use the symbol^. For example, the iron(II) cation is written asFe^2+, and the permanganate anion is asMnO4^-.
  4. Electrons: If you need to equalize a half-reaction, you can use the electron, writing it ase^-.

Operating principle and theoretical basis

Balancing redox reactions is based on two fundamental laws of physical chemistry, which this calculator checks simultaneously.

1. Law of conservation of mass (Mass Balance)

The number of atoms of each element on the left side of the equation (reactants) must be equal to the number of atoms on the right side (products). If we denote the number of atoms of the element $E$ in the $j$ molecule as $a_{E,j}$ , and the stoichiometric coefficient as $x_j$ , then the condition looks like this:

$$\sum_{j \in Reactants} a_{E,j} \cdot x_j = \sum_{k \in Products} a_{E,k} \cdot x_k$$

2. Law of conservation of charge (Charge Balance)

In ORR, electrons are transferred from the reducing agent to the oxidizing agent. The total electric charge on the left side of the equation must be equal to the total charge on the right side. This is especially important for ionic equations.

$$\sum (Charge_{reactants}) = \sum (Charge_{products})$$

The calculator uses the algebraic balance method (Gaussian matrix method), which reduces a chemical problem to a system of linear equations. This allows you to find coefficients even for the most complex reactions that are difficult to balance manually using the electronic balance method.
