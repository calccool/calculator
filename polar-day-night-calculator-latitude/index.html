Polar day and night length calculator by latitude

Polar day and polar night are unique astronomical phenomena characteristic of the high latitudes of the Earth. Their occurrence is due to the inclination of the axis of rotation of our planet relative to the ecliptic plane. This calculator allows you to instantly determine the annual light cycle for any point in the world using modern celestial mechanics algorithms.

Key features of the calculator

When calculating, parameters are used that make the results as close to reality as possible:

  • Geographic latitude: Enter coordinates in decimal format for perfect accuracy.
  • Hemisphere selection: Correct operation for both northern latitudes (Murmansk, Norilsk) and southern latitudes (Antarctic stations).
  • Accounting for atmospheric refraction: Amendment to 0.83°, which “raises” the Sun above the horizon, increasing the actual length of the day.
  • Calculation of white nights: Determination of the period of civil twilight, when the Sun descends below the horizon by no more than -6°.

Mathematical calculation model

The calculator is based on calculating the declination of the Sun ( $\delta$ ) for each day of the year using the formula:

$$\delta = 23.44^\circ \cdot \sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{365} \cdot (d — 81)\right)$$

Where:

  • $d$ — serial number of the day in the year;
  • $23.44^\circ$ - tilt of the earth's axis.

After this, the algorithm determines the height of the sun at the moments of the upper and lower culmination. If the lower culmination taking into account refraction ( $+0.83^\circ$ ) remains above $0^\circ$ , it is fixed polar day. If the upper climax remains below $0^\circ$ - polar night.

Why is the polar day longer than the polar night?

Many users notice that at the same latitude, the number of days of the polar day exceeds the number of days of the polar night. This is not a calculation error, but a consequence of two factors:

  1. Atmospheric refraction: The air refracts the sun's rays, allowing us to see the disk of the Sun when it is actually already below the horizon.
  2. Angular diameter of the Sun: We count the beginning of the day at the top edge of the disk, not at its center.

Instructions for use

To obtain accurate data, follow these steps:

  • Enter the latitude of the place you are interested in (for example, 68.97 for Murmansk).
  • Select the desired hemisphere in the mode switch.
  • Use the toggle switch "White Nights", if you want to know the period when evening twilight turns into morning twilight without the onset of darkness.
  • Click the button "Calculate" to construct a pie chart of the annual cycle.

This tool will be useful for travelers, photographers planning to shoot during “off-peak hours,” as well as residents of northern regions to understand their light conditions.
