Wilks Score Calculator: Online Wilks Score Calculator

In powerlifting, strength is not always measured by the absolute weight on the bar. An athlete weighing 60 kg who lifted a total of 400 kg is objectively stronger than an athlete weighing 100 kg with the same amount. To fairly compare athletes of different weight categories and even different genders, it is used Wilks Coefficient.

Why is the Wilks coefficient needed?

The Wilks system was developed by Robert Wilks, CEO of Powerlifting Australia, in 1994. For a long time it remained the “gold standard” in the IPF (International Powerlifting Federation) for identifying the absolute champion.

Main functions of the coefficient:

  • Comparison of athletes: Allows you to determine the overall winner among all weight categories.
  • Comparison of men and women: The formula takes into account physiological differences, allowing results to be compared between genders.
  • Progress tracking: Even if your body weight increases, the ratio will show whether your relative strength is increasing.

Calculation formula (The Wilks Formula)

Mathematically, the Wilks coefficient is a 5th degree polynomial. Manual calculation is extremely complex and error-prone, so it is recommended to use our automatic tool. However, to understand the mechanics, the formula is as follows:

$$Coeff = \frac{500}{a + bx + cx^2 + dx^3 + ex^4 + fx^5}$$

Where:

  • x — the athlete’s body weight in kilograms.
  • 500 — constant adopted for normalization.
  • Coefficients (a-f) — variables depending on the gender of the athlete.

$$Wilks Score = Total Weight \times Coeff$$

How is Wilks different from IPF GL and DOTS?

Although Wilkes' formula is the most famous, alternatives have emerged in recent years:

  • IPF GL Points: Modern standard of the International Powerlifting Federation (introduced in 2019). It better takes into account the statistics of the equipment and classic divisions.
  • DOTS: The German system, which is considered more objective for very heavy and very light weight categories.

However, most amateur competitions, commercial venues and historical record comparisons still use the good old Wilks.
