The Mathematics of Audio Compression and Normalization

Before encoding to MP3, you can calculate the approximate size of the resulting file. In digital audio, file size directly depends on the bitrate (the number of bits transmitted per second) and the duration of the track. The formula for calculating the $S$ file size (in megabytes) is:

$$S = \frac{B \times T}{8388608}$$

Where $B$ is the selected bitrate (for example, $320000$ bps), and $T$ is the audio duration in seconds. The $8388608$ divider is used to convert bits to megabytes.

Advanced Processing: Peak Normalization

Often original FLAC files (especially old vinyl digitizations) have too quiet sound. Our converter has a function to normalize the peak to a safe limit $-1 \text{ dBFS}$ (decibel full scale). This prevents distortion (clipping) when played back on digital devices.

The algorithm scans all audio samples to find the maximum absolute peak $P_{max}$ . The $M$ gain multiplier is calculated using the following formula:

$$M = \frac{10^{-1/20}}{|P_{max}|}$$

Each sample is then multiplied by a factor of $M$ , making the track as loud as possible, but technically clear and safe for speakers.
