Sugar mash calculator: calculation of hydromodule and alcohol yield

Proper preparation of wort is the foundation of a quality drink. Even a small error in proportions can lead to “badness” due to excessive concentration of sugar or to unnecessary energy expenditure when distilling too “liquid” mash. This calculator allows you to instantly calculate all critical indicators: from the required volume of water to the theoretical yield of absolute alcohol (AS).

The interface supports two operating modes:

  1. Sugar calculation: if you already have a fixed amount of raw materials.
  2. Capacity calculation: if you need to fill the fermentation tank to a certain volume (for example, 80%) to avoid foam splashing out.

What is a hydraulic module and which one to choose?

Hydromodule (HM) - This is the ratio of the mass of sugar to the volume of water used. Our calculator implements three standard presets that are most often used in home distilling:

  • 1:3 (Turbo): High concentration. Suitable only for specialized alcoholic “turbo yeast”, capable of surviving at mash strength of up to 17–20%.
  • 1:4 (Standard): Golden mean. Optimal for most alcoholic yeasts. Provides confident fermentation and strength of about 14–15%.
  • 1:5 (Bread): Light mode. Ideal for regular baker's yeast that is sensitive to high alcohol content.

Formulas and mathematical model of the process

Calculations in the calculator are based on the physical properties of solutions. It is important to consider that when dissolved, sugar increases the total volume of liquid. The volume expansion factor for sugar is approximately 0.6 liters per 1 kg.

The following formulas are used to determine the total values:

Calculation of the total wort volume

The total volume of mash ( $V_{total}$ ) is the sum of the volume of water ( $V_{water}$ ) and the volume occupied by dissolved sugar ( $V_{sugar\_vol}$ ):

$$V_{total} = V_{water} + (M_{sugar} \times 0.6)$$

Where $M_{sugar}$ is the mass of sugar in kilograms.

Determination of strength (ABV)

The potential alcohol content in the mash after fermentation is complete is calculated based on the yield of absolute alcohol (theoretical maximum - 0.6 l with 1 kg of sugar):

$$ABV (\%) = \frac{M_{sugar} \times 0.6}{V_{total}} \times 100$$

Expected yield of raw alcohol

For practical distillation planning, we calculate the volume of raw alcohol with a strength of 40%:

$$V_{40\%} = \frac{V_{as}}{0.4}$$

Advanced settings and visualization

For experienced users there is a block "Theoretical coefficients". You can adjust it:

  • Volume of 1 kg of sugar in solution: default is 0.6 l, but may vary slightly depending on the purity of the raw materials.
  • Yield of AC with 1 kg of sugar: The standard value of 0.6 l takes into account ideal conditions. In practice, due to impurities and the work of yeast, this value may be slightly lower (0.58–0.59 l).

Built-in unit "The fermentation process" clearly demonstrates the filling of your container. The blue area shows the water level, and the textured bottom part shows the volume that the sugar “occupies.” This allows you to visually assess the amount of free space under the foam cap.

How to get the perfect mash

To ensure that the calculation results coincide with reality, follow the basic rules:

  1. Temperature: Maintain temperature between 22-28°C. Overheating above 35°C can kill the yeast.
  2. Water quality: Use bottled or distilled tap water. Boiled water is deprived of the oxygen that yeast needs initially.
  3. Aeration: Before adding yeast, stir the wort vigorously to saturate it with oxygen.
  4. Feeding: For sugar mash, it is advisable to use special nutrient salts, since pure sugar does not contain the necessary microelements for the life of yeast.
