Heating radiator sections calculator: Accurate calculation by area and volume

How to calculate the number of radiator sections?

Correct calculation of the heating system is the key to comfort in winter. If there are few sections, the room will be cold; if there are too many, you will overpay for the equipment and suffer from dry air. Standard formula “1 section per 2 sq. meters" is outdated, as it does not take into account the height of the ceilings, insulation of the walls and type of windows.

Our online radiator calculator uses an advanced method of thermal engineering calculation, taking into account all heat loss coefficients ( $k$ ).

Basic power calculation formula

For standard residential premises with ceiling heights up to 2.7 meters, the basic norm is 100 W of thermal power per 1 m². However, to obtain an accurate result, it is necessary to apply correction factors.

Formula for calculating the required thermal power ( $Q_{total}$ ):

$$Q_{total} = S \cdot 100 \cdot k_1 \cdot k_2 \cdot k_3 \cdot k_4 \cdot k_5 \cdot k_6 \cdot k_7$$

Where:

  • $S$ - room area (length $\times$ width).
  • $100$ - specific power standard (W/m²).
  • $k_1 \dots k_7$ - correction factors (described below).

The total number of sections ( $N$ ) is calculated by dividing the total power by the heat transfer of one section ( $P_{sect}$ ):

$$N = \frac{Q_{total}}{P_{sect}}$$

The power of one section depends on the material and the center distance (usually 500 mm or 350 mm). In the calculator we use average values ​​for standard models (500 mm), but you can indicate your passport data.

Factors affecting the calculation

Our algorithm takes into account the following important parameters, which are often ignored in “quick” calculations:

  1. Ceiling height ( $k_h$ ). If the ceilings are higher than 2.7 m, the volume of air in the room is larger and it is more difficult to warm it up. We introduce a multiplying factor.
  2. Number of external walls ( $k_1$ ).
    • One wall - $k=1.1$
    • Two walls (corner room) – $k=1.2$
    • Three or more - $k=1.3+$
  3. Windows and glazing ( $k_2$ ). Conventional wooden frames lose much more heat ( $k=1.27$ ) than modern triple glazing ( $k=0.85$ ).
  4. Connection diagram. The method of piping affects the efficiency of the battery:
    • Diagonal: Standard (100% efficiency).
    • Lateral: Standard connection (loss ~2-5%).
    • Lower (saddle): The least efficient, power losses can reach 12-15%.
  5. The presence of a screen or niche. If the radiator is hidden behind a decorative screen or installed deep in a niche, convection is disrupted and you lose up to 20% of heat.

How to use the calculator?

  1. Enter room dimensions (length and width).
  2. Specify heat loss parameters: number of external walls, type of windows and degree of insulation of the house.
  3. Select climate zone (minimum temperature in winter).
  4. Select radiator type from the list. The “Section power” field will be filled in automatically, but you can adjust it according to the passport of your product.
  5. Specify installation features (connection diagram and presence of screen).
  6. Click "Calculate sections".

Expert advice: Always round up the resulting number of sections. It is also recommended to add power reserve 10-15% (in the calculator this is the “Power reserve” field) in case of extreme frosts or a decrease in the temperature of the coolant in the housing and communal services system.
