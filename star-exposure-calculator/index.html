Night Sky Shutter Calculator: Rule of 500, 400 and 600

Astrophotography is the art of balancing the accumulation of light with the physical rotation of our planet. Because the Earth constantly rotates on its axis, the stars in the sky appear to be moving objects. If you set your shutter speed too slow, you'll end up with "star tracks" (blurred lines) instead of clear, pinpoint stars.

Our calculator automates complex calculations, allowing you to focus on composition. It supports not only classic Rule 500, but also more modern algorithms for high-resolution cameras.

Basic calculation rules

The choice of rule depends on how demanding you are about sharpness at 100% magnification of the image:

  • Rule 400: The most stringent standard. Recommended for modern multi-megapixel matrices, where even the slightest shift is noticeable at the pixel level.
  • Rule 500: Universal "gold standard". Provides excellent results for social media sharing and medium format printing.
  • Rule 600: Allows slight elongation of stars. Useful in extremely low light conditions where getting light is more important than getting perfect spots.

Formula for calculating shutter speed

The mathematical calculation model is based on the equivalent focal length (EFL). The formula looks like this:

$$t = \frac{R}{f \cdot C}$$

Where:

  • $t$ - maximum exposure time in seconds;
  • $R$ — constant of the selected rule (400, 500 or 600);
  • $f$ - actual focal length of the lens (mm);
  • $C$ is the crop factor of your camera sensor.

Advanced Mode: Milky Way Exposure

In the advanced mode of our calculator you can calculate not only time, but also recommended ISO. This is critical for imaging the Milky Way, where the overall brightness of the sky is often insufficient.

The algorithm analyzes your aperture ( $f/$ aperture) and calculates the required sensitivity to achieve a basic exposure in deep darkness (EV -6).

How to use the visualizer

The built-in simulator shows in real time what kind of “trail” the star will leave under the selected parameters.

  • Gathering Light: Shows the effectiveness of your optics. The wider the aperture and the longer the shutter speed, the higher this figure.
  • Noise level: The indicator will warn you if the calculated ISO is too high to obtain a clean frame.

Tips for the perfect shot

  1. Use a tripod: Any camera movement will completely ruin the calculated sharpness.
  2. Manual focus: Aim at the brightest star in Live View at maximum magnification.
  3. RAW format: Shoot only in uncompressed format so you can pull out shadow detail in post-processing.
  4. Delayed start: Use a 2-second timer or remote control to eliminate vibration from pressing the shutter button.
