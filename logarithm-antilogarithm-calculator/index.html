The concept of logarithm and its purpose

Logarithm is a fundamental mathematical operation, the inverse exponentiation. In simple terms, the logarithm answers the question: “To what power must the base $b$ be raised to get the number $x$ ?”

Formally, this is written as:

$$\log_b(x) = y \quad \iff \quad b^y = x$$

  • $b$ - This base logarithm ( $b > 0$ and $b \neq 1$ ).
  • $x$ - This argument or a logarithmic number ( $x > 0$ ).
  • $y$ - This meaning logarithm (exponent).

Basic properties and rules of logarithms

To successfully work with logarithms, you need to know their key properties. These rules are used to simplify expressions and form the basis for how the calculator works.

  1. Logarithm of the product: $$\log_b(x \cdot z) = \log_b(x) + \log_b(z)$$
  2. Logarithm of quotient (division): $$\log_b(\frac{x}{z}) = \log_b(x) — \log_b(z)$$
  3. Logarithm of degree: $$\log_b(x^k) = k \cdot \log_b(x)$$
  4. Formula for moving to a new base: This formula is critical because it allows you to calculate a logarithm with an arbitrary base $b$ through known natural or decimal logarithms: $$\log_b(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(b)} = \frac{\lg(x)}{\lg(b)}$$

Antilogarithm: inverse operation

Antilog is the inverse function of the logarithm. Calculating the antilogarithm is essentially raising the base to a power, equal to a given number.

If $y$ is a logarithm value, then the antilogarithm of $y$ to base $b$ is:

$$\text{antilog}_b(y) = b^y$$

For example, if $\log_{10}(x) = 3$ , then the antilogarithm of 3 to base 10 is $10^3 = 1000$ .

Limitations of the logarithmic function

To correctly calculate the logarithm, you must remember the strict mathematical restrictions that our calculator automatically checks:

  • Argument ( $x$ ): The logarithm argument must be strictly positive ( $x > 0$ ). The logarithm of zero or a negative number has no meaning in the realm of real numbers.
  • Base ( $b$ ): The base must be positive ( $b > 0$ ) and not equal to one ($b \neq 1$).
    • If $b=1$ , then $1^y$ is always equal to 1, and the equation $1^y = x$ has a solution only at $x=1$ .
    • If $b \le 0$ , the function behaves irregularly and is not a logarithm.
