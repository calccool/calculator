Catch-McArdle Calculator: The Most Accurate BMR Calculation Based on Lean Body Mass

Why is the Katch-McArdle formula considered the most accurate?

Most online calculators use equations (such as Mifflin-St. George or Harris-Benedict) that rely only on overall weight, height, and age. However, these parameters can be deceiving. Two people weighing 90 kg can have completely different energy needs: one is a professional athlete with a mountain of muscles, the other is an obese person.

Katch-McArdle formula is the only popular method for calculating BMR (basal metabolic rate) that ignores age and gender, but takes into account body fat percentage.

How does this work?

Metabolism is determined not by total weight, but by Lean Body Mass (LBM - Lean Body Mass). Muscle burns calories even at rest, while fat tissue is metabolically inert.

The formula looks like this:

$$BMR = 370 + (21.6 \times LBM)$$

Where:

  • BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) - the amount of energy required by the body to maintain life at rest.
  • LBM (Lean Body Mass) - body weight in kg excluding fat.

From BMR to TDEE: Calculating calories for life

BMR is “lounging on the couch” calories. To find out the real daily requirement ( TDEE ), our calculator multiplies BMR by the activity factor:

  • 1.2 — Sedentary lifestyle (office work, no sports).
  • 1.375 — Light activity (workouts 1-3 times a week).
  • 1.55 — Moderate activity (3-5 times a week).
  • 1.725 — High activity (6-7 times a week).
  • 1.9 — Extreme activity (heavy physical work or double training).
