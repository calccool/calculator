Binomial Coefficients Calculator (C of n to k): Combinations and Permutations

In combinatorics and probability theory, a problem often arises: in how many ways can a certain number of elements be selected from a set, if the order of choice is not important? The answer to this question is given by binomial coefficient, which is often called the “number of combinations” or “C of n by k” (n choose k).

Our calculator does this job instantly, calculating not only classic combinations, but also permutations, and also showing the corresponding line of Pascal's triangle.

What is Binomial Coefficient?

The binomial coefficient is an integer indicating how many ways there are to select $k$ objects from a set of $n$ different objects.

The key point here is the order is not important.

  • If you choose 3 fruits for a salad out of 10 available, this is combination (apple + banana + pear is the same salad as pear + banana + apple).
  • If you match 3 digits for the lock code, this is rearrangement (code 1-2-3 and 3-2-1 are different codes).

Calculation formula

Mathematically, the binomial coefficient is denoted as $C(n, k)$ or $\binom{n}{k}$ and is calculated through factorials:

$$C(n, k) = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$$

Where:

  • n — total number of elements.
  • k — the number of elements to select.
  • ! — factorial (the product of all natural numbers from 1 to number n).

Combinations vs Permutations: What's the Difference?

Our calculator outputs two values: $C$ (Combinations) and $P$ (Permutations). Understanding the difference is critical to solving problems.

Relation to Pascal's Triangle

Binomial coefficients are directly related to the famous Pascal's Triangle.

  1. The line number of the triangle corresponds to the number n (starting from 0).
  2. The position of the number in the line corresponds to the number k.

For example, if you enter $n=5$ into the calculator, it will show you the 5th row of Pascal's triangle:1 5 10 10 5 1. Each of these numbers is the answer to the question “how many ways to choose 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 or 5 elements out of 5.”

Examples of use in life

Why is this needed, besides math lessons?

  • Lotteries: What is the chance of winning the 6/45 jackpot? We need to calculate $C(45, 6)$ . The result is 8,145,060 combinations. The chance is 1 in 8 million.
  • Poker: How many unique 5-card hands are there in a 52-card deck? We use $C(52, 5)$ . Answer: 2,598,960 options.
  • Business: In how many ways can you form a team of 3 employees if there are 10 people in the department? $C(10, 3) = 120$ options.

Properties of binomial coefficients

  1. Symmetry: Choosing 2 elements out of 10 is the same as not choosing 8 elements out of 10. $C(n, k) = C(n, n-k)$
  2. Boundary conditions: There is only one way to select 0 elements or all $n$ elements. $C(n, 0) = 1$ and $C(n, n) = 1$
