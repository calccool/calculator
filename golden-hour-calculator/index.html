Golden Hour Calculator: Calculating the Ideal Time for Photography

In photography the term "Golden Hour" Golden Hour describes the short period of time just after sunrise or just before sunset. At this time, the sun is low above the horizon, which creates a specific lighting that cannot be simulated in the studio.

The main advantages of this period:

  • Soft light: Direct sunlight passes through a thicker layer of the atmosphere, becoming scattered.
  • Warm shades: The light acquires a characteristic golden, amber or reddish tint.
  • Long shadows: The low position of the sun emphasizes the texture of objects and adds depth and dimension to photos.

How the calculation algorithm works

Our calculator uses a mathematical model of light phase distribution based on the timing of astronomical sunrise and sunset. Time calculation for each phase is made using the following formulas:

Phase calculation formulas

Let $T_{sr}$ be the sunrise time, and $T_{ss}$ the sunset time in minutes from the beginning of the day.

  1. Morning Blue Hour: $$[T_{sr} — 30, T_{sr}]$$
  2. Morning Golden Hour: $$[T_{sr}, T_{sr} + 60]$$
  3. Evening Golden Hour: $$[T_{ss} — 60, T_{ss}]$$
  4. Evening Blue Hour: $$[T_{ss}, T_{ss} + 30]$$

These intervals are the generally accepted standard in professional settings, although the actual duration may vary slightly depending on latitude and time of year.

Practical tips for successful shooting

To get the most out of using the calculator, follow these guidelines:

  • Arrive early: The golden hour does not always last exactly 60 minutes. In northern latitudes it can stretch, and at the equator it can fly by in a matter of moments.
  • Watch for cloud cover: Thick clouds can completely block the gold, but light clouds act like a huge natural softbox.
  • Use Blue Hour for a city: This is the best time to photograph architecture, when the brightness of artificial lighting on buildings matches the brightness of the darkening sky.
  • Exposure: During Golden Hour it is easy to overexpose the highlights. Shoot in RAW with slight underexposure to preserve detail in the sky.

Seasonal features

The calculator provides presets for different seasons. This is necessary, since the angle of inclination of the earth’s axis affects the length of daylight hours:

  1. Summer: The longest day, hard light at midday is longest.
  2. Winter: The sun never rises high, making the shadows long even in the middle of the day.
  3. Equator: The sun falls almost vertically below the horizon, making the transition from day to night rapid.
