Sum of squares calculator for consecutive numbers (1 to n)

Sum of consecutive squares is a classic problem from number theory and algebra, which is often found in statistics (when calculating variance) and physics (moments of inertia). Our calculator allows you to instantly find the sum of squares of natural numbers not only from 1 to $n$ , but also for any arbitrary range.

Sum of squares formula

To calculate the sum of squares of the first $n$ natural numbers, it is not necessary to add them manually. There is a proven formula (known as the square pyramid formula):

$$S_n = \frac{n(n + 1)(2n + 1)}{6}$$

Where:

  • $S_n$ — the required sum of the series $1^2 + 2^2 + 3^2 + \dots + n^2$ ;
  • $n$ is the final number of the sequence.

How to calculate the amount in a range?

If you need to find the sum of squares not from one, but, for example, from 5 to 10 (that is, $5^2 + 6^2 + \dots + 10^2$ ), our calculator uses the difference method:

$$S_{(a \dots b)} = S_b — S_{a-1}$$

We take the total sum from 1 to the end of the range ( $b$ ) and subtract from it the sum from 1 to the number before the start ( $a-1$ ).

Geometric Meaning: Pyramid Numbers

Why is the result of this sum called a square pyramidal number?

Imagine that you have balls. If you lay out a $3 \times 3$ square (9 balls), put a $2 \times 2$ square (4 balls) on top of it, and 1 ball on the very top, you get a pyramid.

The total number of balls in such a pyramid of height $n$ layers will be equal to the sum of squares from 1 to $n$ .

Where is this applied?

  1. Mathematical statistics: When calculating the standard deviation and sample variance, the sum of squared deviations is used.
  2. Crystallography: When calculating the packing of atoms in crystal lattices.
  3. Analysis of algorithms: To evaluate the complexity of nested loops in programming.
