Hyperglycemia Sodium Correction Calculator (Katz and Hillier Formulas)

Correction of sodium levels - A critical step in the diagnosis and treatment of patients with severe hyperglycemia (eg, diabetic ketoacidosis or hyperosmolar state). High concentrations of glucose in the blood create an osmotic gradient that draws water from the cells into the extracellular space. This results in blood dilution and falsely low sodium levels in laboratory tests.

Our calculator allows you to instantly calculate “corrected” (true) sodium using recognized international methods, which helps to avoid medical errors during infusion therapy.

Why is sodium adjustment needed?

As glucose levels rise, serum sodium decreases by approximately 1.6–2.4 mEq/L for every 100 mg/dl (5.6 mmol/l) glucose above normal. This condition is called translocation hyponatremia.

If the doctor focuses only on the laboratory (low) value and begins aggressive sodium replacement, this can lead to the development of dangerous hypernatremia after normalization of sugar levels, which can lead to cerebral edema or demyelination.

Calculation formulas used

In medical practice, two basic equations are used to calculate adjusted sodium ( $Na_{corr}$ ).

1. Classic Katz formula (Katz, 1973)

This is the most common formula and assumes a sodium reduction of 1.6 mEq/L.

$$Na_{corr} = Na_{meas} + 0.016 \times (Glu — 100)$$

2. Hillier's formula (Hillier, 1999)

Modern research shows that the Katz formula may underestimate the degree of correction at extremely high sugar levels. Hiller's formula uses a factor of 2.4, which gives a more accurate result.

$$Na_{corr} = Na_{meas} + 0.024 \times (Glu — 100)$$

Where:

  • $Na_{meas}$ is the measured sodium level (mEq/L or mmol/L).
  • $Glu$ - glucose level (mg/dL).
