Clinical Dehydration Score (CDS) Calculator: Rehydration Assessment and Calculation

What is the CDS (Clinical Dehydration Scale) scale?

Clinical Dehydration Score (CDS) is a validated clinical tool developed by Drs. Friedman and Goldman to rapidly assess the severity of dehydration in children 1 month to 3 years of age with acute gastroenteritis.

Unlike subjective scores, the CDS uses 4 specific clinical signs, assigning a score of 0 to 2 to each. The sum of points allows doctors and parents to decide whether the child can be fed at home or whether urgent hospitalization for intravenous infusion is required.

Interpretation of results

The Score correlates with the percentage of body weight loss:

  • 0 points: No dehydration (<3% weight loss). Maintenance therapy is required.
  • 1–4 points: Mild to moderate dehydration (3–6% weight loss). Oral rehydration is indicated.
  • 5–8 points: Severe dehydration (>6% weight loss). High risk of shock, immediate medical attention required.

Calculation of fluid volume for rehydration

The main goal of treatment is to replenish the deficiency of fluid and salts (electrolytes). The volume of required fluid ( $V_{total}$ ) is calculated based on the child’s weight and the degree of deficiency.

Formula for calculating deficit

For moderate dehydration (1-4 points), it is usually necessary to replenish 50 ml/kg, for severe dehydration - up to 100 ml/kg in the first 4 hours.

$$V_{rehydrate} = M \times K_{deficit}$$

Where:

  • $V_{rehydrate}$ - volume of solution for desoldering (ml).
  • $M$ — patient’s body weight (kg).
  • $K_{deficit}$ - deficit coefficient (50 or 100).

Maintenance Formula

In addition to replenishing already lost fluid, it is necessary to compensate for current losses (after each episode of vomiting or loose stools):

$$V_{maintenance} \approx 10 \text{ мл} \times M$$

Recommendations for desoldering

  1. What to drink? Use special glucose-saline solutions (GSR), available in pharmacies. Water, juices or soda do not contain the necessary salts and may make diarrhea worse.
  2. How to drink? Fractionally! A large volume of liquid provokes vomiting.
    • Give 1 teaspoon (5 ml) every 2 to 5 minutes.
    • If your child vomits, pause for 10-15 minutes and continue more slowly.
  3. When to call an ambulance?
    • The child cannot drink (lethargic, unconscious).
    • Uncontrollable vomiting (fountain).
    • No urination for more than 6-8 hours.
    • Blood in stool or vomit.

Important: This calculator is a reference tool. If you have any doubts about your child's condition, contact your pediatrician immediately. Severe dehydration develops rapidly in infants.
