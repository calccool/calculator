RAM latency calculator: converting CAS Latency to nanoseconds

When choosing RAM, users often make the mistake of paying attention only to the effective frequency (MT/s or MHz). However, the performance of the memory subsystem is determined by two key factors: throughput (data transfer speed) and latency (response time).

CAS Latency (CL) - this is the number of clock cycles that elapse from the moment the processor requests data until the moment it is issued by the memory module. Since the duration of one cycle directly depends on the frequency, the CL number itself is not an absolute indicator. Our calculator allows you to translate these "abstract" clock cycles into concrete nanoseconds ( $ns$ ) so that you can objectively compare different modules, for example, DDR4 3200 CL16 and DDR5 6000 CL30.

Formula for calculating delay in nanoseconds

To calculate the absolute response time of the first word (First Word Latency), the following mathematical relationship is used:

$$L_{ns} = \frac{CL \times 2000}{f_{eff}}$$

Where:

  • $L_{ns}$ — final delay in nanoseconds;
  • $CL$ — CAS Latency value in clock cycles;
  • $f_{eff}$ — effective memory frequency (in MT/s or effective MHz).

When calculating, it is important to remember that the actual bus clock frequency is two times lower than the effective one (for Double Data Rate memory). The calculator automatically takes this factor into account when processing the entered data.

Interpretation of results: what is considered a good indicator

The result of the calculations allows you to classify your RAM into one of the performance categories. This is critical for gaming systems, where low latency directly affects the stability of 0.1% and 1% Low FPS.

Advanced analysis: subtimings and throughput

In addition to the main CL parameter, professional memory analysis requires taking into account secondary timings. Our tool supports “Full Mode” for detailed analysis:

  • tRCD (Row Column Delay): Delay between row activation and data column access.
  • tRP (Row Precharge): The time it takes to close one line and open another.
  • tRAS (Row Active Time): The minimum time a row should remain open.

Theoretical Bandwidth

Latency determines the responsiveness of the system, while throughput determines the amount of data transferred per second. You can calculate this parameter by selecting the channel mode (Single, Dual or Quad Channel). Calculation formula:

$$BW = \frac{f_{eff} \times 8 \times Channels}{1000}$$

Where $BW$ — throughput in GB/s. Dual-channel operation ( $Dual Channel$ ) doubles this figure, which is extremely important for modern multi-core processors.

Why is it important to know the real delay in nanoseconds?

  1. Generation comparison: DDR5 modules have much higher CL values ​​(eg CL40) than DDR4 (CL16). However, due to the huge frequency (6400+ MT/s), their real latency in nanoseconds is often lower than that of their predecessors.
  2. Overclocking optimization: When overclocking, increasing the frequency often requires weakening (increasing) the timings. The calculator will help you understand if you are receiving data slower after a “successful” frequency overclock.
  3. Stutter diagnostics: If games experience microfreezes at high average FPS, the problem often lies in RAM latency above 12-14 ns.
