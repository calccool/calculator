Operating principle and formulas

The acetate buffer consists of a weak acid (acetic, $CH_3COOH$ ) and its conjugate base (sodium acetate, $CH_3COONa$ ). The calculation of the ratio of components is based on the classical Henderson-Hasselbach equation:

$$pH = pK_a + \log \left( \frac{[A^-]}{[HA]} \right)$$

Where:

  • pH - required acidity of the solution.
  • pKₐ — dissociation constant of acetic acid (at 25°C is 4.76 ).
  • [A⁻] — molar concentration of proton acceptor (sodium acetate).
  • [HA] — molar concentration of proton donor (acetic acid).

Buffer operating range

Acetate buffer is most effective in a pH range close to its $pK_a$ .

  • Optimal zone: pH 4.76
  • Working range: pH 3.7 – 5.8

If you try to prepare a solution with a pH outside this range, the buffer capacity will not be sufficient to maintain a stable environment.

Cooking instructions

Based on the calculator's calculations, follow this algorithm to obtain a solution (for example, 1 liter):

  1. Water preparation: Pour approximately 80% of the final volume of distilled water into a volumetric flask or beaker (for example, 800 ml for 1 liter of solution).
  2. Dissolving salt: Weigh the calculated amount of sodium acetate and add it to the water. Stir with a magnetic stirrer until the crystals are completely dissolved.
  3. Adding acid: Using a pipette, add the calculated volume of concentrated acetic acid.
  4. pH adjustment (optional): Immerse the pH meter electrode in the solution. If necessary, adjust the pH to the ideal value by adding drops of $HCl$ (to lower) or $NaOH$ (to increase).
  5. Finalization: Pour the solution into a volumetric flask and make up to the mark (final volume) with distillate.

Why is ionic strength important?

In our calculator, you enter the total buffer concentration (for example, 100 mM). This is the sum of the acid and salt concentrations:

$$C_{total} = [CH_3COOH] + [CH_3COONa]$$

Controlling ionic strength is important for biochemical applications as it influences the activity of proteins and the solubility of substances.
