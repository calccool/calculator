Water calculator: how much to drink per day by weight, height, age and activity

Water is the basis of metabolism. Most standard recommendations (like “8 glasses a day” or “30 ml per kilogram of weight”) are too general and do not take into account the physiological characteristics of a particular person. The hydration needs of a 20-year-old athlete and a 50-year-old office worker will be dramatically different, even if they are the same weight.

Our smart water balance calculator uses a medical approach. Instead of a linear relationship with weight, we calculate your basal metabolic rate (BMR) and adjust it for physical activity and climate factors.

Scientific calculation formula (Energy consumption method)

The gold standard in dietetics is to link fluid intake to calorie expenditure. According to the recommendations of the WHO and the US Institute of Medicine, for every kilocalorie of energy expended, the body requires approximately 1 ml of water.

Our algorithm works in three stages:

  1. Calculation of BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate): We use the Mifflin-San Geor formula, which is recognized as the most accurate for modern people.
  2. Calculation of TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure): The basal metabolic rate is multiplied by the activity factor.
  3. Adjustment: Correction factors for ambient temperature (sweating) are added.

The mathematical model looks like this:

$$BMR_{муж} = (10 \times W) + (6.25 \times H) — (5 \times A) + 5$$

$$BMR_{жен} = (10 \times W) + (6.25 \times H) — (5 \times A) — 161$$

Where:

  • $$W$$ - Weight (kg)
  • $$H$$ - Height (cm)
  • $$A$$ — Age (years)

The final water norm (

$$V_{water}$$

) is calculated as:

$$V_{water} = (BMR \times K_{активности} \times K_{климата}) \text{ мл}$$

Factors affecting hydration

Why is it important to fill out all fields of the calculator?

  • Age: As people age, their metabolism slows and their basal water needs decrease, but older people's sense of thirst decreases, increasing the risk of dehydration.
  • Floor: In men, muscle mass is usually higher, which requires more fluid for metabolic processes.
  • Climate: In hot weather or high humidity, the body spends up to 500-1000 ml of water additionally just for thermoregulation (cooling the body).

5 signs you're not drinking enough water

  1. Urine color: Dark yellow or amber color is the main indicator of dehydration. Normally it should be light straw colored.
  2. Headache: Often migraines and decreased concentration at the end of the working day are caused precisely by a lack of fluid.
  3. Dry skin: Water is necessary for the elasticity of the epidermis.
  4. False hunger: The centers of thirst and hunger in the brain are nearby. We often eat when we are actually thirsty.
  5. Fatigue: Decreased blood volume causes the heart to work harder to deliver oxygen to the organs.

How to drink water correctly?

  • Morning: Drink 1-2 glasses of water immediately after waking up to jump-start your body.
  • Before eating: A glass of water 30 minutes before meals improves digestion and helps control appetite.
  • Uniformity: Don't try to drink the whole amount in the evening. Drink in small portions (100-150 ml) throughout the day.

Frequently asked questions:

How much water should you drink per 1 kg of weight?
Answer: from 30 to 35 ml. For example, with a weight of 70 kg - from 2100 to 2450 ml.

Is it possible to drink water “in reserve”?
No. Excess water causes dilution of sodium in the blood, swelling and stress on the kidneys.

How to adapt the norm to hot weather or illness?
Increase your consumption by 20–30%, especially at high temperature or active loss of fluid (for example, with ARVI).
