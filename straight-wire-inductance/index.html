Straight Wire Inductance Calculator + Reactance Calculator

Many radio amateurs are accustomed to thinking that only coils have inductance. However, any conductor through which current flows creates a magnetic field around itself, which means it has its own inductance.

At low frequencies (audio, household power supply 50 Hz), the inductance of a 10 cm long jumper is negligible and can be neglected. But in HF and microwave technology (FM band, Wi-Fi, digital electronics with steep signal edges) even an extra millimeter of a capacitor leg or track on a printed circuit board can turn into significant reactance, which will disrupt the operation of the circuit.

Our calculator allows you to instantly calculate the parasitic inductance of a circular conductor, as well as find out its reactance ( $X_L$ ) at a given frequency.

Calculation formula

To calculate the inductance of a straight round wire, an approximate formula is used (for the case when the length of the wire is significantly greater than its diameter $l \gg d$ ):

$$L = 2 \cdot l \cdot \left( \ln \left( \frac{4l}{d} \right) — 0.75 \right) \cdot 10^{-3}$$

Where:

  • L — inductance (μH);
  • l — wire length (cm);
  • d — wire diameter (cm);
  • ln — natural logarithm;
  • 0.75 — coefficient that takes into account the internal inductance of the conductor at low and medium frequencies.

Important information about the skin effect: At very high frequencies, current is forced toward the surface of the conductor (skin effect), and the internal inductance tends to zero. In this case, the coefficient $0.75$ in the formula is replaced by $1$ . Our algorithm uses a universal coefficient suitable for most amateur radio applications.

Material influence: why is magnetic permeability important?

The ability to select materials has been added to the calculator. This is critical if you are using steel leads (like those found on cheap resistors) instead of copper.

  • Copper, Aluminum, Silver ( $\mu \approx 1$ ): Non-magnetic materials. Inductance depends only on geometry.
  • Steel, Iron ( $\mu \gg 1$ ): Ferromagnets enhance the magnetic field inside a conductor. This leads to a sharp increase in internal inductance and losses at high frequencies. Steel wire is strictly not recommended for use in HF circuits.

Reactance ( $X_L$ )

Inductance itself is simply a proportionality factor. To understand how a wire will affect a signal, you need to know its inductive (reactive) reactance at the operating frequency. It is calculated using Ohm's law for alternating current:

$$X_L = 2 \pi \cdot f \cdot L$$

Where:

  • $X_L$ — resistance (Ohm);
  • $f$ — frequency (Hz);
  • $L$ — inductance (H).

Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

Does wire insulation affect inductance?

No, for a single straight wire the insulation material (PVC, enamel, air) does not affect the magnetic field, and therefore the inductance. Insulation is only important when calculating stray capacitance between adjacent wires.

Is it possible to reduce the inductance of a wire?

Yes, there are two ways:

  1. Shorten the conductor (linear relationship: 2 times shorter = 2 times less inductance).
  2. Use a flat busbar or several parallel wires instead of one round one.

In what units is the result?

The calculator displays the value in nanohenry (nH) and duplicates in microhenry (µH), since at short lengths the values ​​are very small (1 µH = 1000 nH).
