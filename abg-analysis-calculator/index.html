How does the diagnostic algorithm work?

The calculator determines the primary disorder by assessing the pH deviation and then looks for the cause of this deviation (respiratory or metabolic).

1. Determination of the type of violation

The main types of imbalance are determined by the direction of the shifts:

  • Respiratory Acidosis: pH ↓, PaCO2​ ↑ (Lungs retain CO₂).
  • Respiratory Alkalosis: pH ↑, PaCO2​ ↓ (Hyperventilation, CO₂ leaching).
  • Metabolic Acidosis: pH ↓, HCO3−​ ↓ (accumulation of acids or loss of bases).
  • Metabolic Alkalosis: pH ↑, HCO3−​ ↑ (Accumulation of bases or loss of acids).

2. Compensation Assessment

The body is always trying to return the pH to normal.

  • No compensation: Only one parameter has changed (PaCO2​ or HCO3−​), the second is normal.
  • Partial compensation: Both parameters have been changed, but the pH is still not back in the 7.35–7.45 range.
  • Full compensation: Both parameters are changed, but the pH is within normal limits.

Additional Features: Anion Gap

When identifying metabolic acidosis, it is critical to calculate Anion Gap. This helps differentiate causes (eg, diabetic ketoacidosis, lactic acidosis, or toxin toxicity).

The calculation requires data on electrolytes (Na+ and Cl−).

Anion Gap Formula:

AG=[Na+]−([Cl−]+[HCO3−​])

  • Standard AG: 8 – 16 mmol/l.
  • High AG: Indicates the presence of non-measured anions (ketones, lactate, methanol, ethylene glycol).
  • Normal AG: Characteristic of bicarbonate loss (diarrhea, renal tubular acidosis).

Oxygenation Assessment

In addition to the acid-base balance, the tool evaluates the efficiency of gas exchange by PaO2 level:

  • > 80 mmHg: Norm.
  • 60 – 80 mmHg: Mild hypoxemia.
  • 40 – 60 mmHg: Moderate hypoxemia.
  • < 40 mmHg: Severe hypoxemia (life threatening).
